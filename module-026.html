Process Safety Management Program

01 What Is PSM & When Does It Apply?

▼

OSHA’s Process Safety Management (PSM) standard, found at 29 CFR 1910.119, is one of the most comprehensive and consequential safety regulations in the United States. It was enacted in response to catastrophic industrial accidents — including Bhopal (1984, over 3,000 deaths), Texas City (2005, 15 deaths), and West, Texas (2013, 15 deaths) — where the unexpected release of highly hazardous chemicals caused mass casualties.

PSM applies to facilities that use, store, manufacture, handle, or move certain Highly Hazardous Chemicals (HHCs) in quantities at or above threshold quantities (TQs) established by OSHA. BBB Tank Services frequently works at refineries, petrochemical plants, chemical facilities, and terminals that are PSM-covered. Understanding PSM is not optional for a contractor working at these facilities.

What Makes a Facility PSM-Covered?

Covered Chemicals Include

  • Flammable liquids or gases ≥10,000 lbs (crude oil, gasoline, LPG, etc.)
  • Listed toxic chemicals above threshold (chlorine, ammonia, hydrogen sulfide)
  • Reactive chemicals above threshold
  • Explosive chemicals above threshold
  • Over 130 specifically listed chemicals with individual TQs

Common BBB Tank Services Customer Types

  • Oil refineries
  • Petrochemical plants
  • Chemical manufacturing facilities
  • Terminals handling flammable or toxic products
  • LPG/NGL storage and fractionation facilities
  • Wastewater treatment plants with chlorine > 1,500 lbs
How to Know You’re at a PSM Facility: The host employer (customer) will tell you during site orientation. PSM facilities must provide contractors with specific information before work begins. If you are unsure whether a facility is PSM-covered, ask your BBB Tank Services supervisor before starting work. Operating at a PSM facility without understanding its requirements puts you and others at risk.

PSM vs. EPA’s Risk Management Program (RMP)

PSM (OSHA) and RMP (EPA, 40 CFR Part 68) are parallel programs designed to prevent catastrophic chemical releases. PSM focuses on worker safety; RMP focuses on protecting the surrounding community and environment. Many facilities are covered by both. As a contractor, PSM is the primary standard that governs your work obligations — but awareness of RMP is valuable context.

⚠ Why PSM Matters for Contractors. OSHA data shows that contractors account for a disproportionate share of injuries and fatalities at PSM-covered facilities. This occurs because contractors are often less familiar with the specific processes, hazards, and procedures at the host facility than permanent employees are. PSM’s contractor element exists specifically to close this knowledge gap.

02 The 14 Elements of PSM

▼

OSHA’s PSM standard consists of 14 elements that together create a comprehensive safety management system for covered processes. Every element has specific requirements for both host employers and contractors. The elements highlighted in orange are the ones most directly relevant to BBB Tank Services workers at covered facilities.

1

Process Safety Information (PSI)

Technical data on hazardous chemicals, technology, and equipment used in the process

2

Process Hazard Analysis (PHA)

Structured study identifying hazards, causes, and consequences of process failures

3

Operating Procedures

Written procedures for all phases of operation including startup, shutdown, and emergencies

4

Training

Initial and refresher training for all employees involved in covered processes

5

Contractors

Requirements for selecting, training, and overseeing contractors at PSM facilities

6

Pre-Startup Safety Review (PSSR)

Formal review before startup of new or modified processes to confirm all safeguards are in place

7

Mechanical Integrity

Inspection, testing, and maintenance of critical equipment (pressure vessels, piping, relief valves)

8

Hot Work Permit

Formal permit required for ignition-source work in or near a covered process

9

Management of Change (MOC)

Formal review and authorization required before making any change to process, equipment, or procedures

10

Incident Investigation

Thorough investigation of incidents and near-misses to identify root causes and prevent recurrence

11

Emergency Planning & Response

Plans for responding to accidental releases, including evacuation, emergency contacts, and coordination with local responders

12

Compliance Audits

Audits every 3 years to verify the management system is functioning effectively

13

Trade Secrets

Confidentiality requirements for proprietary process information shared with workers

14

Employee Participation

Workers must be involved in PHA development and other PSM elements; access to PSM documents guaranteed

✓ Orange-highlighted elements are the ones you will most directly encounter and be responsible for as a BBB Tank Services contractor. Sections 3–6 of this module cover each in detail.

03 Contractor Requirements & Process Safety Information

▼

PSM Element 5 — Contractors

The Contractors element of PSM creates a shared responsibility between the host employer (the facility/customer) and the contract employer (BBB Tank Services). Both have specific, non-delegable obligations.

Host Employer (Customer) Must:

  • Inform BBB Tank Services of known process hazards that may affect contractor personnel
  • Explain applicable parts of the facility’s Emergency Action Plan
  • Develop and implement procedures to control contractor entry, exit, and presence near covered processes
  • Periodically evaluate contractor safety performance
  • Maintain a log of contract employer injuries and illnesses at the covered process

BBB Tank Services (Contract Employer) Must:

  • Ensure each contractor employee is trained in the work practices necessary to safely perform their job
  • Ensure each contractor employee is informed of potential fire, explosion, or toxic release hazards at the facility
  • Ensure each contractor employee knows the applicable provisions of the facility Emergency Action Plan
  • Document that each contractor employee has received and understood the required training
  • Ensure contractor employees follow safety rules of the facility
  • Advise the host employer of any hazards created by BBB Tank Services’ work

PSM Element 1 — Process Safety Information (PSI)

Before any work begins at a PSM-covered facility, the host employer must make Process Safety Information available to workers. PSI is the technical foundation of the entire PSM system — it documents the properties and hazards of the chemicals, the process technology, and the equipment used.

PSI CategoryWhat It ContainsWhy Contractors Need It
Chemical Hazard Information Toxicity, permissible exposure limits, physical data, reactivity data, thermal and chemical stability, corrosivity, SDS Tells you what chemicals you may be exposed to and their specific hazards — inputs to your PPE selection and emergency response planning
Process Technology Information Block flow diagrams, process description, maximum intended inventory, safe operating limits (temperature, pressure, flow, composition) Tells you what the process does and what the consequences of deviation are — helps you understand why procedures are the way they are
Process Equipment Information Materials of construction, P&IDs (Piping and Instrumentation Diagrams), electrical classification, relief valve design, safety system design Essential for work on or near process equipment — P&IDs tell you what is connected to what and where isolation points are located
P&IDs Are Not Optional. For any work involving breaking into process piping, isolating equipment, or modifying instrumentation, you must have access to the current P&ID for that section of the process. Outdated or incorrect P&IDs have contributed to multiple fatalities when workers opened connections to lines that turned out to contain hazardous materials.

04 Process Hazard Analysis & Safe Work Permits

▼

PSM Element 2 — Process Hazard Analysis (PHA)

A Process Hazard Analysis (PHA) is a formal, structured study of a covered process that systematically identifies potential hazards, determines their causes and consequences, and recommends safeguards. PHAs are conducted by multidisciplinary teams using recognized methodologies. For contractors, the PHA produces the risk picture that informs all safe work practices at that facility.

Common PHA Methodologies

HAZOP (Hazard & Operability Study)

  • Most rigorous PHA method
  • Uses guide words (No, More, Less, Reverse, Other Than) applied to process parameters
  • Identifies deviations and their causes, consequences, and safeguards
  • Required for complex processes

What-If Analysis

  • Team brainstorms “What if X happens?” scenarios
  • Less structured than HAZOP; good for simpler processes
  • Produces a list of scenarios with consequences and recommended actions

Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) / FMEA

  • Top-down (FTA) or bottom-up (FMEA) analysis of failure modes
  • Quantitative or qualitative
  • Used to analyze specific high-risk events in detail

What PHA Results Mean for Contractors

The PHA produces a set of findings and recommendations. These may include safeguards, procedures, PPE requirements, and restricted-access zones specifically because of risks identified in the PHA. When you are told at a PSM facility that:

  • “You must wear a supplied air respirator in that area” — a PHA finding likely identified a toxic release scenario there
  • “Two-man rule applies throughout this unit” — a PHA safeguard requiring continuous mutual observation
  • “That valve cannot be operated while the unit is in service” — a PHA finding identified a hazardous consequence if it is operated incorrectly
  • “You need a hot work permit anywhere in this dike” — a PHA found the entire dike area to be a classified flammable zone

These are not arbitrary rules — they are the outputs of systematic hazard studies. Following them is not optional.

PSM Element 8 — Hot Work Permits

At PSM-covered facilities, hot work permits are a formal, documented authorization specifically required by the standard — not just a company preference. Hot work permits must be issued for any work that could be an ignition source in or near a covered process, including:

  • Welding, cutting, or brazing
  • Grinding (spark-generating)
  • Use of non-explosion-proof electrical equipment in classified areas
  • Internal combustion engines operated within the process unit boundary

The hot work permit must specify the location, work to be done, fire protection measures, atmospheric monitoring requirements, and the name of the person issuing authorization. See Module 027 (Hot Work Program) for the full BBB Tank Services hot work procedure.

Scenario — PHA in Practice

The “Simple” Line Break

A BBB Tank Services crew is asked to replace a 2-inch isolation valve on a product line at a refinery. The crew leader looks at the line, sees it is tagged “drained and isolated,” and begins loosening the flange bolts to remove the valve. As the final bolt is removed, residual hydrocarbon under pressure sprays from the flange. Two workers are doused with product. The line was marked as drained but a check valve upstream had trapped significant pressure that the drain procedure did not relieve.

What should have happened: The facility’s HAZOP for this line had identified exactly this scenario — trapped pressure upstream of a check valve — as a hazard, with the safeguard being a specific double-block-and-bleed procedure plus pressure gauge verification before breaking the flange. The crew should have reviewed the facility’s safe work permit and LOTO procedure for this specific task, which would have specified the required verification steps. “It looks drained” is never adequate on a PSM-covered process line.

05 Management of Change & Pre-Startup Safety Review

▼

PSM Element 9 — Management of Change (MOC)

The Management of Change (MOC) process is one of the most important PSM elements for contractors to understand. MOC requires that any change to process chemicals, technology, equipment, procedures, or facilities at a covered facility must go through a formal review and authorization process before the change is made. “Change” is interpreted broadly — it includes everything from changing a gasket material to rerouting a process line to modifying a control setpoint.

⚠ The Most Common Contractor MOC Violation: A BBB Tank Services crew completes a repair and realizes that to reconnect a line, it would be easier (or necessary) to route it slightly differently than the original configuration. The crew makes the change without informing the facility. This is an unauthorized MOC — potentially a serious OSHA violation and, more importantly, a hazard that has not been evaluated. The change may affect pressure ratings, thermal expansion, support requirements, instrumentation, or process flow in ways the contractor cannot predict without the process knowledge the host facility has.

What Must Go Through MOC

Always Requires MOC

  • Any change to a process chemical (including concentration, grade, or alternative supplier)
  • Changes to process equipment (different pipe size, different valve type, different material)
  • Changes to control setpoints or alarm limits
  • Changes to procedures (even “temporary” ones)
  • Changes to the physical layout of process piping or equipment
  • Software or programming changes to process control systems

What Does NOT Require MOC

  • “Replacement in kind” — replacing a failed component with an identical one (same material, size, rating, and configuration)
  • Routine maintenance that restores equipment to its original design specification
  • Note: “Better than original” or “equivalent but different” is still a change and still requires MOC

The MOC Process

1
Change Request InitiatedWorker or supervisor identifies a needed change and submits a formal MOC request describing the change, the reason, and the scope.
2
Technical ReviewEngineering, process safety, and operations review the change for safety implications, code compliance, and process impact. PHA may be updated.
3
AuthorizationAppropriate level of management approves the change based on risk level. Higher-risk changes require higher levels of authorization.
4
Training & Documentation UpdateAffected workers are trained on the change. P&IDs, procedures, and other documents are updated before or immediately after implementation.
5
Implementation & PSSR (if required)The change is implemented. If the change affects process safety, a Pre-Startup Safety Review is conducted before the modified process is returned to service.
6
Close OutMOC is documented and closed. Temporary changes have defined expiration dates and revert unless extended through a new MOC.

PSM Element 6 — Pre-Startup Safety Review (PSSR)

A PSSR is a formal safety check conducted before a new or modified process is started up. It confirms that all safety-related requirements have been met before the process is exposed to hazardous chemicals. PSSRs are triggered by:

  • New covered processes being placed into operation for the first time
  • Major modifications to a covered process that required a MOC
  • Any change that would require an update to the process safety information

For BBB Tank Services crews completing maintenance at a covered facility, you may be asked to participate in or support a PSSR by:

  • Confirming that all work has been completed per the scope and that no unauthorized changes were made
  • Confirming that all materials, tools, and scaffolding have been removed from the equipment
  • Signing off on the physical inspection component of the PSSR checklist
  • Being available to answer questions about the work scope during the PSSR walkdown

06 Incident Investigation & Emergency Response at PSM Facilities

▼

PSM Element 10 — Incident Investigation

OSHA’s PSM standard requires that every incident that resulted in, or could have resulted in, a catastrophic release of a covered chemical be investigated. This includes near-misses. Investigations must:

  • Begin as promptly as possible, no later than 48 hours after the incident
  • Be conducted by a team including at least one person knowledgeable in the process involved and a contract employee if the incident involved contract work
  • Identify the nature of the incident, its factors, and root causes
  • Produce a written report with recommendations
  • Have their recommendations resolved and documented within a reasonable timeframe

BBB Tank Services Contractor Reporting Obligations

As a contractor at a PSM facility, you have reporting obligations that go beyond normal workplace incident reporting:

You Must Report Immediately:

  • Any near-miss involving process equipment, chemicals, or energy sources — even if no injury occurred
  • Any unplanned release of a process chemical, however small
  • Any equipment failure, unexpected condition, or deviation from the approved work scope
  • Any situation where you believe a procedure was inadequate for the actual conditions encountered
  • Any injury or illness related to process chemical exposure

Why Reporting Everything Matters:

  • Near-misses at PSM facilities are often leading indicators of future catastrophic events
  • PSM investigations require the earliest possible notification to preserve evidence and witness accounts
  • OSHA requires host employers to maintain a log of contractor incidents at covered processes
  • Unreported near-misses allow the same conditions to recur with potentially worse consequences

PSM Element 11 — Emergency Planning & Response

PSM-covered facilities are required to establish and implement emergency action plans covering catastrophic chemical releases. As a contractor, you will receive this information during site orientation. Your responsibilities during a facility emergency are:

  1. 1
    Know the emergency signal before work begins. PSM facilities use sirens, PA announcements, or radio calls to signal emergencies. Know what the signal sounds like and what it means before you start work.
  2. 2
    Know the designated shelter-in-place and evacuation assembly points. Different emergency scenarios may require different responses — some require evacuation upwind; others (toxic gas releases) may require shelter-in-place indoors. Know which applies when.
  3. 3
    Stop work and secure the work area immediately when an emergency signal sounds. Leave tools and equipment. Ensure the work area is safe to leave (no open flanges, open hatches, or uncontrolled energy).
  4. 4
    Proceed to your designated assembly point and report to your crew supervisor. Your supervisor will account for all BBB Tank Services personnel and report the count to the facility.
  5. 5
    Do not re-enter the facility until cleared by the facility emergency coordinator. A BBB Tank Services supervisor alone does not have the authority to authorize re-entry during a facility emergency.

PSM Facility Site Orientation Checklist

Before beginning work at any PSM-covered facility, confirm you have received and understand all of the following from the host employer:

Process Hazard Information

  • Chemicals present and their hazards (toxicity, flammability, reactivity)
  • Maximum intended inventory in the area where you will work
  • Safe operating limits you need to be aware of
  • Specific hazards identified in the PHA for your work area

Emergency & Safety Information

  • Emergency signal types and what each means
  • Your assembly point location and headcount procedure
  • Shelter-in-place location if applicable
  • Facility emergency contact numbers
  • Hot work permit and LOTO authority contacts
  • Restricted areas and no-go zones
✓ Your Right to Stop Work at a PSM Facility. Workers at PSM-covered facilities have the right to stop work when they believe an imminent hazard exists — even if this means stopping a process. This is not insubordination; it is a recognized safety right. If you encounter conditions at a PSM facility that appear unsafe and your supervisor is unavailable, you may and should stop work and request facility safety personnel review the situation before continuing.
