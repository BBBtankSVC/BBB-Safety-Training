Welding Safety & Hot Work Program

Controlling ignition sources, fume hazards, and fire risk during welding, cutting, grinding, and all hot work operations at BBB Tank Services petroleum storage tank sites.

01 Hot Work Defined & Why Tank Sites Are Different

▼

What Is Hot Work?

Hot work is any operation that produces sparks, open flames, heat, or electrical arcs that could serve as an ignition source. At BBB Tank Services this includes:

Welding & Cutting

  • Arc welding (SMAW, GMAW, FCAW, GTAW)
  • Oxy-fuel gas welding and cutting
  • Plasma arc cutting
  • Brazing and soldering
  • Torch heating and preheating

Spark-Generating Work

  • Angle grinding and disc cutting
  • Needle scaling & wire brushing on steel
  • Abrasive blasting with steel shot
  • Chipping and chiseling on metal
  • Powder-actuated fastener tools

Other Ignition Sources

  • Non-explosion-proof electrical equipment in classified areas
  • Gasoline-powered equipment within process unit boundaries
  • Open-flame heaters and heat guns
  • Pipe thawing equipment

Why Petroleum Tank Sites Carry Uniquely Elevated Risk

Low Ignition Energy

  • Petroleum vapors ignite at energies as low as 0.2 millijoules — far below a spark or welding arc
  • Flammable vapor can exist at low concentrations invisible to the eye
  • LEL for gasoline vapor is approximately 1.4% by volume

Catastrophic Consequences

  • A single tank fire can rapidly escalate to neighboring tanks (boilover, BLEVE, UVCE)
  • Tank explosions have killed welders and bystanders hundreds of feet away
  • Criminal liability has followed unauthorized hot work tank incidents

⚠ Gas-Free Certification Required. No hot work may be performed on or immediately adjacent to any petroleum storage tank without a gas-free certificate issued following atmospheric testing that confirms flammable vapors, O&sub2;, and toxic gases are within acceptable limits. This is an absolute requirement with no exceptions.

02 Hot Work Permits & Atmospheric Requirements

▼

When a Hot Work Permit Is Required

BBB Tank Services requires a hot work permit for all spark- or flame-producing work within 35 feet of any petroleum storage tank, and for all hot work on or inside a tank structure. Permits are valid for one shift only (max 12 hours) and must be reissued each day work continues.

⚠ Continuous Monitoring Required During Hot Work. A single test before work is not sufficient. Petroleum vapors can migrate, wind can shift, adjacent tanks can begin transfer operations. Workers performing hot work on or near tanks must wear a calibrated multi-gas personal monitor during the entire operation.

Fire Watch Requirements

A designated fire watch must be posted during all hot work near petroleum tanks and must remain for a minimum of 30 minutes after all hot work ceases. The fire watch must:

  • Have no other assigned task during the watch period
  • Have a charged fire extinguisher within arm's reach
  • Maintain clear line of sight to all areas where sparks or slag could land
  • Have communication means to alert the site of fire
  • Know the facility emergency response procedure
  • Immediately stop work if atmospheric conditions change or fire is observed

03 Pre-Work Fire Prevention & The Pyrophoric Hazard

▼

Pre-Work Fire Prevention Checklist

  1. 1
    35-foot clearance zone established. Remove all combustibles (rags, paper, wood, hoses, flammable liquids) from within 35 feet. Shield combustibles that cannot be removed with welding blankets.
  2. 2
    Floor drains and openings covered. Sparks can travel into drains and ignite vapors in drainage systems. Cover all drains, sumps, and openings within the spark zone with non-combustible covers.
  3. 3
    Surfaces wet down where applicable. Dampen combustible surfaces (wooden platforms, dried vegetation, petroleum-contaminated soil) in surrounding areas where sparks may land.
  4. 4
    Connections to tanks blanked or closed. All piping connections between the hot work location and adjacent tanks must be confirmed closed, blanked, or physically isolated to prevent vapor migration.
  5. 5
    Fire protection staged and confirmed ready. Minimum two fire extinguishers at the work location. Nearest hose station charged and accessible. Fire watch briefed and positioned.

Pyrophoric Iron Sulfide — The Hidden Tank Hazard

⚠ Pyrophoric Iron Sulfide (FeS) Can Spontaneously Ignite Without Any External Spark. Pyrophoric iron sulfide forms on the interior surfaces of tanks that held sour crude oil or sulfur-containing products through the reaction of H&sub2;S with iron in the tank shell. When exposed to air during tank entry, cleaning, or inspection plate opening, it can spontaneously combust — causing fires or explosions in a tank that tests gas-free. This has killed workers during fully permitted, gas-free tank cleaning operations.

Identifying Pyrophoric Risk

  • Black, brown, or dark-colored sludge or scale on tank interior surfaces
  • Tanks known to have contained sour crude or product with >0.5% sulfur content
  • Any tank with a history of H&sub2;S readings during cleaning
  • Visible glowing or smoking from scale when tank is opened

Minimum Controls

  • Tank must be emptied, gas-freed, and isolated per API 2015 before any work
  • Interior surfaces must be kept wet throughout the entire cleaning process
  • Pyrophoric scale must be wetted before disturbing — never allow it to dry
  • Removed scale kept wet and disposed in sealed, water-filled containers
  • No hot work until all surfaces cleaned, wetted, and tested

Minimum Conditions for Hot Work on Petroleum Tanks

Per API RP 2009, all of the following must be confirmed before any hot work on or inside a petroleum storage tank:

  • Tank completely emptied of all liquid product
  • Tank gas-freed — atmosphere confirmed <1% LEL
  • All connections to the tank isolated (blanked, double-block-and-bleed, or spectacle blind)
  • Tank ventilated and maintained gas-free with continuous monitoring throughout the operation
  • All pyrophoric deposits identified, wetted, and controlled
  • Hot work permit issued and fire watch positioned

Ventilation During Welding

  • Open outdoor welding: adequate natural ventilation usually sufficient for mild steel if wind keeps fume plume from the welder's breathing zone
  • Confined or semi-confined areas: LEV must capture fumes at the source; position capture hood 6–12 inches from the arc
  • Tank interiors: mechanical ventilation must maintain a minimum 10 air changes per hour; LEV at the arc required for stainless, galvanized, or coated material
  • Never weld downwind of yourself: keep your face out of the fume plume at all times

⚠ Arc Eye (Photokeratitis). UV radiation from the welding arc can cause arc eye — a painful corneal burn — from even brief unprotected exposure at distances up to 50 feet. Never look at a welding arc without a proper filter lens. When another welder is working nearby, position yourself so you cannot directly see their arc.

05 Gas Cylinder Safety

▼

Compressed gas cylinders store enormous energy. An oxygen cylinder at full pressure contains gas at over 2,000 psi. Mishandled cylinders kill workers every year.

💨 Oxygen (O&sub2;) Cylinders

  • NEVER contact oil or grease — oxygen under pressure reacts explosively with hydrocarbons. Use only oxygen-rated fittings and regulators. Never use oil-based thread sealants.
  • Store and use in upright, secured position
  • Hose color: green (NFPA standard)
  • Store at least 20 ft from fuel gas cylinders or behind a 5-ft fire-resistant barrier (30-min rating)
  • Never use oxygen to blow off work or clothing
  • Cap all cylinders when not in use or being transported

🔥 Acetylene (C&sub2;H&sub2;) Cylinders

  • Maximum use pressure: 15 PSI. Acetylene becomes unstable and can decompose explosively above this pressure. Never exceed 15 PSI at the regulator.
  • Store and use upright at all times. If on its side, stand upright for at least 1 hour before using.
  • Hose color: red (NFPA standard)
  • Store at least 20 ft from oxygen cylinders
  • Flashback arrestors required at both regulator and torch body
  • Never allow acetylene to contact silver, copper, or copper alloys (forms explosive acetylides)

General Cylinder Handling Rules

  • Secure all cylinders at all times — chained or strapped to a cart, wall, or structure. A falling full cylinder can shear the valve and become a lethal projectile.
  • Cap all cylinders when the regulator is not attached
  • Never roll cylinders on their side — use a hand truck designed for gas cylinders
  • Inspect regulators before connecting: check for cracked gauges, damaged diaphragm, or oil contamination
  • Open valves slowly to prevent pressure surge
  • Check hose connections for leaks with soapy water before lighting the torch; never use an open flame to check for gas leaks
  • Hoses must have flashback arrestors at both ends (regulator connection and torch connection)
  • Store cylinders away from heat sources; never expose to temperatures above 125°F (52°C)
Scenario — Cylinder Incident

The Tipped Cylinder

A BBB crew finishes a torch-cutting job and sets the unsecured acetylene cylinder against the tank shell to move to the next location. The cylinder tips over, shearing the valve at the base. Acetylene discharges rapidly near hot metal and welding equipment.

What should have happened: Cap all cylinders immediately when the regulator is removed. Transport only on proper hand carts, never leaning unsecured against surfaces. A cylinder with a sheared valve becomes a rocket — the force of gas discharge has driven cylinders through concrete block walls. On a petroleum tank site this scenario is potentially catastrophic.

06 Electrical Safety for Arc Welding & Final Checklist

▼

Arc Welding Electrical Hazards

Primary Voltage Shock

  • Line voltage on the primary side of the welder (220–480V)
  • Contact with primary voltage is almost always fatal
  • Never open the welder cabinet without trained maintenance personnel
  • Inspect power supply cord and plug before each use

Secondary Voltage Shock

  • Output voltage from the welder (20–80V DC/AC)
  • Fatal under wet conditions or if current crosses the chest
  • Never touch the electrode or electrode holder with bare or wet hands
  • Keep body insulated from the workpiece and electrode simultaneously

Electrical Safety Rules for Welding

  • Keep dry. Never weld in wet conditions or while standing in water. Perspiration-soaked clothing significantly reduces body resistance. Replace wet gloves before continuing.
  • Insulate yourself from the workpiece. In confined spaces, use dry insulating pads under your knees and body. Do not touch the workpiece with bare skin simultaneously.
  • Connect the work lead close to the weld point. Keeps welding current from flowing through adjacent equipment, piping, or instrumentation, which can cause arc burns at unexpected locations and damage sensitive electronics.
  • Inspect electrode holders and cables before each use. Frayed insulation or loose connections must be repaired before welding. Never tape over damaged insulation on welding leads.
  • Turn off the welder when leaving the work or changing electrodes in damp or confined conditions.
  • Dry gloves mandatory in confined spaces. Perspiration, metal contact, and limited movement make electrical shock risk significantly higher than open-air welding.
