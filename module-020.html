Control of Hazardous Energy

Lockout & Tagout Program

Protecting BBB Tank Services workers from the unexpected release of hazardous energy during maintenance, repair, and servicing of equipment and tank systems.

01 Why Lockout / Tagout Exists

▼

Every year, the unexpected release of hazardous energy kills and injures thousands of workers across the United States. OSHA estimates that proper LOTO procedures prevent approximately 120 fatalities and 50,000 injuries annually. Workers are crushed by machinery that unexpectedly starts, electrocuted by equipment that was thought to be de-energized, or caught in pressurized systems that release without warning.

⚠ The Defining Risk: Hazardous energy incidents occur because someone assumed a machine was safe — and it was not. A pump that was shut off can restart if a control circuit is still energized. A valve that was closed can be opened by a coworker who doesn’t know anyone is working downstream. A compressed air line that was disconnected at the tool can still have pressure at the fitting. LOTO eliminates assumptions. It creates a physical guarantee that energy cannot be released.

OSHA Standard

The Control of Hazardous Energy standard is found at 29 CFR 1910.147 (General Industry) and referenced at 29 CFR 1926.417 (Construction). The standard applies whenever workers perform servicing or maintenance on machines or equipment where the unexpected energization, start-up, or release of stored energy could cause injury.

Scope — When Does LOTO Apply at BBB Tank Services?

LOTO applies any time a BBB Tank Services employee performs maintenance, repair, or service on equipment where unexpected energization or stored energy release is a hazard. This includes:

Pump & Motor Maintenance

  • Replacing pump seals or impellers
  • Motor replacement or inspection
  • Coupling alignment work
  • Clearing jammed pump suction

Valve & Piping Work

  • Replacing valves or valve packing
  • Breaking flange connections
  • Cleaning strainers under pressure
  • Installing blind flanges or blinds

Tank Equipment Service

  • Servicing roof drives or mixers
  • Level gauge and instrument maintenance
  • Vacuum truck and transfer equipment service
  • Any work inside tank manway openings tied to active systems

When LOTO Does NOT Apply

  • Normal production operations — LOTO is for servicing and maintenance, not for normal operating tasks
  • Cord-and-plug equipment — if a tool is powered solely by a cord and plug, unplugging it and keeping the plug under your exclusive control provides equivalent protection (no additional lockout required)
  • Minor tool changes with adequate protection — routine adjustments if guarding is maintained and the activity is part of normal production (rare exception, specific criteria apply)

02 Types of Hazardous Energy

▼

Hazardous energy is any energy that, if released unexpectedly, could cause injury. There are six types. At BBB Tank Services job sites, workers encounter all six. Identifying and controlling every applicable energy type for each piece of equipment is the foundation of an effective LOTO procedure.

⚡ Electrical

  • Motors and control circuits
  • Solenoid valves
  • Instrument panels and PLCs
  • Lighting circuits in confined spaces
  • Control: Disconnect switch, circuit breaker

💨 Pneumatic

  • Air-operated valves and actuators
  • Pneumatic tools and lines
  • Instrument air systems
  • Air-powered pumps
  • Control: Isolation valve + bleed residual pressure

💧 Hydraulic

  • Hydraulic actuators and cylinders
  • Hydraulic torque wrenches
  • Hydraulic-driven equipment
  • Control: Isolation valve + bleed & block system pressure

⚙ Mechanical

  • Springs under compression or tension
  • Counterweights
  • Gravity — unsupported elevated components
  • Rotating equipment with stored inertia
  • Control: Block, pin, or restrain the component

🔥 Thermal

  • High-temperature process fluid in lines and vessels
  • Steam systems
  • Heat tracing on piping
  • Hot tank surfaces
  • Control: Isolation + cool-down confirmation

⚗️ Chemical

  • Pressurized chemical lines
  • Stored chemical energy in process fluids
  • Reactive chemicals under pressure
  • Control: Double-block-and-bleed or blind/bleed

Stored vs. Active Energy

One of the most frequently overlooked aspects of LOTO is stored (residual) energy — energy that remains in the equipment even after the primary energy source has been isolated:

  • Capacitors in electrical systems retain charge after power is disconnected — must be discharged
  • Compressed air or hydraulic pressure trapped downstream of a closed valve — must be bled
  • Springs in valves, dampers, and actuators that are pre-loaded — must be released or blocked
  • Gravity — elevated components (tank lids, press rams, suspended loads) that could fall — must be blocked or lowered
  • Thermal energy in hot pipes and vessels that continue to be dangerous after isolation — must cool sufficiently
  • Process fluid trapped in pipe sections between two closed valves — must be drained and vented
⚠ Residual Energy Is the Hidden Killer. More LOTO-related injuries occur from stored or residual energy than from failure to isolate the primary energy source. Isolating the main electrical disconnect is not enough if a capacitor bank is still charged. Closing the main isolation valve is not enough if trapped pressure remains between the valve and the work point. Always identify and release all residual energy before starting work.

03 Key Roles & Lockout vs. Tagout

▼

Employee Roles Under OSHA 1910.147

RoleDefinitionResponsibilities
Authorized Employee The person who applies the lockout/tagout device(s) to control hazardous energy. This is the worker performing the maintenance or service. Performs LOTO procedure; applies their personal lock; verifies zero energy state; removes their lock when work is complete. Only this person may remove their own lock.
Affected Employee A worker who operates or uses the equipment being locked out, or who works in an area where LOTO is being performed. Must be notified before LOTO is applied and before it is removed. Must not attempt to operate equipment that is locked or tagged out. Must not remove any LOTO device.
Other Employee Any worker whose work operations are in an area where LOTO procedures are being performed. Must not attempt to restart or re-energize locked-out equipment. Must report any tampered or missing LOTO device to their supervisor immediately.

Lockout vs. Tagout — What’s the Difference?

🔒 Lockout (Preferred)

  • A physical padlock is applied to the energy isolation point, preventing it from being operated or moved
  • Only the worker who applied the lock can remove it (with their personal key)
  • Cannot be removed without cutting the lock or using a management override procedure
  • Provides the highest level of protection — a physical barrier that cannot be accidentally defeated
  • Use lockout whenever the isolation device can accept a lock.

🏷️ Tagout (Secondary)

  • A warning tag is attached to the energy isolation point when a lock cannot be applied (the device does not have a lock hasp)
  • A tagout device is a WARNING — it does not physically prevent operation of the device
  • Provides less protection than lockout and additional protective measures are required
  • Only acceptable when it can be demonstrated that tagout provides equivalent protection to lockout
  • Lockout is always preferred; tagout is a last resort when lockout is not possible.
BBB Tank Services Policy: Lockout is always the required method when the energy isolating device can accept a padlock. If a device cannot accept a lock, a hasp adapter must be installed before tagout is used. Tagout alone is never preferred and must be approved by a supervisor with documentation of why lockout was not feasible.

LOTO Hardware

Each authorized employee at BBB Tank Services is issued a personal LOTO kit containing:

  • Personal padlock — unique key; worker keeps the only copy; name/ID engraved or labeled on the lock body
  • Lockout hasp — multi-lock device that allows multiple workers to apply their own lock to a single isolation point for group lockout
  • Warning tags — bright red/orange “DANGER — Do Not Operate” tags with the worker’s name, date, and reason
  • Valve lockout devices — adjustable ball valve lockouts, gate valve lockouts, and plug lockouts
  • Electrical lockout devices — circuit breaker lockouts, disconnect lockouts, plug lockouts

04 The LOTO Procedure — Step by Step

▼

BBB Tank Services follows an 8-step LOTO application procedure based on OSHA 1910.147 requirements. Every step must be completed in sequence. Skipping steps — even one — eliminates the protection the procedure is designed to provide.

1
Prepare — Identify All Energy Sources

Review the equipment-specific LOTO procedure (written procedure required for complex equipment). Identify every energy source: electrical disconnects, pneumatic shutoffs, hydraulic isolation points, stored energy locations, and process fluid isolation valves. Know the equipment before touching it.

2
Notify Affected Employees

Inform all affected employees that the equipment is being locked out for maintenance. Tell them the reason and the estimated duration. They must not attempt to operate the equipment until they are notified that LOTO has been removed.

3
Shut Down the Equipment

Turn off, stop, or shut down the machine or equipment using the normal stopping procedure (control panel, stop button, etc.). Do not use the energy isolation device as the primary shutoff — shut down normally first, then isolate.

4
Isolate All Energy Sources

Operate every energy isolation device (disconnect switches, circuit breakers, valves) to the safe (de-energized, closed, or blocked) position. Isolate all energy sources — not just the obvious primary one. Complex equipment may have multiple electrical feeds, multiple valve isolation points, and stored energy sources that all require isolation.

5
Apply Lockout / Tagout Devices

Apply your personal padlock (or tag if lockout is not feasible) to each isolated energy point. If using a hasp for multiple locks, apply your lock to the hasp. Write your name, date, and reason on the tag attached to every device. The lock and tag remain until YOU personally remove them.

6
Release or Restrain All Stored Energy

Bleed residual air or hydraulic pressure from isolated sections. Discharge electrical capacitors. Release or block spring-loaded components. Lower suspended loads or place blocking devices to prevent movement under gravity. Allow hot systems to cool to a safe temperature. Do not proceed until all stored energy is eliminated or controlled.

7
Verify Zero Energy State — Test Before Touch

Before anyone puts their body in the danger zone, verify that the equipment is truly de-energized: attempt to start the machine using its normal operating controls (push the start button — it should not start). Use a meter to verify zero voltage on electrical equipment. Check a pressure gauge downstream of isolated valves to confirm zero pressure. This is the most critical step — do not skip it.

8
Begin Work

Only after all seven steps above are complete may work begin. The equipment is now under your personal control. As long as your lock is on the isolation point, the equipment cannot be energized without your knowledge.

⚠ Test Before You Touch. Step 7 is where LOTO failures are most often discovered — the machine starts when you push the button, revealing that an energy source was missed. This discovery must happen before any body part is in the danger zone, not after. Always press the start button to verify zero energy before reaching into equipment.

05 Restoring Energy, Group Lockout & Shift Changes

▼

Restoring Energy — Lockout Release Procedure

Restoring energy is as disciplined as applying lockout. Cutting corners during release is a major cause of injury to workers who are still in the equipment when it restarts.

  1. 1
    Verify work is complete. Inspect the equipment to confirm all work has been finished, all tools and materials have been removed from inside or around the equipment, and all guards and covers have been reinstalled.
  2. 2
    Verify all workers are clear. Physically confirm that all personnel are out of the danger zone and positioned a safe distance from the equipment. Do a headcount. Do not assume — verify.
  3. 3
    Remove all LOTO devices. Each authorized employee removes their own personal lock. The last lock removed must be removed by the authorized employee who applied it. LOTO devices are removed in reverse order of application.
  4. 4
    Notify affected employees. Inform all affected workers that LOTO has been removed and the equipment is being returned to service.
  5. 5
    Restore energy. Re-energize the equipment using the normal startup sequence.

Group Lockout

When multiple authorized employees work on the same equipment, each person applies their own personal lock to a group lockout hasp at the primary energy isolation point. This ensures that the equipment cannot be re-energized until every worker has removed their lock — confirming that every worker has cleared the equipment.

  • A group lockout hasp can accept multiple padlocks simultaneously — each worker puts on their own
  • One worker is typically designated as the Group Lockout Coordinator who maintains a log of who has locks applied and ensures accountability
  • Complex multi-energy systems may require a lockout box: all isolation point keys are placed in a lockable box, and each worker applies their personal lock to the box
  • No worker removes their lock until they have personally confirmed their work is complete and they are clear

Shift Changes

When a LOTO job spans a shift change, the lock transfer must be done in a continuous, overlap manner:

  1. The incoming worker applies their lock to the LOTO point before the outgoing worker removes theirs
  2. Both locks are on simultaneously during the transfer — there is never a moment without protection
  3. The outgoing worker briefs the incoming worker on the status of the job, all energy isolation points, and any stored energy controls in place
  4. Only then does the outgoing worker remove their lock
⚠ Never Remove Another Worker’s Lock. This is one of the most fundamental rules in LOTO. Only the authorized employee who applied a lock may remove it. If a worker leaves the job site, becomes ill, or is unavailable, removal of their lock requires a documented management override procedure: confirmation that the worker is not in the danger zone, supervisor authorization, and notification to the affected worker. This is a deliberate process — not something done casually.
Scenario — The Forgotten Lock

Shift Change Goes Wrong

A pump is locked out for seal replacement. The day shift worker gets called away at the end of the shift before the job is complete and leaves the lock on without briefing the night shift. The night shift supervisor assumes the job is done and asks a new operator to try to start the pump. The operator pushes the start button but it won’t run — the lock is still on. He reports this to the supervisor, who asks whether to just cut the lock since “the job must be done.”

Correct response: Never cut a lock without following the management override procedure. Call the original authorized employee. Confirm his location and that he is not inside the equipment. Get supervisor sign-off. Document the override. Only then, under supervision, remove the lock and verify the equipment is safe to restart. The few minutes this takes is nothing compared to the consequence of restarting a pump that still has someone working on it.

06 Written Procedures, Training & Auditing

▼

Written LOTO Procedures

OSHA requires a written energy control procedure for each type of equipment where employees perform service or maintenance and where there is unexpected energization or stored energy risk. BBB Tank Services maintains written LOTO procedures for all major equipment types. Each procedure must include:

  • Specific steps for shutting down the equipment
  • Location and type of every energy isolation device (with photos where helpful)
  • Type and magnitude of hazardous energy in the equipment
  • Method for verifying de-energization and zero stored energy
  • Special requirements (multiple workers, complex systems, permits required)
Where to Find Written LOTO Procedures at BBB Tank Services: Equipment-specific LOTO procedures are maintained in the job site safety binder and are accessible digitally on company tablets. Before performing LOTO on any unfamiliar equipment, request the written procedure from your supervisor. If a written procedure does not exist for the equipment you are working on, stop and contact your supervisor before beginning work.

Annual Program Audit

OSHA requires at least an annual inspection of each energy control procedure and an annual certification review confirming each authorized employee can apply the procedure correctly. BBB Tank Services conducts these audits by:

  • Reviewing each written LOTO procedure against current equipment configuration
  • Observing each authorized employee perform LOTO on the equipment they service
  • Documenting deficiencies and retraining any employee who cannot correctly execute the procedure
  • Certifying completion of the annual review with equipment identification, date, and employees covered

Immediate Reporting Requirements

Report immediately to your supervisor any of the following LOTO situations:

  • A lock or tag that has been removed without authorization
  • Equipment that restarted or energized while a lock was applied
  • A near-miss where unexpected energy release almost caused injury
  • Missing, damaged, or insufficient LOTO hardware (missing hasp, damaged locks)
  • Any situation where a written procedure was inadequate for the actual equipment encountered
