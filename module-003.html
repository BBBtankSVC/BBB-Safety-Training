Benzene Protection Program

📅 Rev. 10/31/2025 ⚖ OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1028 & 1926.1128 ⏱ ~20 min read 📝 10-question quiz
🚨Critical Exposure Limits — Know These First
Absolute Prohibition All BBB Tank Services employees are strictly prohibited from working in any atmosphere with a benzene level of 500 ppm or more (IDLH — Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health).
Requires Written CEO + Safety Director Authorization Working in any atmosphere that exceeds 250 ppm of benzene requires a policy exception authorized in writing by BBB's CEO AND Safety Director. A detailed Safe Action Plan must also be developed, reviewed, and signed by all designated employees before work starts.
Action Level vs. PEL The Action Level is 0.5 ppm (8-hour TWA) — triggers medical surveillance and monitoring requirements. The PEL (Permissible Exposure Limit) is 1.0 ppm (8-hour TWA) — exposure above this requires engineering controls and respirators.
📋1–3. Purpose, Scope & Key Definitions

This program applies to all BBB Tank Services employees and subcontractor employees who may be in an area where benzene is present or who may be accidentally exposed to benzene in the course of their work.

When exposure exceeds the PEL, this program is implemented to reduce employee exposure to or below the PEL — primarily through engineering and work practice controls. A project-specific written compliance program will be developed for each project with benzene exposure.

Key Definitions:

Action Level
Airborne concentration of benzene at 0.5 ppm calculated as an 8-hour time-weighted average (TWA). Triggers monitoring and medical surveillance.
PEL
Permissible Exposure Limit: 1.0 ppm (8-hour TWA). Exposure above this requires engineering controls and respiratory protection.
IDLH
Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health: 500 ppm or more. Employees are strictly prohibited from working in IDLH atmospheres.
Authorized Person
Any person specifically authorized by the employer whose duties require entry into a regulated benzene area.
Employee Exposure
Exposure to airborne benzene that would occur if the employee were not using respiratory protective equipment.
Benzene (C₆H₆)
Liquefied or gaseous benzene, including benzene contained in liquid mixtures and vapors released by those liquids.
👷4. Responsibilities
  • Project Manager / Site Supervisor — Responsible for disciplinary action resulting from failure to follow this program.
  • Director of Safety (or designee) — Responsible for monitoring and providing guidance for the implementation of this program.
  • First Line Supervisor / Foreman / Leadsperson — Responsible for the implementation and training of the Benzene Protection Program on-site.
📍5. Potential Exposure Locations

Employees may encounter benzene exposure in situations involving gasoline products during pipeline repair and maintenance operations. Potential locations include:

  • Gasoline and petroleum pipelines
  • Pipeline valve assemblies
  • Tank repair, maintenance, and cleaning operations at plant and pipeline facilities
  • Refinery operations
  • Marine, rail, bulk terminals, and service station operations
⚕6. Characteristics & Health Effects of Benzene

Benzene is a clear, colorless liquid with a pleasant, sweet odor. It is found in gasoline and many petroleum products.

⚠ Critical Warning The odor of benzene does NOT provide adequate warning of its hazard. Never rely on smell to determine if benzene levels are safe.

How benzene enters the body: Swallowing, inhaling, or skin/eye contact can all cause serious health effects.

Short-Term (Acute) Overexposure:

  • Breathlessness, irritability, euphoria, or feeling giddy
  • Eye, nose, and respiratory tract irritation
  • Headache, dizziness, nausea, or feeling intoxicated
  • Severe exposures may lead to convulsions and loss of consciousness

Long-Term (Chronic) Exposure:

Serious Long-Term Risk Chronic benzene exposure — even at relatively low concentrations — can cause various blood disorders ranging from anemia to leukemia. Leukemia is an irreversible and potentially fatal disease. Many blood disorders from benzene exposure may occur without symptoms.

Fire & Explosion Hazard:

  • Benzene liquid is highly flammable and its vapors can form explosive mixtures.
  • Benzene vapors are heavier than air and can travel along the ground — ignition can occur far from the handling site.
  • Hazardous decomposition products include toxic gases and vapors such as carbon monoxide.
🛡7. Safe Work Procedures
  • Site-specific safe work procedures will be developed for each project with benzene exposure.
  • Smoking, open flames, and all ignition sources are prohibited in areas where benzene is being handled or where there is benzene exposure.
  • Fire extinguishers must be readily available in areas where there is benzene exposure.
  • Host employers must inform BBB personnel of any benzene exposures before work commences.
  • All employees performing work will be notified and informed about the host employer's benzene safety procedures.
🏥8. Medical Surveillance

Medical surveillance is required for employees exposed to benzene at or above the action level 30 or more days per year, or at or above the PEL for 10 or more days per year.

Company Pays All Costs All costs for physical exams, laboratory work, and medical procedures under this program are paid by BBB Tank Services, LLC — at no cost to the employee.

Four Types of Medical Exams:

Initial Exam
Provided when an employee is first assigned to work where benzene exposure is possible. Includes occupational history, complete physical, and full blood count.
Periodic Exam
Provided annually to each exposed employee. Includes updated history, physical signs review, and complete blood count.
Post-Employment Exam
Required at the conclusion of any job requiring benzene physicals, before the employee is released or transferred to a non-benzene job site.
Emergency Exam
Required if an employee is exposed to benzene in an emergency. A urine sample (Phenol Test) must be collected. If phenol level is ≥75 mg, blood counts are repeated monthly for 3 months.
Physician's Written Opinion Within 15 days of each examination, BBB will obtain and provide the employee with a copy of the examining physician's written opinion, including any recommended work limitations.

Medical Removal Protection:

If a physician refers an employee to a hematologist/internist, the employee will be removed from benzene-exposure areas until the physician decides it is safe to return. BBB will maintain the employee's current wage rate, seniority, and other benefits for up to 6 months during removal.

🗄9. Record Keeping
40 yrs
All benzene exposure monitoring and medical surveillance records must be kept for the duration of employment plus 30 years, or 40 years — whichever is longer.

Records include all monitoring results, employee notifications of monitoring results, and all medical surveillance documentation. Records are stored in the employee's personnel folder at the Corporate Office and are available to proper authorities upon request.

Copies are provided only to the individual, their authorized representative, or their personal physician — and only by written request to the Corporate Office.

😷10–12. Respirator Use & Selection

Respirators are required during:

  • Periods while engineering and work practice controls are being installed.
  • Work operations where compliance with PEL using engineering controls is not feasible (e.g., vessel cleaning, maintenance with intermittent exposure).
  • When feasible controls are not yet sufficient to reduce exposure to or below the PEL.
  • All emergency situations.
Provided Free of Charge The company provides all approved respirators and filters at no cost to the employee. Training on proper use, maintenance, and limitations is also provided.

Air-purifying elements must be replaced at the expiration of their service life OR at the beginning of each shift in which they are used, whichever comes first. All selected respirators must be approved by NIOSH.

Any employee who cannot use a negative-pressure respirator will be allowed to use a powered air-purifying respirator or supplied-air respirator.

🦺13. Protective Clothing & Equipment
  • Protective clothing (barrier overalls, aprons, sleeves, gloves, boots, etc.) must be worn over any body parts that could be exposed to liquid benzene.
  • Splash-proof safety goggles must be worn if benzene may contact the eyes.
  • A face shield must be worn if the face could be splashed with liquid benzene.
  • All PPE is provided by the company at no cost to the employee.
  • PPE must meet the requirements of 29 CFR 1910.133.
📊14. Exposure Levels — Required Actions

The level of protection required increases with benzene concentration. Know which level applies to your work area:

Benzene Level Risk Level Required Actions
< 0.5 ppm Low Annual project evaluation, initial medical surveillance, annual monitoring.
0.5 – 1.0 ppm
(At or above Action Level)		 Moderate Initial medical surveillance; semi-annual medical exams; quarterly monitoring until two consecutive results show <0.5 ppm.
1.0 – 10.0 ppm
(Above PEL)		 High Chemical cartridge respirator (organic vapor, half mask) or supplied-air with half mask required. Medical surveillance within 30 days; semi-annual exams; monthly monitoring.
10.0 – 50.0 ppm Very High Immediately withdraw all non-essential employees. Full-face respirator or SCBA required. All training, medical surveillance, and respiratory programs must be completed before entry. Sign in/out required.
50.0 – 250 ppm Extreme Supplied-air respirator with full mask in positive-pressure mode required. Engineering controls only. All surveillance and training completed; sign in/out required.
250 – 500 ppm CEO Auth Required Written authorization from CEO AND Safety Director required. Safe Action Plan signed by all employees. Supplied-air respirator with full-face piece, helmet or hood, positive pressure mode.
> 500 ppm PROHIBITED Employees are STRICTLY PROHIBITED from working in this atmosphere. IDLH condition.
🚪15. Escape Procedures
Immediate Escape Required Benzene can be fatal in a short period at concentrations greater than 10,000 ppm. Anyone exposed to such high concentrations must use an organic vapor gas mask or self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA) with full face piece for escape — and must begin escaping immediately upon sensing vapors or receiving an alarm.

Emergency situation — 100 ppm or greater: If an employee is exposed to a benzene release of 100 ppm or more due to equipment failure or other emergency, the employee must participate in a special medical testing program. Special tests will be provided by the end of the employee's work shift. If results are positive, additional tests are provided as soon as practicable and repeated one month later.
