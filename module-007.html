Electrical Safety Program

📅 Rev. 10/31/2025 ⚖ OSHA 29 CFR 1910.332–333 · NFPA 70E ⏱ ~18 min read 📝 10-question quiz
⚡1. Purpose

This program is adopted to prevent electrically related injuries to personnel from direct or indirect electrical contact, and to prevent damage to company property and client facilities when work is performed near or on energized equipment or circuits.

Authorized Personnel Only Only employees who are qualified, knowledgeable about this program, and authorized by the company may repair or replace electrical components or electrically powered tools or equipment. All others are prohibited.

Electricity has long been recognized as a serious workplace hazard, exposing employees to electric shock, electrocution, fires, and explosions. Safe work practices regarding electricity shall be followed by all employees as they relate to their specific job assignments.

👷2. Responsibilities
Management Must:
Provide training for all qualified and unqualified employees. Conduct inspections to identify electrical safety deficiencies. Guard and correct all electrical deficiencies promptly. Ensure all new electrical installations meet codes and regulations.
Employees Must:
Report electrical deficiencies immediately. Do NOT work on electrical equipment unless authorized and trained. Thoroughly inspect all electrical equipment before use. Know how to distinguish exposed live parts from other equipment parts.
🎓3. Training — Qualified vs. Unqualified Persons
Unqualified Persons
Must be trained in any electrically related safety practices necessary for their safety. They may NOT work on or near exposed energized parts. Must maintain specific clearance distances from all live electrical equipment.
Qualified Persons
Must be trained in: distinguishing live parts, determining nominal voltage of exposed parts, and clearance distances per 1910.333(c). Must complete ALL training requirements in this program. Must be capable of safely working on energized circuits and familiar with PPE, insulating materials, and insulated tools.
Who Faces Higher Electrical Risk? Occupations with higher-than-normal electrical accident risk include: blue collar supervisors, electricians, industrial machine operators, mechanics and repairers, painters, riggers and roustabouts, stationary engineers, and welders.
🔌4–5. De-Energized & Energized Parts

If an employee is exposed to live energized parts, those parts shall be de-energized before work begins — with two exceptions:

  • De-energizing would create additional or increased hazards (e.g., deactivating emergency alarm systems or hazardous location ventilation).
  • De-energizing is not feasible due to equipment design or operational limitations (e.g., testing circuits that can only be tested while energized).
< 50V
Live parts operating at less than 50 volts to ground need not be de-energized if there will be no increased exposure to electrical burns or explosion due to electric arcs.
De-energized Does NOT Mean Safe Conductors and parts of electric equipment that have been de-energized but have NOT been locked out or tagged shall be treated as energized parts and all energized-part precautions apply.

If live parts cannot be de-energized, other safety-related work practices must be used to protect employees from direct contact with any part of their body or indirect contact through conductive objects.

🔒7–11. Lockout & Tagout Procedures
Both Lock AND Tag Required A lock AND a tag shall be placed on each disconnecting means used to de-energize circuits and equipment. Lockout-only or tagout-only is NOT permitted as a safe work procedure, except in specific documented circumstances when a lock cannot be applied.

When applying locks and tags:

  • Locks must be attached to prevent any person from operating the disconnecting means without undue force or tools.
  • Each tag must contain a statement prohibiting unauthorized operation of the disconnecting means and removal of the tag.
  • Control circuit devices such as push buttons, selector switches, and interlocks may NOT be used as the sole means of de-energizing circuits or equipment.
  • Stored electric energy that could endanger personnel must be released. Capacitors must be discharged.

Verifying the de-energized condition:

  • A qualified person must operate the equipment controls or otherwise verify it cannot be restarted.
  • A qualified person must use test equipment to verify circuit elements are de-energized.
  • Testing must also check for inadvertently induced voltage or voltage back-feed.
  • If the circuit is more than 600 volts nominal, test equipment must be checked for proper operation immediately after the test.
Re-Energizing — Lock/Tag Removal Each lock and tag must be removed by the employee who applied it, or under their direct supervision. If the employee is absent, a qualified person designated for this task may remove it — but only if the on-duty supervisor confirms the employee is unavailable AND ensures the employee is notified the lock/tag was removed before they return to work.

Before re-energizing, a qualified person must visually inspect and test to confirm all tools, jumpers, shorts, and grounds have been removed. All employees must be warned to stay clear, and a visual determination must be made that all employees are clear.

🏗12–14. Working Near Overhead Lines & Clearance Distances

If work is to be performed near overhead lines, the lines shall be de-energized and grounded, or other protective measures provided, before work starts. Only qualified persons may work on energized circuit parts that cannot be de-energized.

10 ft
Minimum clearance for unqualified persons from unguarded energized overhead lines rated at 50 kV or below. Add 4 inches for every 10 kV over 50 kV.

Unqualified persons are specifically prohibited from performing work on or near exposed energized parts. Unqualified persons working on the ground may not bring any conductive object closer to unguarded energized overhead lines than the 10-foot minimum.

🚛15. Vehicular & Mechanical Equipment Near Power Lines
10 ft
Any vehicle or mechanical equipment with elevated parts must maintain a 10-foot clearance from energized overhead lines. For voltages over 50 kV, add 4 inches for every 10 kV over that voltage.
  • Vehicle in transit with structure lowered: clearance may be reduced to 4 feet (plus 4 in. per 10 kV over 50 kV).
  • If insulating barriers are installed and rated for the voltage: clearance may be reduced to within the designed working dimensions of the barrier.
  • Employees standing on the ground may NOT contact the vehicle or its attachments unless using protective equipment rated for the voltage or the equipment is positioned so no un-insulated part can come closer than permitted.
  • If equipment near overhead lines is intentionally grounded, employees must NOT stand at the grounding location when there is a possibility of overhead line contact.
📋16–22. Additional Electrical Safety Rules

Illumination (Section 16)

  • Employees may NOT enter spaces with exposed energized parts unless illumination is sufficient to perform work safely.
  • Employees may NOT perform tasks near exposed energized parts where lack of lighting precludes observation of the work.
  • Employees may NOT reach blindly into areas that may contain energized parts — ever.

Portable Ladders (Section 19)

Nonconductive Side Rails Required Portable ladders must have nonconductive side rails if used where the employee or ladder could contact exposed energized parts.

Conductive Apparel (Section 20)

  • Conductive articles of jewelry and clothing — watches, bracelets, rings, key chains, necklaces, metalized aprons, metal headgear — may NOT be worn near exposed energized parts.

Exception: Such articles may be worn if they are rendered nonconductive by covering, wrapping, or other insulating means.

Confined Workspaces (Section 17)

  • When working in a confined or enclosed space with exposed energized parts, protective shields, barriers, or insulating materials must be provided and used to avoid inadvertent contact.
  • Doors and hinged panels must be secured to prevent them swinging into an employee and causing contact with energized parts.

Interlocks (Section 22)

Defeating Safety Interlocks Only a qualified person following established safe work procedures may defeat an electrical safety interlock — and only temporarily while working on the equipment. The interlock system must be returned to its operable condition immediately when work is completed.
