Respiratory Protection Program

Selecting, fitting, and maintaining respirators that protect BBB Tank Services employees from petroleum vapors, toxic gases, chemical fumes, and oxygen-deficient atmospheres.

01 Respiratory Hazards in Tank Services Work

▼

BBB Tank Services employees are regularly exposed to respiratory hazards that are invisible, odorless (or olfactory-fatiguing), and immediately life-threatening if uncontrolled. Understanding what hazards are present — and why each one demands specific protection — is the foundation of using respiratory protection effectively.

Primary Respiratory Hazards at BBB Tank Services

HazardSourceOSHA PEL / IDLHKey Risk
Hydrogen Sulfide (H&sub2;S) Crude oil, petroleum sludge, produced water, sour service tanks PEL: 20 ppm ceiling; IDLH: 100 ppm Immediate olfactory paralysis at high concentrations; rapid incapacitation and death
Benzene Crude oil, gasoline, aromatic hydrocarbons; present in vapors above most petroleum tanks PEL: 1 ppm TWA; IDLH: 500 ppm Known human carcinogen; causes leukemia and aplastic anemia with chronic exposure
Petroleum Vapors (VOCs) Tank vapors, sludge disturbance, product transfer Product-dependent; gasoline: 900 ppm PEL (as aliphatic hydrocarbon) CNS effects, fire/explosion hazard; often contain benzene and H&sub2;S
Oxygen Deficiency Tank interiors, confined spaces, inert gas-purged vessels Acceptable: 19.5–23.5%; IDLH: <16% Rapid incapacitation without warning; O&sub2; levels can drop to lethal levels in seconds
Carbon Monoxide (CO) Vehicle exhaust near tank vents, gas-powered equipment in confined spaces PEL: 50 ppm TWA; IDLH: 1,200 ppm Colorless, odorless; carboxyhemoglobin formation leads to unconsciousness
Lead Dust/Fume Grinding, blasting, or cutting lead-painted tank shells PEL: 50 μg/m³ TWA; IDLH: 700 mg/m³ Cumulative neurotoxin; no safe level (see Module 019)
Acid/Caustic Mist Chemical cleaning operations, mixing of cleaning solutions HCl: 5 ppm ceiling; NaOH: 2 mg/m³ ceiling Severe respiratory tract irritation and chemical burns
Silica Dust Abrasive blasting with silica-containing media on tank structures PEL: 0.05 mg/m³ TWA (respirable); IDLH: 25 mg/m³ Silicosis, lung cancer; OSHA Action Level is 0.025 mg/m³
OSHA 29 CFR 1910.134 Requirements: When atmospheric concentrations of a respiratory hazard exceed the permissible exposure limit (PEL) and engineering controls cannot reduce exposure to safe levels, BBB Tank Services must establish a written Respiratory Protection Program (RPP), provide appropriate respirators at no cost to employees, train workers, conduct medical evaluations, perform fit testing, and maintain the respirators.
⚠ Air-Purifying Respirators Cannot Create Oxygen. The most critical rule in respirator selection: air-purifying respirators (including N95s, half-face APRs, and full-face APRs) remove contaminants from the air but do nothing about oxygen concentration. In any oxygen-deficient atmosphere (<19.5% O&sub2;) — including tank interiors — only atmosphere-supplying respirators (SCBA or supplied-air) are acceptable.

02 Respirator Types & Assigned Protection Factors

▼

Respirators are classified into two broad categories: Air-Purifying Respirators (APR), which filter or chemically react with contaminants in the ambient air, and Atmosphere-Supplying Respirators (ASR), which provide clean air from an independent source. Each type is assigned an Assigned Protection Factor (APF) — the level of protection a properly functioning, correctly worn respirator provides to a typical wearer.

How APF Is Used in Respirator Selection

To select a respirator for a known hazard concentration: Required APF = Hazard Concentration ÷ Exposure Limit. Choose a respirator whose APF equals or exceeds the required APF.

Example: H&sub2;S concentration = 300 ppm; OSHA PEL = 20 ppm ceiling. Required APF = 300 ÷ 20 = 15. A half-face APR (APF 10) is NOT adequate. A full-face APR (APF 50) IS adequate.

Filtering Facepiece (N95, N100)

APF 10
  • Disposable; covers nose and mouth
  • N95: 95% efficient vs. non-oil aerosols
  • N100/P100: 99.97% efficient
  • No cartridges; cannot protect against gases or vapors
  • Use: nuisance dust, non-toxic aerosols only
  • Not adequate for petroleum vapor or H&sub2;S

Half-Face APR

APF 10
  • Reusable rubber or silicone facepiece
  • Covers nose and mouth; does not protect eyes
  • Uses replaceable cartridges/canisters
  • Requires fit testing; tight seal required
  • Cannot be used in O&sub2;-deficient atmospheres
  • Use: moderate vapor/gas exposures up to 10× PEL

Full-Face APR

APF 50
  • Covers entire face including eyes
  • Uses replaceable cartridges/canisters
  • Requires fit testing
  • Cannot be used in O&sub2;-deficient atmospheres
  • Provides eye protection from vapor and splashes
  • Use: higher-exposure vapor/gas tasks up to 50× PEL

PAPR (Powered Air-Purifying)

APF 25–1,000
  • Battery-powered blower forces air through filters/cartridges
  • Hood/helmet: APF 25; tight-fitting full-face: APF 1,000
  • Loose-fitting hoods do not require fit testing
  • Heavier; better comfort for extended wear
  • Cannot be used in O&sub2;-deficient atmospheres

Supplied Air Respirator (SAR)

APF 25–10,000
  • Breathable air supplied via airline from compressor or cylinder manifold
  • Can be used in O&sub2;-deficient atmospheres
  • Continuous flow (APF 25 hood / 1,000 tight-face) or pressure-demand (APF 1,000+)
  • Limited mobility; tethered to air supply
  • Must include escape cylinder for emergency egress

SCBA (Self-Contained Breathing Apparatus)

APF 10,000
  • Compressed air cylinder worn on back; full self-contained
  • Pressure-demand type required for IDLH atmospheres
  • Full mobility; limited by cylinder duration (30–60 min)
  • Required for all IDLH atmospheres and unknown concentrations
  • Required for confined space rescue operations

03 Cartridge & Filter Selection

▼

Selecting the correct cartridge or filter for the hazard is as important as selecting the correct respirator type. A cartridge matched to the wrong chemical provides no protection — the contaminant passes through as if the cartridge were not there.

NIOSH Cartridge Color Code System

NIOSH establishes a standardized color coding for respirator cartridges and canisters to aid in correct selection. Always verify by reading the label — color alone is not sufficient, as some cartridges combine protection types:

■ GreenAmmonia & methylamineAmmonia and related aminesAmmonia refrigerant systems (rare at tank sites)

■ BlackOV + P100 combinationOrganic vapors AND particulates (most common combination cartridge)Lead/paint removal with solvent vapors; most versatile for tank exterior work
■ OrangeOrganic vapor, H&sub2;S (some)Some manufacturers use orange for H&sub2;S-specific cartridges; always verify labelVerify label for H&sub2;S — not all orange cartridges cover H&sub2;S at meaningful concentrations
⚠ H&sub2;S Cartridge Limitations. Standard organic vapor (OV) cartridges provide limited and unreliable protection against hydrogen sulfide. H&sub2;S at concentrations approaching IDLH (100 ppm) requires either a dedicated H&sub2;S-specific cartridge (and only on a full-face APR) or supplied-air/SCBA. At BBB Tank Services, the default for any work involving H&sub2;S risk above 10 ppm is supplied air or SCBA — not cartridge respirators. See Module 013 (H&sub2;S Safety) for the full H&sub2;S respirator selection protocol.

Cartridge Service Life & End-of-Service-Life Indicators (ESLI)

Organic vapor cartridges have a finite capacity — they can only absorb a limited amount of organic vapor before they are exhausted and vapor begins passing through (breakthrough). Exhausted cartridges often cannot be detected by smell because olfactory fatigue prevents detection of the very vapors that indicate failure.

  • OSHA requires a cartridge change schedule based on: the type of contaminant, its concentration, humidity, temperature, work rate, and cartridge capacity — determined before use, not by waiting for odor breakthrough
  • Change cartridges before the shift if they have already been used. Cartridges stored overnight with vapors adsorbed can desorb (off-gas) the contaminant back into the facepiece at high concentrations when donned the next day
  • Change immediately if you smell or taste the contaminant through the respirator (indicates breakthrough or poor seal), if breathing resistance increases significantly, or if the cartridge has been dropped in water
  • Some cartridges have End-of-Service-Life Indicators (ESLI) that change color when the sorbent is exhausted — check before each use
  • P100 filters (particulate only) can be reused until breathing resistance is excessive or the filter is physically damaged; they do not have a chemical capacity limit

04 Medical Evaluation & Fit Testing

▼

Before any employee is required to wear a tight-fitting respirator, OSHA mandates two prerequisites: a medical evaluation and a fit test. These are not formalities — they exist because wearing a respirator places physiological demands on the user, and a respirator that does not seal to the face provides no meaningful protection regardless of its ratings.

Medical Evaluation (OSHA 1910.134 Appendix C)

A physician or other licensed health care professional (PLHCP) must evaluate whether a worker can safely wear a respirator. The evaluation uses OSHA’s mandatory medical questionnaire (Appendix C to 1910.134) and may include a physical examination. Conditions that can prevent safe respirator use include:

Conditions Potentially Disqualifying

  • Asthma or other significant lung disease
  • Cardiac conditions (heart disease, uncontrolled hypertension)
  • Claustrophobia that prevents facepiece use
  • Epilepsy or seizure disorder
  • Prior lung disease (COPD, emphysema, chronic bronchitis)

Medical Evaluation Requirements

  • Must be completed before fit testing or respirator use
  • Must be repeated if health status changes or PLHCP recommends it
  • Employee answers questionnaire confidentially — employer receives only pass/fail clearance, not medical details
  • Provided at no cost to the employee

Fit Testing

A fit test verifies that a specific respirator model and size creates an adequate seal on an individual worker’s face. Because facial geometry varies significantly between individuals, the same respirator model may provide excellent protection for one person and poor protection for another. Fit testing is required:

  • Before first use of any tight-fitting respirator
  • Annually thereafter
  • Any time the worker reports a change in their physical condition that could affect fit (significant weight change, facial scarring, dental work, new prescription for corrective lenses)
  • When a different respirator model, style, or size is to be used

Qualitative Fit Test (QLFT)

  • Pass/fail test using the wearer’s sensory detection of a test agent (isoamyl acetate “banana oil,” saccharin, bitrex, or irritant smoke)
  • No instrumentation required; less expensive
  • Valid for half-face APRs with APF up to 10 only
  • Not acceptable for full-face respirators, PAPRs, or higher-protection devices

Quantitative Fit Test (QNFT)

  • Measures actual face-seal leakage using a probe and particle counter (e.g., TSI PortaCount)
  • Produces a numerical fit factor; must meet minimum for the respirator type
  • Required for full-face APRs (minimum fit factor 500) and higher-protection devices
  • Acceptable for all respirator types; more objective and rigorous
⚠ Beards and Facial Hair Invalidate Fit Tests. Any facial hair in the sealing zone of a tight-fitting facepiece — even 1–2 days of stubble — prevents an adequate face seal and renders the fit test invalid. Workers who must wear tight-fitting respirators must be clean-shaven at the seal area. Mustaches above the upper lip (outside the sealing zone) are generally acceptable; verify with the respirator manufacturer’s guidance.

05 Donning, Seal Checks & Doffing

▼

Correct donning and a successful user seal check are required every time a tight-fitting respirator is put on. A fit test passes on an annual basis — a user seal check verifies the seal is adequate for each individual use, since conditions change daily.

Donning a Half-Face or Full-Face APR

  1. 1
    Inspect before donning. Check the facepiece for cracks, tears, or deformation. Confirm cartridges are the correct type, properly seated, and not expired or saturated. Check exhalation valve for proper seating and absence of cracks.
  2. 2
    Position the facepiece. Place the respirator on your face with the nose cup centered over the nose. The chin should seat fully into the chin cup. Do not let the bottom seal rest on the chin instead of below it.
  3. 3
    Position straps before tightening. For most half-face respirators: bottom straps go behind the neck, top straps go over the crown. For full-face: position all four straps before tightening any of them.
  4. 4
    Tighten straps evenly. Tighten bottom straps first, then top straps. Tighten until the facepiece seats firmly — not until it is uncomfortable. Over-tightening can distort the face seal and actually reduce fit quality.
  5. 5
    Perform a user seal check (see below) before entering the hazardous area.

User Seal Check — Two Methods

Negative Pressure Seal Check

  • Cover the inhalation valve(s) or cartridge openings with your palms
  • Inhale gently and hold your breath for 10 seconds
  • If the facepiece collapses slightly toward your face and holds that slight collapse — seal is good
  • If air leaks in around the face seal — adjust and repeat
  • Most useful for half-face respirators

Positive Pressure Seal Check

  • Cover the exhalation valve with your palm
  • Exhale gently and hold for 10 seconds
  • If slight positive pressure builds inside the facepiece without noticeable leakage at the seal — seal is good
  • If you feel air leaking at the face seal — adjust and repeat
  • Preferred check after positive-pressure (SCBA/SAR) use
⚠ If the Seal Check Fails Three Times. If you cannot achieve a good seal after three attempts at adjustment, do not enter the hazardous area. The problem may be the wrong respirator model or size for your face geometry, or a defect in the facepiece. Report to your supervisor and request a different respirator or return to fit testing.

Doffing (Removing) a Respirator

  • Remove while still in the clean area — do not remove the respirator in a contaminated atmosphere
  • Remove by grasping the straps behind the head — avoid touching the front of the facepiece (contaminated surface)
  • For chemical work or lead tasks: remove outer gloves first, then remove the respirator by the straps, then wash hands before touching the facepiece further
  • Do not lay the respirator face-down on a contaminated surface between uses
Scenario — Seal Check Failure

The Beard Problem

A worker is issued a half-face APR for an organic vapor task inside a tank shell. He has been growing a beard for a week. He performs the negative pressure seal check — he can clearly feel air entering along his jaw on both sides. He adjusts the straps tighter, but the same leak is present. He reasons that it is a minor leak and the cartridges will still reduce exposure significantly.

Correct response: Stop. A failing seal check means the respirator is providing unknown and unreliable protection — potentially no protection at all. APR effectiveness depends on the seal: if air is bypassing the cartridge through the face seal leak, the wearer is breathing unfiltered atmosphere. He must not enter the hazardous area until he is either clean-shaven at the seal area or a different protection method (loose-fitting PAPR, supplied air) that does not require a face seal is substituted.

06 Inspection, Maintenance, Storage & Program Requirements

▼

Respirator Inspection

All respirators must be inspected before each use and after each cleaning. Emergency-use respirators (SCBAs kept for rescue) must be inspected monthly and after each use.

Facepiece — Check For:

  • Cracks, tears, or distortion in the rubber/silicone
  • Damaged or missing head straps
  • Lens cracks, scratches, or fogging (full-face)
  • Exhalation valve: flat, clean, without cracks; seats properly
  • Inhalation valve: present, undamaged, seats properly

Cartridges & Fittings — Check For:

  • Correct cartridge type for the hazard (read the label)
  • Cartridge securely threaded or snapped onto the facepiece — no play or wobble
  • No signs of moisture damage or saturation
  • Expiration date not exceeded
  • ESLI color-change indicator (if present) not triggered

Cleaning & Disinfecting

  • Clean after each use, or before being given to another wearer (respirators should be assigned to individual workers when possible)
  • Remove cartridges and valves before cleaning the facepiece (do not immerse cartridges in water)
  • Wash with a mild detergent and warm water, or use OSHA-approved disinfectant wipes
  • Rinse thoroughly and allow to air dry completely before storing
  • Do not use solvents, bleach, or harsh cleaners on the facepiece — these degrade silicone and rubber
  • Inspect exhalation and inhalation valves after cleaning; replace if deformed by the cleaning process

Storage

  • Store in a sealed bag or dedicated respirator case away from dust, chemicals, sunlight, and extreme temperatures
  • Do not hang on a hook by the straps — this deforms the facepiece and destroys the seal geometry
  • Do not store with cartridges attached in the long term — adsorbed vapors can off-gas into the facepiece
  • Store separately from contaminated PPE, chemicals, or materials that could off-gas into the sealed bag
  • Emergency SCBAs stored in vehicles must be protected from extreme heat (Texas summers can exceed 140°F inside vehicle cabins — this damages seals and reduces cylinder pressure through thermal expansion)

BBB Tank Services Written Respiratory Protection Program

OSHA requires a written RPP when respirators are required. The BBB Tank Services RPP includes:

Required Elements

  • Procedures for selecting respirators
  • Medical evaluation procedures
  • Fit testing procedures
  • Training requirements
  • Procedures for use in IDLH atmospheres

Maintenance Elements

  • Cleaning, disinfecting, storage, and repair procedures
  • Inspection procedures and schedules
  • Cartridge change-out schedule
  • SCBA monthly inspection records

Administrative Elements

  • Name of the program administrator
  • Evaluation of the program effectiveness
  • Annual review and update of the written program
  • Recordkeeping for medical evaluations and fit tests
✓ Voluntary Use Respirators. If a worker voluntarily chooses to wear a respirator when it is not required by BBB Tank Services (e.g., wearing a filtering facepiece for personal comfort during a dusty but low-hazard task), the employer must still provide Appendix D of OSHA 1910.134 (a brief information sheet on the limitations of respirator use) to the employee. No fit testing or medical evaluation is required for voluntary filtering facepiece use, but employers must ensure voluntary use does not create a hazard (e.g., wearing a respirator in a hot confined space when it is not needed can contribute to heat stress).
