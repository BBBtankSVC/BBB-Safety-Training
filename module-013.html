GFCI & Assured Grounding Program

Understanding ground fault protection, assured grounding requirements, and OSHA compliance for electrical safety on BBB Tank Services job sites.

01 Ground Faults — What They Are & Why They Kill

▼

Electrical current always seeks the path of least resistance back to its source. A ground fault occurs when current escapes its intended path and flows through an unintended conductor — which could be a person, a puddle of water, or a metal surface connected to ground.

⚡ Why This Matters: It takes as little as 10 milliamps (0.010 A) to cause muscle freeze — the inability to let go of an energized object. As little as 100 mA passing through the chest for one second can cause fatal ventricular fibrillation. A standard 15-amp circuit can deliver 150 times the lethal dose.

Common Causes of Ground Faults at Tank Services Job Sites

Equipment & Cord Issues

  • Damaged or cut extension cord insulation
  • Worn or cracked tool casings exposing internal wiring
  • Missing or broken ground pins on plugs
  • Moisture inside power tools
  • Improperly repaired splices or connections

Environmental Conditions

  • Water on tank surfaces, grating, or decking
  • Condensation inside tanks and confined spaces
  • Wet concrete or soil completing the return path
  • Metal scaffolding and tank shells conducting fault current
  • Cleaning water and chemical spray during tank work

How a Fault Flows Through a Person

When a worker touches a tool or surface carrying fault current while simultaneously contacting a grounded surface (wet ground, metal tank, wet boots), they complete the circuit. Current enters at the point of contact, travels through the body — often through the chest and heart — and exits through the grounding contact. Severity depends on current magnitude, path through the body, and duration of contact.

Tank Work Is Higher Risk: Confined spaces inside tanks are often wet, humid, and surrounded by conductive metal. Workers in cramped positions may be unable to pull away from energized equipment. These conditions make ground fault protection non-negotiable on every BBB Tank Services job site.

02 How GFCI Works & Types of Devices

▼

A Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI) continuously monitors current flowing out through the hot wire and returning through the neutral wire. Under normal conditions these are equal. If even a tiny amount leaks through an unintended path, the GFCI detects the imbalance and shuts off the circuit almost instantly.

Detection Threshold & Response Time

Trip Sensitivity

  • Trips at 4–6 milliamps of imbalance
  • Well below the 10 mA muscle-freeze threshold
  • Well below the 100 mA fibrillation threshold
  • Cannot be adjusted or defeated in the field

Response Time

  • Interrupts the circuit in 1/40th of a second or less
  • Fast enough to prevent electrocution in most scenarios
  • Does NOT protect against line-to-neutral contact
  • Does NOT replace the circuit breaker (which protects wiring)
Important Distinction: A circuit breaker protects wiring from overheating and fire. The GFCI protects people from electrocution due to ground faults. Both are required and serve different purposes.

Types of GFCI Devices

TypeDescriptionTypical BBB Tank Services Use
Receptacle-Type Built into an outlet with TEST and RESET buttons. Protects that outlet and any outlets wired downstream. Fixed receptacles in shops, trailers, and permanent site structures
Portable Cord-Type Plug-in unit with built-in GFCI. Plugs into any standard outlet and protects all tools connected to it. Primary field method — required whenever site outlets are not confirmed GFCI-protected
Breaker-Type GFCI built into a circuit breaker installed in the panel, protecting the entire branch circuit. Permanent electrical panels in facilities; not typically present at mobile field sites
✓ Field Rule: On every BBB Tank Services field job, each crew must have at least one cord-type GFCI for every power tool in use. If you cannot verify a receptacle is GFCI-protected, use a portable cord-type GFCI — no exceptions.

03 OSHA Requirements

▼

OSHA mandates ground fault protection for temporary power situations. BBB Tank Services operations may fall under Construction or General Industry standards depending on scope — both impose similar requirements.

Construction Standard — 29 CFR 1926.404(b)(1)

For all construction worksites, OSHA requires one of the following for 120V, single-phase, 15- and 20-amp receptacle outlets that are not part of the permanent building wiring:

  • Option A: GFCI protection — All such receptacles are protected by a GFCI device, OR
  • Option B: Assured Equipment Grounding Conductor Program (AEGCP) — A written program with specific inspection and testing requirements (see Section 4)
BBB Tank Services Policy: We use GFCI protection as our primary method of compliance. The AEGCP supplements GFCI for equipment where cord-type GFCIs cannot be used — it does not replace GFCI as the default approach.

General Industry — 29 CFR 1910.304 & 1910.305

For facilities and non-construction environments, OSHA requires GFCI protection for receptacles in bathrooms, rooftops, kitchens, wet areas, and temporary construction-like power installations (e.g., generator power delivered to a job site inside a facility).

OSHA Penalties (2025)

Violation TypeMaximum Penalty
Serious (worker exposed to shock hazard without GFCI)Up to $16,550 per violation
Willful or RepeatedUp to $165,514 per violation
Failure to AbateUp to $16,550 per day past abatement date
⚠ OSHA Enforcement Focus: Electrocution is one of OSHA’s “Fatal Four” in construction. OSHA inspectors specifically check for GFCI compliance. Non-compliance results in public citations that can affect BBB Tank Services’ ability to bid on customer contracts.

04 Assured Equipment Grounding Conductor Program (AEGCP)

▼

The AEGCP is OSHA’s written, systematic alternative to GFCI. It verifies that equipment grounding conductors are intact through regular inspection and testing. It applies when GFCI devices cannot be used for specific equipment.

⚠ Key Difference: A GFCI automatically protects the worker in real time. The AEGCP relies on periodic testing — it does not interrupt a fault. GFCI is always the preferred method.

Required Elements of the BBB Tank Services AEGCP

Per OSHA 29 CFR 1926.404(b)(1)(iii), the program must include:

  1. 1
    Written Program. A written AEGCP document is maintained on every job site where the program applies, identifying the responsible person.
  2. 2
    Equipment Identification. All covered cord sets, receptacles, and tools are distinctly marked — typically by color-coded tape changed each quarter.
  3. 3
    Visual Inspection Before Each Use. A competent person visually inspects every cord, plug, tool, and receptacle for damage before each day’s use. Damaged equipment is immediately removed from service.
  4. 4
    Continuity Test. All equipment grounding conductors are tested for continuity with an ohmmeter before first use, after any repair, and at 3-month intervals. Results are documented.
  5. 5
    Receptacle Polarity & Grounding Test. All receptacles and plugs are tested to verify correct wiring (hot, neutral, ground) before first use, after repair, and quarterly. A plug-in outlet tester is used.
  6. 6
    Documentation. All test results are recorded: date, equipment description and ID, and name of the person who performed the test. Records are kept on-site and available for OSHA inspection.
Scenario — Field Application

When Would BBB Tank Services Use the AEGCP?

A crew is operating a large industrial vacuum (over 20 amps, 3-phase) that cannot connect through a standard cord-type GFCI. The AEGCP applies: the equipment must be inspected before use, continuity and polarity tests must be current, and the equipment must bear the current quarter’s color-coded tape.

Even in this scenario, any 120V single-phase tools used alongside the vacuum — lights, hand tools, extension cords — must still use GFCI protection.

05 Inspection, Testing & Tagging

▼

Daily Visual Inspection — Every Worker’s Responsibility

Before using any cord, tool, or GFCI device, perform a quick visual inspection. This takes less than 60 seconds and can prevent a fatality.

ItemWhat to Look ForIf Damaged — Do This
Extension CordsCuts, abrasions, exposed copper, burn marks, missing ground pinRemove from service. Tag “DO NOT USE.” Return to supervisor.
Power Tool CordsFraying at cord-tool junction, exposed wiring, cracked insulationRemove tool from service. Tag “DO NOT USE.” Do not repair in the field.
Plugs & ConnectorsCracked casing, bent or missing prongs, burn marks, loose connectionsRemove from service. Do not straighten bent prongs.
Cord-Type GFCICracked housing, damaged indicator lights, TEST/RESET buttons not functioningTest using TEST/RESET procedure. If GFCI fails, remove from service.
Receptacle GFCICracked face, loose mounting, unresponsive TEST/RESET buttonsDo not use. Notify supervisor — requires replacement by a qualified electrician.

GFCI TEST/RESET Procedure

All GFCI devices must be tested before first use each day and after any tripping event.

  1. 1
    Plug in a test load. Plug a lamp or trouble light into the GFCI output to confirm power is passing through normally.
  2. 2
    Press the TEST button. The load must go off immediately. If it does not, the GFCI is faulty — remove from service.
  3. 3
    Press the RESET button. The load must come back on. If it does not reset or the button does not click and hold, the GFCI is faulty — remove from service.
  4. 4
    Document for AEGCP equipment. Record date, GFCI ID, and your name in the inspection log. Note any anomalies in the daily safety report.

Quarterly Color-Code System (AEGCP)

Color Schedule Example

  • Green — Q1 (Jan–Mar)
  • Blue — Q2 (Apr–Jun)
  • Orange— Q3 (Jul–Sep)
  • Purple— Q4 (Oct–Dec)

Tape Application Rules

  • Applied to the plug end and at the cord-tool exit
  • Previous quarter’s tape removed before new tape applied
  • Wrong-quarter or missing tape = remove from service
  • Tape applied only after passing all tests

06 Safe Work Practices & When a GFCI Trips

▼

Safe Electrical Practices — Daily Habits

  • Never remove the ground pin. Eliminating the ground pin eliminates grounding protection entirely.
  • Never use damaged cords. Tape over damaged insulation is not a repair — it is a masked hazard. Remove the cord from service.
  • Route cords safely. Keep cords out of walkways and away from sharp edges. Never run them under mats where damage goes unseen. Use cord bridges at crossing points.
  • Keep cords out of water. If a cord or tool gets wet, unplug it, allow it to dry, and inspect it before re-use. GFCI protection does not make wet equipment safe to use.
  • Unplug tools before changing accessories (bits, blades, wheels). Never yank a cord from an outlet — grip the plug body.
  • Use only three-wire extension cords with three-prong tools. Never use a two-prong extension cord with a grounded tool.
  • Keep extension cords as short as feasible. Long runs increase resistance, heat, voltage drop — and create more cord exposed to damage.

When a GFCI Trips — Step by Step

⚡ A tripping GFCI is a warning, not an inconvenience. Never simply reset it and resume work without investigating. A persistent or recurring trip means a ground fault exists and must be identified and corrected before work continues.
  1. 1
    Stop work. Unplug the tool or equipment. Do not attempt a reset with equipment still connected.
  2. 2
    Inspect the tool and cord. Look for visible damage. If found, tag “DO NOT USE” and remove from service. Do not reset.
  3. 3
    If no visible damage, press RESET once. Connect a known-good tool and test. If the GFCI trips again with a different tool, the problem is in the GFCI or circuit — notify your supervisor.
  4. 4
    If the GFCI trips again — stop. Do not continue resetting. A real fault exists. Notify your supervisor and a qualified electrician before any equipment is used on that circuit.
  5. 5
    Document and report. Record in the daily safety log: date, time, which GFCI, what equipment was connected, number of trips, and action taken.
Scenario — Think It Through

The Recurring Trip Inside the Tank

A crew member is using an angle grinder inside a tank. The cord-type GFCI trips. He presses RESET, plugs back in, and the GFCI trips again within 30 seconds. He tells the foreman the GFCI must be “weak” and asks to plug directly into the generator without it to finish faster.

Correct response: Deny the request. Two trips in quick succession means a ground fault is present — either the tool is damaged or the cord is compromised. Inside a metal tank with potential for moisture and full-body conductive contact, bypassing GFCI protection could be fatal. Remove the grinder and cord from service, inspect both, and use a different tool with a verified working GFCI before resuming work.
