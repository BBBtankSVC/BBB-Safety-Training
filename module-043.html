Ammonia Awareness

Recognizing, avoiding, and responding to ammonia releases at petroleum facilities — a colorless, pungent gas that can incapacitate and kill at concentrations that accumulate rapidly in equipment rooms and low-lying areas.

01 Ammonia Properties & Where It Is Found

▼

Ammonia (NH&sub3;) is a colorless gas with a sharp, pungent odor detectable by most people at concentrations as low as 5 parts per million (ppm). It is one of the most widely produced industrial chemicals in the world and is present at many petroleum and petrochemical facilities in quantities sufficient to create life-threatening exposure if released. BBB Tank Services workers may encounter ammonia in refrigeration systems, water treatment operations, and certain processing facilities.

Lighter Than Air — But Not Always Safe Up High. Ammonia vapor (density 0.59) is lighter than air and tends to rise and disperse in open outdoor environments. However, in enclosed or semi-enclosed spaces — compressor rooms, refrigeration equipment rooms, trenches, and pits — ammonia can accumulate to dangerous concentrations even before it rises to exit points. Near the point of release, concentrations can reach lethal levels regardless of the gas's overall tendency to rise. Never assume an area is safe simply because the leak is at a lower level.

Where Ammonia Is Found at Petroleum & Industrial Facilities

☃ Refrigeration Systems

The most common source. Large industrial refrigeration systems using anhydrous ammonia as refrigerant are found at LNG/LPG terminals, natural gas processing plants, and some refineries for process cooling. Often PSM-covered (TQ 10,000 lbs).

💧 Water Treatment

Ammonia or ammonium hydroxide (aqueous ammonia) used in cooling tower biocide programs, boiler water treatment, and industrial wastewater pH adjustment at refineries and petrochemical plants.

⚙ Gas Processing

Nitrogen compounds in crude oil and natural gas feedstocks can produce ammonia as a process byproduct. Found in amine treating units, hydrotreaters, and sour water strippers at refineries.

🔧 Chemical Storage

Concentrated ammonium hydroxide solution (aqua ammonia) stored in drums or totes for chemical injection. Tank pressures can build if containers are exposed to heat, leading to ruptured seals or relief valve venting.

🏭 Fertilizer Facilities

Anhydrous ammonia storage tanks and distribution systems at agricultural or fertilizer facilities adjacent to petroleum terminals. Catastrophic tank failures have killed dozens of workers and bystanders at such facilities.

🔤 Emissions & Vents

Low-level ammonia emissions from sour water strippers, amine units, and some tanks can create localized concentrations in prevailing wind shadow areas or in equipment pits below grade level at the work area.

02 Health Effects & Exposure Limits

▼

Ammonia is a severe irritant to the eyes, nose, throat, and respiratory tract. Its corrosive alkaline nature causes chemical burns to moist tissue on contact. The progression of health effects with increasing concentration is rapid — the difference between uncomfortable irritation and life-threatening injury can occur across a concentration change of just a few hundred ppm that develops in seconds during a catastrophic release.

⚠ Olfactory Fatigue — Why “I Can’t Smell It Anymore” Is the Most Dangerous Sign. Ammonia initially announces itself with a powerful, unmistakable odor. But above approximately 100 ppm, the olfactory nerves become fatigued and the perceived odor diminishes even as the actual concentration continues to rise. A worker who enters an ammonia-contaminated space, notices the strong smell briefly, and then thinks “it doesn’t smell as bad now” is potentially at IDLH or above. Never rely on odor as your primary ammonia detection method at any concentration that has caused fatigue.

Specific Health Effects

Eyes (Most Sensitive Organ)

  • Immediate tearing and redness at low concentrations
  • Severe chemical burns at moderate concentrations
  • Permanent vision damage or blindness possible from liquid ammonia contact or high vapor concentrations
  • Aqueous ammonia splash is an ocular emergency — irrigate with water immediately for a minimum of 15–20 minutes

Respiratory Tract

  • Upper airway: burning, coughing, choking at moderate concentrations
  • Laryngospasm (throat spasm closing the airway) at high concentrations
  • Pulmonary edema (fluid in the lungs) from severe exposure — may be delayed 24–48 hours after the exposure event
  • Symptoms that resolve after leaving the exposure area can return hours later as pulmonary edema develops

Recognizing Ammonia Hazard Indicators on a Job Site

Before and during work at any facility that may have ammonia, be alert to:

Visual Indicators

  • NFPA 704 diamond with white “SA” (simple asphyxiant) or blue health hazard rating of 3 on ammonia storage or piping
  • Yellow or white-painted refrigerant piping (ammonia refrigerant lines are often color-coded yellow)
  • Frost or ice formation on pipes or fittings (indicates a liquid ammonia line; also a sign of a potential leak point)
  • White mist or visible cloud near equipment (liquid ammonia flashing to vapor in humid air)
  • Corrosion staining near pipe fittings, valve stems, or relief valve discharge points

Sensory Indicators

  • The sharp, pungent “cleaning products” smell at any detectable level in a work area warrants investigation and monitoring
  • Eye irritation or tearing without obvious cause — ammonia may be present at low concentration
  • Throat or nasal irritation when moving through an area
  • A previously strong ammonia smell that suddenly seems to fade may indicate olfactory fatigue at increasing concentration — this is dangerous, not reassuring

04 Detection & Monitoring

▼

Ammonia Detection Methods

Personal Multi-Gas Monitors

  • Standard 4-gas monitors (LEL, O&sub2;, CO, H&sub2;S) do NOT detect ammonia — a separate ammonia-specific sensor or 5-gas monitor with an NH&sub3; sensor is required
  • At facilities known to have ammonia systems, BBB Tank Services requires a calibrated ammonia sensor on or near the work area
  • Confirm the sensor has been calibrated for ammonia — cross-sensitivity to other gases can produce false readings; check the sensor's cross-interference chart
  • Set the alarm at 25 ppm (NIOSH STEL) as the first alarm and 50 ppm (OSHA PEL) as the evacuation alarm

Fixed Detection Systems

  • PSM-covered ammonia facilities are required to have fixed ammonia detection systems in refrigeration equipment rooms and other areas of high ammonia inventory
  • Fixed systems alarm at multiple levels — confirm alarm setpoints and response procedures during site orientation
  • If a fixed ammonia alarm activates, treat it as a real release immediately — do not enter the alarmed area without appropriate respiratory protection
  • Know the location of the facility’s ammonia detector display panel so you can see current readings without entering a potentially contaminated area

Ammonia Detector Tube Testing

Colorimetric detector tubes (Draeger, Gastec) are a simple and reliable method for confirming ammonia presence and estimating concentration during routine assessment. Tubes are specific to ammonia (not cross-sensitive to H&sub2;S), require no electronics, and provide results in minutes. Limitations:

  • Single-use; results are a snapshot, not continuous monitoring
  • Accuracy is typically ±25% of reading — adequate for screening but not for precise exposure assessment
  • Temperature and humidity affect accuracy; check correction factors
  • Detector tubes require the worker to approach the sampling area — do not use in areas of suspected high concentration without respiratory protection

Where to Monitor at Facilities with Ammonia Systems

  • Before entering any enclosed space that may contain ammonia refrigerant or ammonia process equipment
  • Near relief valve discharge stacks — these open to atmosphere and can discharge during high-pressure events
  • Downwind of suspected leak sources — while ammonia is lighter than air, wind-driven ammonia vapor can accumulate at lower levels in the prevailing wind direction from an elevated leak
  • At the work boundary before entering any area flagged by the facility as containing ammonia systems
  • Continuously during work when adjacent ammonia systems are in service
⚠ Your 4-Gas Monitor Does Not Detect Ammonia. The standard personal multi-gas monitor issued to BBB Tank Services workers detects LEL, O&sub2;, CO, and H&sub2;S. It does not have an ammonia sensor. If you are working at a facility with ammonia refrigeration or ammonia water treatment systems and your monitor is not configured with an NH&sub3; sensor, you are working without ammonia detection. Confirm sensor configuration before entering any work area at an ammonia-handling facility.

05 Emergency Response to Ammonia Release

▼

Immediate Response — All Personnel

  1. 1
    Evacuate immediately and move upwind. Do not attempt to locate the source, assess the severity, or rescue others without SCBA. Move crosswind or upwind and to higher ground where possible. Ammonia is lighter than air but near the release point concentrations may be lethal at any elevation.
  2. 2
    Do not re-enter the area for any reason without SCBA. Air-purifying respirators with ammonia cartridges are inadequate for IDLH atmospheres or unknown concentrations. Only positive-pressure SCBA provides reliable protection for rescue or emergency response entry.
  3. 3
    Activate the facility emergency alarm. Pull the nearest manual alarm station or call the facility emergency number. Ammonia releases at PSM facilities require immediate activation of the facility’s emergency response plan.
  4. 4
    Call 911. A significant ammonia release is a hazmat emergency. Local emergency responders have specialized equipment and SCBA for ammonia releases. Provide the facility address, the nature of the release (ammonia), and the estimated number of people potentially exposed.
  5. 5
    Report to the assembly point and account for all crew. Follow the facility EAP (see Module 028). Report any crew member who is missing or symptomatic to the facility emergency coordinator immediately.
  6. 6
    Provide first aid to exposed workers while awaiting EMS. For anyone who was in the release area, immediately irrigate eyes and skin with large amounts of water. Remove contaminated clothing. Keep the exposed person calm and warm; monitor for delayed respiratory symptoms.

Shelter-in-Place vs. Evacuation for Ammonia Releases

Because ammonia is lighter than air, it tends to rise and disperse in open environments. The decision between evacuation and shelter-in-place depends on:

Evacuate When

  • The release is outdoors and you can move upwind and uphill away from the cloud
  • The facility emergency coordinator directs evacuation
  • You are in an enclosed space where ammonia is accumulating
  • Any personal monitor alarms for ammonia
  • You can smell ammonia and cannot immediately identify a route that takes you away from the source

Shelter-in-Place When

  • A large outdoor ammonia cloud covers the evacuation route and moving into it would increase exposure
  • The facility emergency coordinator specifically directs shelter-in-place
  • When sheltering, seal the building, shut off HVAC, and remain inside until the all-clear is given
  • Ammonia can infiltrate a building over time — SIP is a temporary measure while awaiting emergency responders, not an indefinite solution
Scenario — Unexpected Release

The Refrigeration Room

A BBB crew is performing mechanical work on a pump in a compressor building adjacent to an ammonia refrigeration equipment room. The fixed ammonia alarm activates. One crew member notices the sharp ammonia smell and his eyes begin to water. Another crew member says “it’s probably just a small leak, let’s give it a minute.”

Correct response: Evacuate immediately — do not “give it a minute.” The time between detecting an ammonia release and reaching a dangerous concentration can be seconds in an enclosed space. All crew evacuates upwind. The worker who is already symptomatic (watering eyes) receives water irrigation to his eyes immediately upon exiting. 911 is called. No one re-enters without SCBA. The BBB supervisor accounts for all crew at the assembly point and reports to the facility emergency coordinator. Even the crew member who had no immediate symptoms is evaluated by EMS, because delayed pulmonary edema is a documented risk from ammonia exposure even at concentrations that initially cause only mild irritation.

Skin & Eye Protection

  • Eye protection: Chemical splash goggles with indirect vent whenever working with aqueous ammonia or in areas where liquid ammonia spills are possible. Safety glasses alone are inadequate — ammonia vapor irritates eyes even from airborne exposure, and splash from liquid sources causes severe injury.
  • Skin protection: Nitrile or neoprene chemical-resistant gloves; chemical-resistant apron for liquid ammonia handling. Aqueous ammonia is caustic and causes chemical burns on skin contact.
  • Body protection: FRC with long sleeves and full coverage; chemical splash suit (Tyvek C or higher) for work involving potential liquid ammonia contact or known release scenarios.
  • No cotton for liquid ammonia contact: Liquid anhydrous ammonia at -28°F causes severe cryogenic burns on contact. Any clothing saturated with liquid anhydrous ammonia can cause massive freeze burns. Remove contaminated clothing immediately.

First Aid for Ammonia Exposure

Eye Exposure

  • Irrigate immediately with large amounts of tepid water for a minimum of 15–20 minutes
  • Remove contact lenses if present and easily removed without additional trauma
  • Hold eyelids open during irrigation — the natural instinct to close them traps ammonia against the cornea
  • Seek emergency medical evaluation immediately — even mild-appearing eye exposure warrants ophthalmology assessment

Skin Exposure

  • Remove contaminated clothing immediately while flushing with water
  • Flush affected skin with large amounts of water for at least 15 minutes
  • Do not apply creams, oils, or chemical neutralizers
  • For liquid anhydrous ammonia contact, treat as both a chemical burn AND a cryogenic injury — seek emergency medical care

Inhalation Exposure

  • Move to fresh air immediately; keep the person calm and at rest
  • If breathing is difficult, administer oxygen if trained and available
  • Seek emergency medical evaluation for all inhalation exposures, even if symptoms seem mild
  • Delayed pulmonary edema can develop 4–48 hours after exposure — any worker who inhaled ammonia must be monitored by medical personnel, not cleared at the scene
