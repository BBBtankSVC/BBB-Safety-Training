Fire Watch & Fire Protection Training

📅 Rev. 10/31/2025 ⚖ OSHA 29 CFR 1910 Subpart L · 1926 Subpart F ⏱ ~12 min read 📝 10-question quiz
📋1–3. Purpose, Scope & Training Requirements

This module establishes methods and guidelines for training personnel in fire watch and fire protection. It applies to all employees and subcontractors at BBB Tank Services-controlled job sites, and is specifically intended for personnel assigned as Fire Watch during burning or welding operations.

  • Fire Watches are trained at the worksite by the Site Supervisor.
  • Training must be documented and employee training files updated.
  • Training is conducted when employees are initially hired and annually thereafter.
Root Cause of Fires Fires do not just happen — they are caused by carelessness in operating equipment, handling hazardous materials, or personal habits like smoking. Individual employees can protect themselves and their coworkers by learning how to prevent fires and staying alert to hazards before they become emergencies.
🔥4. Four Classes of Fire — Know Each One

All employees must know the four classes of fire, their burning characteristics, and the correct extinguishing agent for each. Using the wrong agent can make things worse.

A
Ordinary Combustibles
Wood, paper, cloth, rubber, plastic
Use: Water ✓
B
Flammable Liquids & Gases
Oils, gasoline, grease, paint
Use: CO₂ or Dry Chemical ✓
C
Electrical Equipment
Energized wiring, panels, motors
Use: CO₂ or Dry Chemical ✓
NEVER Water ✗
D
Combustible Metals
Magnesium, titanium, potassium
Use: Special Approved Agent Only ✓
Class C — Never Use Water Water must NEVER be used on fires involving energized electrical equipment. Water conducts electricity and will cause electrical shock AND spread the fire.

All fires require four components: Ignition source + Heat + Fuel + Oxygen. Remove any one of these and the fire cannot sustain itself.

The #1 cause of workplace fires is electrical equipment — specifically damaged electrical cords, loose electrical connections, overloaded circuits, and defective power tools. Other common causes include welding and cutting operations, chemical reactions, and heaters.

🛡4. General Fire Prevention Rules

Three main principles of fire prevention:

  • Be alert to trouble before a fire starts.
  • Eliminate unsafe habits that can lead to fires.
  • Conduct a fire prevention investigation of your work area prior to work start to remove any potential fire hazards.
  • Never tamper with or move firefighting equipment except for actual firefighting use.
  • Report any equipment defects to your supervisor immediately.
  • Know the location and proper operation of all fire protection equipment in your work area.
  • Used rags must be kept in metal or metal-lined containers with metal covers.
  • Never use flammable liquids for cleaning purposes.
  • Before using solvents, discuss needed precautions with your supervisor.
  • Smoking is only allowed in designated smoking areas. Discard butts in approved containers — never on the floor or in trash cans.
  • All chemicals and solvents must be in properly labeled and approved containers.
If Your Clothing Catches Fire DROP to the ground and ROLL to create a smothering effect, or use a fire blanket or other means if available. This is the standard "Stop, Drop, and Roll" response.

Know your primary and secondary exit routes from your work area at all times. When an alarm sounds, evacuate immediately. Know site-specific codes for emergency pages before work begins.

🧯5. Fire Extinguisher Rules & When NOT to Fight
⚠ Do NOT Attempt to Fight a Fire If:
  • You do not know what is burning.
  • The fire is spreading rapidly out of control.
  • The fire is between you and your exit or escape path.
  • You do not have adequate equipment.
  • You might inhale toxic smoke.
Only trained and qualified personnel are permitted to fight fires. Your training covers only small smolders and fires that can be put out with a portable extinguisher.

General extinguisher rules:

  • A fire extinguisher must be within 20–30 feet of any flame or ignition-type operation in progress.
  • Know where the nearest extinguisher is, what type of fire it should be used on, and how to operate it — before a fire occurs.
  • All fires, whether ignitions or smolders, must be reported to the Site Supervisor so an investigation can be initiated to determine root cause.
  • Any extinguisher that has been used or is not fully charged must be returned to the Site Supervisor for replacement immediately.
  • Keep work areas clean and orderly — preventing small fires from becoming major disasters.
  • Equipment must never be refueled while running or when hot.
👁6. Fire Watch for Welding & Cutting Operations
35 ft
Welding sparks can travel as far as 35 feet. Fire watch personnel must account for this full radius when checking the area.

Safe procedures prior to and during welding operations:

  • Ensure the area has been checked by an authorized person with a calibrated meter for flammable gases and vapors before work begins.
  • Remove all combustibles such as paper and rags from the area.
  • Have a fire extinguisher and misting hose (if required) on hand and ready before work starts.
  • Ensure proper PPE is on hand and being worn.
  • If the piece to be welded or cut cannot be moved to a fire-hazard-free area, remove the hazards first. If hazards cannot be removed, cover with fire-retardant material or use guarding to confine heat, sparks, and slag.
30 min
Fire watch must remain on site a minimum of 30 minutes after grinding and welding operations are complete — to ensure no smoldering or fires break out.
🎯7. How to Use a Fire Extinguisher — The PASS Method

Fire extinguishers are for incipient stage fires only — the initial or beginning stage when a fire can be controlled with a portable extinguisher. If the fire has grown beyond that stage, do not fight it.

8–12 ft
Stand 8 to 12 feet away from the fire when using an extinguisher — far enough that sparks, embers, and burning residue won't blow back at you from the pressure stream.

Remember the word PASS:

P
Pull the Pin
This allows you to discharge the extinguisher. Without pulling the pin, the handle will not work.
A
Aim at the Base
Aim the nozzle at the BASE of the fire — not the flames. To extinguish, you must attack the fuel source, not the visible flames above it.
S
Squeeze the Handle
Squeeze the top handle or lever to release the pressurized extinguishing agent.
S
Sweep Side to Side
Sweep from side to side — NOT up and down — until the fire is completely out. Then move away and ensure the fire does not re-ignite.
First Rule — Don't Panic Keep your calm and wits about you. Do not let an adrenaline rush cause you to lose control of the situation. A calm, practiced approach is far more effective.
