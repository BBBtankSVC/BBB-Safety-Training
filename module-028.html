Emergency Action Plan

What every BBB Tank Services employee must know before an emergency happens — alarms, evacuation, shelter-in-place, headcounts, and site-specific response procedures at petroleum tank facilities.

01 What Is an EAP & Why Every Worker Must Know It

▼

An Emergency Action Plan (EAP) is a written document that outlines the procedures employees must follow in the event of an emergency at a workplace. OSHA requires a written EAP for most workplaces under 29 CFR 1910.38 (General Industry) and 29 CFR 1926.35 (Construction). At BBB Tank Services, the EAP is not a document kept in a binder — it is a set of actions every employee must know by heart and be able to execute without hesitation.

The fundamental rule of emergency response is: you cannot learn what to do during an emergency. Workers who do not know the alarm signals, assembly points, and reporting procedures before an emergency occurs will lose critical seconds — and seconds matter in petroleum tank emergencies where fires spread rapidly, toxic vapors accumulate, and explosions can occur without warning.

What the EAP Must Cover (OSHA 1910.38)

Required EAP Elements

  • Procedures for reporting a fire or other emergency
  • Emergency escape routes and procedures
  • Procedures for workers who remain to operate critical equipment before evacuating
  • Procedures for accounting for all workers after evacuation
  • Rescue and medical duties for any workers designated to perform them
  • Names and contact information of persons to contact for more information

Site Orientation Requirement

  • OSHA requires workers to be briefed on the EAP before being assigned to a job
  • At every customer site, the facility’s own EAP must be reviewed during site orientation (see Module 023 — PSM)
  • The BBB supervisor is responsible for ensuring every crew member receives site EAP orientation before work begins
  • Workers must know the specific assembly point, alarm signals, and emergency contacts for every site they work at
⚠ The Three Things You Must Know Before Work Starts on Any Site:
  1. What does the emergency alarm sound like, and what does it mean?
  2. Where is my primary and secondary assembly point?
  3. Who is the site emergency coordinator and how do I reach them?
If you cannot answer all three, ask your supervisor before the work day begins.

02 Types of Emergencies & Alarm Signals

▼

BBB Tank Services employees may encounter several distinct types of emergencies at petroleum storage tank facilities. Each type requires a specific initial response. Knowing which emergency is occurring and what action it demands is the first decision you must make when an alarm sounds.

🔥 Fire or Explosion

Continuous siren or horn; sometimes visual strobe. Evacuate immediately via designated route upwind. Do not attempt to fight a tank fire — evacuate the entire dike area. Call 911.

💨 H&sub2;S / Toxic Gas Release

Intermittent horn or specific H&sub2;S alarm tone; sometimes PA announcement. Evacuate crosswind or upwind to designated assembly point. Do not re-enter without supplied-air respirator. Call site emergency coordinator.

🚑 Medical Emergency

Radio call “Medical emergency at [location]” or verbal alert. Do not move the victim unless in immediate danger. Designate someone to call 911 and someone to meet EMS at the gate. Keep the area clear.

💧 Chemical or Petroleum Spill

PA announcement or radio call. Stop ignition sources immediately. Evacuate downwind workers. Notify the facility coordinator. Do not attempt cleanup without proper PPE and authorization.

⛈ Severe Weather

Tornado siren or weather alert radio. Move to the designated shelter (not a trailer or vehicle). Avoid elevated structures such as tank roofs or scaffolding during lightning and high-wind events.

🏠 Shelter-in-Place

Specific SIP alarm tone or PA announcement: “Shelter in place.” Move to the designated solid building. Close all doors, windows, and HVAC. Do not evacuate outdoors during a toxic vapor release from an adjacent unit.

Understanding Your Site’s Alarm System

No two petroleum facilities use identical alarm systems. During site orientation, confirm the following for every job site:

Alarm TypeWhat to Confirm at Orientation
Fire/General AlarmSignal type (continuous horn, siren, PA), duration, and whether different signals mean different emergencies
H&sub2;S AlarmThreshold that triggers site-wide alarm (often 10–15 ppm), alarm tone, and whether evacuation or shelter-in-place applies
All-Clear SignalWhat signal or announcement confirms it is safe to return; never assume all-clear without hearing the designated signal
Drill vs. Real AlarmHow the facility communicates when an alarm is a drill; if uncertain, always respond as if the alarm is real
When in Doubt, Treat Every Alarm as Real. The consequence of evacuating unnecessarily for a drill is minor inconvenience. The consequence of ignoring a real alarm is potentially fatal. If you hear an alarm and cannot immediately confirm it is a drill, evacuate.

03 Emergency Roles & Responsibilities

▼

Effective emergency response depends on everyone knowing their role before the emergency occurs. At a BBB Tank Services job site, emergency roles overlap with the host facility’s own emergency organization. Understanding who is responsible for what prevents the confusion and delay that cost lives during real incidents.

🚨 Facility Emergency Coordinator (Host)

  • The host employer’s designated person responsible for activating the facility EAP
  • Directs the overall emergency response including evacuation, shelter-in-place, and emergency services coordination
  • Has the authority to direct all personnel on site during an emergency, including contractors
  • BBB workers must follow instructions from the facility emergency coordinator during all emergencies on customer sites

👷 BBB Supervisor / Crew Lead

  • Responsible for accounting for all BBB Tank Services personnel after evacuation
  • Reports BBB headcount to the facility emergency coordinator at the assembly point
  • Has the authority and responsibility to order immediate evacuation of BBB crew without waiting for facility instruction when imminent danger exists
  • Maintains a current roster of all BBB and subcontract workers on the job site each day

👓 BBB Worker (All Personnel)

  • Know the emergency signals, assembly point, and EAP before work begins
  • Evacuate or shelter-in-place immediately when an alarm sounds; do not wait for a supervisor’s order
  • Report to the designated assembly point and remain there until accounted for
  • Immediately inform the BBB supervisor if any BBB worker is missing, injured, or did not make it to the assembly point
  • Never re-enter an evacuated area without explicit authorization from the facility emergency coordinator

📞 Emergency Services (911)

  • Call 911 for any fire, explosion, serious injury, or toxic release where professional emergency response is needed
  • Provide: facility address and gate number, nature of emergency, number of people potentially involved, and your callback number
  • Designate one person to meet emergency services at the facility entrance and guide them to the incident location
  • Never assume the facility has already called 911 — call directly if in doubt

Critical Operations Personnel

Some petroleum facilities require specific workers to remain at designated posts to perform shutdown or isolation tasks before evacuating. This is rare for BBB Tank Services contract crews — our personnel are almost always in the “evacuate immediately” category. However, if a BBB supervisor is specifically designated by the facility as a critical operations person for a given task, they must:

  • Know exactly which actions to perform and in what sequence
  • Have a defined time limit for completing the actions before self-evacuating regardless of completion
  • Have a buddy who monitors their status during the critical operations period
  • Never exceed the defined time limit — no equipment is worth a life

04 Evacuation Procedures & Assembly Points

▼

The Evacuation Sequence

1
Stop Work & Secure Immediately

Stop all work the instant an alarm sounds. If safe to do so in 5 seconds or less: close any open hatches, shut off ignition sources, drop tools. Do not spend more than 5 seconds securing anything — your life is worth more than any task state.

2
Alert All Nearby Workers

As you leave, verbally alert any coworkers near you who may not have heard the alarm. Do not assume everyone has heard — tanks, equipment noise, and hearing protection can mask alarm signals.

3
Use the Designated Escape Route

Follow the pre-identified primary escape route. If blocked (fire, spill, collapse), use the alternate route. Do not take shortcuts through areas you haven’t been trained on. In petroleum tank emergencies, moving upwind is the priority — vapors and toxic gases are heavier than air and travel downwind.

4
Do Not Use Elevators, Go Downwind, or Re-Enter

Never re-enter a building or area that has been evacuated. Never drive a vehicle through an active fire or vapor release area. If you become trapped, shelter in place and communicate your location.

5
Report to Assembly Point & Stay

Go directly to the designated assembly point. Report to the BBB supervisor. Stay at the assembly point until the all-clear is given by the facility emergency coordinator — not by a rumor, not by a coworker, only by the official all-clear signal or announcement.

6
Headcount & Missing Person Report

The BBB supervisor accounts for all BBB personnel by name. If anyone is unaccounted for, report immediately to the facility emergency coordinator — do not search for a missing worker yourself. Emergency responders will conduct the search.

Assembly Points at Petroleum Tank Sites

Assembly points must be established and confirmed before work begins. Key rules:

  • Upwind location: Assembly points at petroleum facilities must be upwind of the tank farm. Since wind direction changes, know where “upwind” is from the specific facility’s primary prevailing wind direction, and be prepared to relocate the assembly point if wind shifts during an emergency.
  • Distance: A minimum of 500 feet from the tank farm boundary is typical for petroleum facilities. Some facilities specify distances of 1,000 feet or more based on their hazard analysis. Follow the facility’s specified distance.
  • Primary and alternate: Always know both the primary and the alternate assembly point. If the primary route is blocked, you must go to the alternate without hesitation.
  • No vehicles: Never drive to the assembly point during an active emergency — vehicle traffic interferes with emergency responders and creates additional ignition risk in a flammable vapor environment.
Scenario — Missing Worker

The Headcount Problem

A fire alarm sounds at a refinery tank farm. A BBB crew of five evacuates to the assembly point. The supervisor counts four workers. One crew member — a welder — is unaccounted for. Another crew member says “he went to get tools from the truck, he’ll be here.” The supervisor is tempted to wait a few minutes before reporting.

Correct response: Report the missing worker to the facility emergency coordinator immediately. Do not wait, do not assume, and do not send another crew member to look for him. Emergency responders will locate him and have the equipment and training to do so safely. Every minute of delay in reporting a missing worker potentially delays a rescue. He may be injured, trapped, or overwhelmed by smoke — or he may simply be at the truck and will check in shortly. Either way, report first and let the emergency coordinator make the determination.

05 Shelter-in-Place & Specific Emergency Responses

▼

Shelter-in-Place — When and Why

Shelter-in-Place (SIP) is the opposite of evacuation: instead of moving away from the building, you move into a solid building and seal it to protect against an outdoor hazard. SIP is ordered when evacuating outdoors would expose workers to greater risk than staying inside — specifically when a toxic gas cloud (H&sub2;S, ammonia, chlorine) is moving across the facility in a way that would expose evacuees.

When Shelter-in-Place Is Ordered

  • Toxic gas release from an adjacent process unit where outdoor evacuation would route workers through the gas cloud
  • Large-scale H&sub2;S release where the gas cloud covers the entire site perimeter
  • Tornado or severe weather warning where outdoor evacuation creates tornado risk
  • The facility emergency coordinator will specify when SIP applies — follow their instruction

How to Shelter-in-Place

  • Move to the designated SIP building immediately — solid construction, no open process areas
  • Close all doors, windows, and exterior openings
  • Shut off HVAC, fans, and any air intake systems
  • Seal gaps under doors with towels, tape, or clothing if available
  • Stay away from windows; move to interior rooms if possible
  • Do not leave until the all-clear is announced

Medical Emergency Response

  1. 1
    Ensure scene safety first. Before approaching a downed worker, confirm the hazard that caused the injury is not still active (vapor release, energized equipment, falling hazard). Never become a second victim by entering an unsafe scene without appropriate protection.
  2. 2
    Call for help immediately. Have someone call 911 AND the facility emergency number simultaneously. Do not delay calling 911 to notify the facility first — call both.
  3. 3
    Do not move the victim unless they are in immediate physical danger (fire, toxic atmosphere). Moving an injured worker can worsen spinal injuries. Stabilize in place and wait for EMS unless the hazard demands immediate removal.
  4. 4
    Provide basic care within your training. Workers with First Aid / CPR certification (Module 011) should provide care within their level of training. Workers without certification should keep the victim calm, warm, and still, and manage bystanders.
  5. 5
    Meet EMS at the gate. Designate one person whose only job is to meet emergency services at the facility entrance and guide them directly to the victim. This single action can save critical minutes.

Severe Weather Response at Tank Sites

  • Tornado warning: Move immediately to the designated tornado shelter — a solid masonry or concrete building, never a trailer, portable office, or vehicle. Tank roofs and scaffolding must be immediately cleared when a tornado warning is issued.
  • Lightning: Suspend all work on tank roofs, scaffolding, and elevated surfaces when lightning is within 10 miles. Workers on metal structures or in open areas are at high risk. Move to a solid building — not under trees or near isolated tall structures.
  • High winds: Scaffold work and elevated operations should be suspended when sustained winds exceed 25 mph or gusts exceed 35 mph. Loose materials on scaffolding become projectiles. Secure all equipment and descend before conditions deteriorate.

06 Reporting Emergencies & Post-Emergency Procedures

▼

How to Report an Emergency

Effective emergency reporting is fast, specific, and calm. A panicked or vague report delays emergency response. Use this information structure every time:

Emergency Report Structure — Memorize This:
  1. Who you are: “This is [name] with BBB Tank Services”
  2. Where you are: Specific facility name, address, tank number, or GPS coordinates
  3. What happened: Fire / explosion / H&sub2;S release / injury / spill — be specific
  4. How many people involved: Number of injured, missing, or potentially exposed
  5. Your callback number: Stay on the line or be reachable at this number

Post-Emergency Requirements

After any emergency event — even one that did not result in injury — the following must occur before BBB Tank Services work resumes:

  • Scene clearance by facility: Only the facility emergency coordinator or authority having jurisdiction (AHJ) can authorize re-entry into an evacuated area. BBB supervisors may not make this determination independently.
  • Incident report: Every emergency that involved BBB personnel, equipment, or property must be reported to BBB Tank Services management within the same shift. A written incident report must follow within 24 hours.
  • Near-miss reporting: An emergency that was averted — a fire that was stopped before spreading, a worker who was rescued from a toxic atmosphere — is a near-miss. Near-misses must be reported with the same urgency as actual incidents. The near-miss that goes unreported becomes the next fatality.
  • Equipment inspection: Any BBB equipment that was exposed to fire, chemical release, or flooding must be inspected before being returned to service. Scaffolding exposed to a structural event must be re-inspected by the competent person before re-use.
  • Stop Work review: Following any significant emergency, the BBB supervisor must conduct a crew debrief to identify what happened, what the crew did well, and what needs to change before work continues.
