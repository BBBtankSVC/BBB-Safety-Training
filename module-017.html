In-Service Tank Work

Recognizing and controlling the unique hazards of working on tanks that still contain product — from gauging and sampling to roof maintenance and external inspection.

⏱ Est. 40 min 📋 6 Sections ✅ 10-Question Quiz 📅 Rev. Oct 2025

  • Identify the primary hazards present at an in-service storage tank
  • Describe the vapor zone classifications around a tank in service

  • Explain grounding and bonding requirements to prevent static ignition

  • Describe floating roof hazards specific to in-service operations

01 What Is In-Service Tank Work?

▼

An in-service tank is any storage tank that contains product — petroleum, chemicals, or other hazardous materials — and has not been fully emptied, isolated, cleaned, and gas-freed. In-service work means any task performed on or around a tank while it is in this condition, including tanks that have been partially emptied but still hold liquid or vapors.

In-service work is fundamentally different from working on an out-of-service, cleaned, and gas-freed tank. The tank is still an active hazardous system. Product movement, vapor generation, and the potential for sudden release are all ongoing. This requires a disciplined, pre-planned approach every time.

Common In-Service Tank Tasks at BBB Tank Services

Inspection & Measurement

  • Manual gauging (innage/ullage)
  • Automatic gauge maintenance
  • Sampling for product quality
  • External visual inspection
  • Shell thickness testing (UT)
  • Leak detection surveys

Roof & Vent Maintenance

  • Thief hatch inspection and gasket replacement
  • Pressure/vacuum vent inspection and cleaning
  • Floating roof seal inspection
  • Roof drain maintenance
  • Geodesic and fixed-roof vent work
  • Level instrument maintenance

External Maintenance

  • Shell painting and coating
  • Insulation installation/repair
  • Stairway and handrail maintenance
  • Cathodic protection testing
  • Nozzle and valve maintenance (isolated)
  • Secondary containment inspection
⚠ The Defining Hazard: Flammable Vapor. Petroleum storage tanks continuously generate flammable vapors from the liquid surface. These vapors are heavier than air and accumulate at the tank roof, around open hatches, in the vapor space, and anywhere vapor can escape. A single ignition source — a spark, a hot surface, static discharge, or a non-intrinsically-safe device — can ignite these vapors with explosive force. This hazard is present at every in-service petroleum tank, every time.

02 Hazard Identification & Vapor Zones

▼

Primary Hazards of In-Service Tank Work

Fire & Explosion Hazards

  • Flammable vapors from product surface and breathing losses
  • Vapor accumulation at roof openings (hatches, vents, seals)
  • Static electricity discharge during gauging and sampling
  • Non-intrinsically-safe equipment introduced near open hatches
  • Sparks from tools, vehicles, or falling objects striking steel
  • Heated surfaces (catalytic converters, exhaust pipes) near vapor zones

Chemical & Physical Hazards

  • H&sub2;S and benzene vapors at tank openings
  • Falls from tank roofs and stairways
  • Roof collapse — especially corroded fixed roofs or pontoon-style floating roofs
  • Product overflow if tank is filled above working capacity
  • Extreme shell temperatures from solar heating (Texas summers: tank shells can exceed 150°F)
  • Pressurized tanks — unexpected release when opening fittings

Vapor Zone Classification

The area around an in-service petroleum storage tank is divided into classified zones based on the likelihood of flammable vapor being present. Understanding these zones tells you what equipment is allowed and what controls are required in each area.

Flammable Vapor Zone Classification — In-Service Petroleum Tank

Zone 0 — Vapor Space Interior (Continuous Hazard) Inside the tank above the liquid surface. Flammable mixture present continuously. No entry without full gas-free certification, confined space permit, or supplied-air protection. Absolutely no ignition sources.
Zone 1 — Immediate Roof Area / Open Hatches (Intermittent Hazard) Within 5 feet of any tank opening (hatch, vent, seal gap, gauge pole) when open. Flammable vapors present intermittently during normal operation. Intrinsically safe equipment only. No open flames, non-IS radios, non-IS flashlights, or spark-producing tools.
Zone 2 — General Roof & Diked Area (Low-Probability Hazard) The tank roof (with all openings closed), the area within the dike wall, and within 10 feet of the tank shell. Flammable vapor unlikely under normal conditions but possible during abnormal events. Non-sparking tools preferred; no open flames without a hot-work permit.
Unclassified — Beyond the Dike Wall (General Area) Beyond 10 feet from the tank shell and outside the dike area under normal conditions. Standard industrial equipment permitted. No vehicle parking facing toward the tank; exhaust directed away from the dike.
⚠ Wind Direction Matters. Vapors released from open hatches or vents travel downwind. Always position yourself and your crew upwind of any open tank opening. Wind direction can shift — monitor throughout the job and relocate personnel if the wind shifts to blow vapors toward the work area.

03 Ignition Source Control & Static Electricity

▼

Controlling ignition sources is the single most critical safety requirement for in-service tank work. No amount of atmospheric monitoring replaces the discipline of eliminating every possible ignition source before opening any tank fitting.

The Fire Triangle at an In-Service Tank

Two elements of the fire triangle are always present at an in-service petroleum tank: fuel (flammable vapor) and oxygen (from air mixing with vapor at openings). The only element under your control is ignition. Every procedure in this section is designed to eliminate all potential ignition sources.

Prohibited Ignition Sources in Tank Work Zones

Always Prohibited in Zone 0 & Zone 1

  • Open flames of any kind (lighters, matches, torches)
  • Smoking — including e-cigarettes and vaping devices
  • Non-intrinsically-safe (non-IS) electrical equipment
  • Ordinary (non-IS) flashlights, phones, cameras, radios
  • Spark-producing hand tools (steel striking steel)
  • Any hot-work activity (welding, cutting, grinding)
  • Vehicles and internal combustion engines
  • Non-explosion-proof motors or pumps

Required Controls for Zone 2

  • No smoking within the dike area
  • No open flames without a hot-work permit
  • Non-sparking (brass, bronze, or spark-resistant alloy) tools preferred
  • Vehicle exhaust directed away from tank; catalytic converters not facing tank
  • Hot work permit required for any grinding or cutting
  • Fire watch posted during any grinding or cutting

Static Electricity — The Invisible Ignition Source

Static electricity is generated whenever dissimilar materials move against each other — a gauging tape moving through product, a sample thief being lowered, product flowing through a pipe, or even a worker moving across a plastic surface. At an open tank hatch with flammable vapor present, a static discharge of only 0.2 millijoules is enough to ignite many petroleum vapors.

Grounding & Bonding Requirements

  1. 1
    Tank grounding. The tank itself must be properly grounded to earth through its foundation grounding system. Verify the grounding system is intact before starting any in-service work. Do not assume it is functional — check for broken or corroded ground straps.
  2. 2
    Bond all equipment to the tank. Any metal equipment brought near the tank — vacuum trucks, pumps, portable containers — must be bonded to the tank with a bonding cable before any connections are made or any hatches are opened. Bond first, then open.
  3. 3
    Use conductive gauging and sampling equipment. Gauging tapes, bob weights, sample thieves, and gauging poles must be made of conductive material (brass, stainless steel, or conductive composite) and must be bonded to the tank before being lowered through any opening.
  4. 4
    Lower equipment slowly. The rate at which a gauging tape or sample thief is lowered affects static charge buildup. Lower slowly and steadily — never drop equipment into the tank or lower at speed. After contact with product, allow a 30-second relaxation time before retrieving.
  5. 5
    Avoid plastic and synthetic materials at open hatches. Nylon rope, plastic funnels, and synthetic fabrics can accumulate static charge. Use conductive alternatives near open tank fittings. Do not use plastic sample bottles at the hatch opening — lower to depth, fill, then retrieve.
⚠ The 30-Second Rule for Sampling. After a sample thief or gauging device contacts product, allow at least 30 seconds before retrieving it. This relaxation period allows static charge that built up during the lowering process to dissipate before the device is brought back through the vapor-rich zone at the hatch opening — the point of highest ignition risk.
Scenario — Static Ignition Near Miss

The Wrong Sampling Bottle

A crew member opens a gauging hatch on a crude oil tank to collect a sample. He reaches into his truck and grabs a clean plastic sample bottle, then lowers it through the hatch on a nylon rope to collect the sample. As he pulls it back up through the hatch opening, there is a flash at the hatch rim. No fire results, but everyone nearby felt the shock wave.

What went wrong: Two compounding static hazards — a plastic (non-conductive) sample bottle and a nylon (non-conductive) rope. Neither was bonded to the tank. As the rope and bottle moved through the vapor-rich hatch opening, static charge that accumulated during sampling discharged at the hatch rim, igniting vapor momentarily. The correct procedure: use a conductive sample thief on a conductive wire or cable, bonded to the tank before opening the hatch. Lower slowly, allow 30-second relaxation, retrieve slowly.

04 Gauging, Sampling & Atmospheric Monitoring

▼

Pre-Task Atmospheric Monitoring

Before opening any hatch, vent, or fitting on an in-service tank, atmospheric testing must be performed. Monitor at the work location — not just at ground level. Flammable vapors at a closed hatch may be far higher concentration than at grade.

ParameterInstrumentAcceptable Range for Work
% LEL (Flammable Vapor)Catalytic bead or infrared combustible gas detector<10% LEL in work area; <1% LEL at ignition sources
Oxygen (O&sub2;)Electrochemical O&sub2; sensor19.5% – 23.5%
H&sub2;SElectrochemical H&sub2;S sensor<10 ppm for unprotected work; <1 ppm at face level near open hatches
Benzene (if crude/gasoline)Photoionization detector (PID)<0.5 ppm (OSHA PEL); <0.1 ppm NIOSH REL target
Monitor at the Hatch Level, Not Ground Level. Test with the probe positioned at the hatch opening before opening it, and again immediately after opening. Concentrations directly at the opening can be orders of magnitude higher than at ground level, especially on calm, hot days when vapors stratify and do not disperse.

Manual Gauging Procedure

  1. 1
    Check the wind. Position yourself upwind of the hatch. Confirm the wind direction will carry any vapor releases away from you and your crew.
  2. 2
    Don required PPE. At minimum: hard hat, safety glasses, FRC (flame-resistant clothing), chemical-resistant gloves, and a 4-gas monitor clipped at the collar.
  3. 3
    Bond the gauging equipment to the tank. Clip the bonding cable to the gauging tape reel and attach to a grounded point on the tank before touching the hatch. Verify the bond is secure.
  4. 4
    Open the hatch slowly and stand to the side. Do not position your face directly over the opening. Open slowly, allowing vapor to dissipate before leaning over. If your monitor alarms — close the hatch immediately and move upwind.
  5. 5
    Lower the tape slowly and steadily. Use a slow, controlled motion. No dropping or free-spooling. Allow the tape to settle in product for 30 seconds before reading.
  6. 6
    Retrieve slowly. Pull the tape upward at a steady, controlled rate. Wipe clean as it passes the hatch rim to prevent dripping flammable product on the tank roof.
  7. 7
    Close and secure the hatch immediately after completing the gauge. Never leave a hatch open unattended. Record your gauge reading and note any observation about vapor release, foam, or unusual odor.

Product Sampling

All sampling from in-service tanks follows the same bonding, slow-lowering, and 30-second relaxation requirements as gauging. Additional considerations:

  • Use a weighted, conductive sample thief (bacon bomb, weighted bottle) bonded to the tank
  • Only open sample containers away from the hatch opening — move downwind to a safe area before unsealing sample bottles
  • Label samples immediately with tank ID, date, time, sample depth, and your name
  • Store samples in approved containers in a cool, ventilated area — not in a truck cab
  • If sampling a sour crude (H&sub2;S-containing) tank, SCBA must be within reach and H&sub2;S levels verified before opening the hatch

05 Floating Roof Tanks & Roof Work

▼

Floating roof tanks (external floating roof — EFR; and internal floating roof — IFR) present unique hazards not present on fixed-roof tanks. The floating roof rests on the product surface and rises and falls with the liquid level. Working on a floating roof means working directly above the product and its vapors.

Floating Roof Hazards

Structural Hazards

  • Roof sink risk — a compromised pontoon, leg failure, or drainage blockage can cause the roof to tilt or sink into the product
  • Accumulated rainwater on the roof adds weight and accelerates sinking if roof drains are blocked
  • Corrosion of roof deck plating can create weak spots that fail under foot traffic
  • Leg sockets corrode and can collapse without warning

Vapor & Chemical Hazards

  • The seal area between the roof rim and tank shell contains the highest vapor concentrations on an EFR tank
  • Seal gaps allow continuous vapor escape — assume Zone 1 conditions anywhere near the shell on a floating roof
  • Rim fires are a leading cause of tank fires; damaged or missing seals dramatically increase risk
  • H&sub2;S accumulates in the rim space on sour service tanks

Safe Practices for Floating Roof Work

  • Check roof condition before stepping onto it. From the top of the stairway, visually inspect the roof deck for pooled water, unusual tilt, visible corrosion damage, or sunken areas. Do not step onto a roof that shows any of these conditions without engineering approval.
  • Never work alone on a floating roof. At minimum, a qualified standby person must be stationed at the top of the access stairway with communication to the worker and the ability to call for help.
  • Know the roof level before ascending. Confirm the current product level and roof elevation. A roof that is too low (near the landing legs or anti-rotation guides) may be close to settling — work is restricted when the roof is in the critical zone.
  • Walk on designated walkway surfaces only. Walk on pontoons or structural members — never on the center deck plating of a single-deck roof, which is not designed to support body weight.
  • Only intrinsically-safe equipment on the roof. The entire floating roof surface is Zone 1 or Zone 2 depending on proximity to the rim. Use only IS-rated flashlights, monitors, and communication devices.
  • Wear FRC and a personal H&sub2;S and LEL monitor at all times on the roof. Keep your monitor alarm audible and unobstructed at all times.
  • Roof drain maintenance priority. Blocked roof drains are one of the leading causes of floating roof sinkings. Inspect and clear drains before any extended work period on the roof.

Rim Seal Inspection

Rim seal inspection is one of the most common floating roof maintenance tasks BBB Tank Services performs. The seal prevents product vapors from escaping the annular space between the roof rim and the tank shell.

⚠ Seal Work = Zone 1 Conditions. Any work within 5 feet of the tank shell on a floating roof — including seal inspection, adjustment, or replacement — must be treated as Zone 1. This means: no non-IS equipment, no spark-producing tools, no steel striking steel. Use bronze or brass tools only. Confirm LEL readings are below 10% before and continuously during seal work.
Scenario — Floating Roof Assessment

The Tilted Roof

A crew arrives to perform a routine seal inspection on a large crude oil EFR tank. Before descending the stairway to the roof, the crew leader looks down and notices the roof appears to be tilted approximately 5–6 inches lower on one side. There is also significant standing water visible near the shell on the low side.

Correct action: Do not step onto the roof. A tilted floating roof is a serious structural warning sign — it may indicate blocked roof drains, a compromised pontoon, or leg failure. Immediately notify the customer/facility representative and your BBB supervisor. Do not attempt to inspect the roof or clear the drains until engineering has assessed the roof condition and confirmed it is safe to enter. Document the observation with photos from the stairway and submit an incident report.

06 Pre-Job Planning, Permits & Emergency Procedures

▼

Pre-Job Planning Requirements

No in-service tank work may begin without completing a pre-job plan. At BBB Tank Services, the supervisor or crew leader is responsible for completing the following before any in-service tank work begins:

  1. 1
    Identify the product. Know exactly what is in the tank. Obtain the SDS. Understand the flash point, vapor pressure, H&sub2;S content, benzene content (for crude/gasoline), and any other relevant chemical properties.
  2. 2
    Obtain all required permits. Check which permits are required for the specific task (see permit table below). Permits must be signed before work begins — not during or after.
  3. 3
    Identify and communicate emergency information. Know the location of the nearest fire extinguisher, emergency shower, first aid kit, and muster point. Know the facility’s emergency contact number. Brief all crew members before starting.
  4. 4
    Establish communication. Ensure all crew members have working IS radios or a reliable communication method. Confirm check-in intervals if workers will be on the roof or near the tank.
  5. 5
    Verify equipment and PPE. Confirm all 4-gas monitors are bump-tested, bonding cables are available and in good condition, and all PPE including FRC is on and being worn correctly by every crew member before work begins.
  6. 6
    Conduct a tailgate safety meeting. Review the hazards, controls, permit conditions, emergency procedures, and muster point with all crew members. All crew sign the JSA/JHA before starting.

Permit Requirements by Task

📋 In-Service Tank Work — Permit Reference

  • Gauging & Sampling — No permit required, but JSA/JHA required; atmospheric monitoring required; bonding checklist completed
  • Roof Work (inspection, seal, vents) — Roof Entry JSA required; buddy/standby required; IS equipment verified; atmospheric monitoring continuous
  • External Maintenance (painting, insulation) — JSA required; hot-work permit required if any grinding or spark-producing work is performed
  • Nozzle/Valve Work — Requires facility isolation and depressurization confirmation; JSA; atmospheric monitoring at work point
  • Any Tank Interior Entry — Tank must be taken out of service, emptied, and gas-freed; Confined Space Entry Permit required (see Module 022)
  • Hot Work Within Dike Area — Hot Work Permit required; fire watch for 30 minutes post-work; LEL verified <1% at ignition source location (see Module 027)

Emergency Procedures — In-Service Tank Incidents

Vapor Release / Gas Alarm

  • Stop all work immediately
  • Close any open hatches or fittings if safe to do so without delay
  • Evacuate upwind to the muster point
  • Account for all personnel
  • Call facility emergency number and 911 if exposure has occurred
  • Do not re-enter until cleared by competent person with gas meter

Fire or Ignition at Tank

  • Activate facility fire alarm immediately
  • Evacuate all personnel from the dike area
  • Call 911 — do not attempt to fight a tank fire without trained HAZMAT/fire resources
  • Shut off vehicle engines and remove vehicles if safe to do so from outside the dike
  • Meet emergency responders at the facility gate with tank product information and SDS
  • Account for all personnel at the muster point
⚠ Never Fight a Tank Fire Yourself. A storage tank fire is a major industrial emergency requiring specialized foam and suppression equipment operated by trained fire professionals. A BBB Tank Services crew’s job in a tank fire is to evacuate safely, account for all personnel, call 911, and provide facility and product information to emergency responders. Do not attempt suppression beyond a very small incipient fire (e.g., a small drip fire at grade that can be knocked down with a portable extinguisher without approaching the tank).
