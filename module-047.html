01 What Is Hexavalent Chromium & How Is It Generated

▼

Chromium is a metallic element that exists in several different oxidation states. Most naturally occurring chromium is in its trivalent form (Cr III), which is relatively stable and considered non-hazardous at typical exposure levels. Hexavalent chromium (Cr VI or Cr&sup6;⊃⁺) is a highly reactive, strongly oxidizing form of chromium that is a confirmed human carcinogen. The “hexavalent” refers to the +6 oxidation state — what makes this form of chromium uniquely dangerous compared to trivalent chromium.

Cr VI is not mined or purchased as a raw material at BBB Tank Services job sites — it is generated as a byproduct of high-heat operations on chromium-containing metals. Every time a BBB welder or cutter applies a flame or arc to stainless steel or chrome alloy, Cr VI is formed in the fume. This makes Cr VI awareness a daily concern for any crew performing welding or cutting on stainless steel components at refineries and petrochemical facilities.

How Cr VI Is Generated at BBB Tank Services

The Welding Arc & Oxidation Process

  • Stainless steel contains 10.5–26% chromium by weight. When the welding arc or cutting flame heats this metal above its melting point, the chromium oxidizes in the presence of atmospheric oxygen.
  • At these temperatures, a fraction of the chromium forms hexavalent chromium compounds (primarily chromium trioxide, CrO&sub3;, and chromates) rather than the trivalent form.
  • These Cr VI compounds volatilize into the welding fume as submicron particles — small enough to penetrate deep into the lung and bypass the body’s upper respiratory filtration mechanisms.
  • The amount of Cr VI generated per unit of weld varies by process: FCAW and SMAW generate more fume than GTAW; cutting generates higher concentrations than welding.

Why Cr VI Particles Are Especially Hazardous

  • Submicron particle size: Cr VI particles in welding fume are typically 0.1–1.0 microns — far below the size that can be seen, tasted, or smelled. They are invisible and odorless.
  • Deep lung deposition: Particles this small are not filtered by the nose and upper airways; they deposit directly in the alveoli (gas-exchange surfaces) of the lungs where clearance is slow and absorption is high.
  • High solubility of some forms: Certain Cr VI compounds are soluble in the lung environment, allowing them to migrate to distant tissues and organs.
  • No exposure “threshold”: Like other carcinogens, no scientifically established concentration below which Cr VI causes no increased cancer risk.
⚠ Cr VI Is Classified as a Group 1 Human Carcinogen. The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), the National Toxicology Program (NTP), and OSHA all classify Cr VI as a confirmed human carcinogen. This means the scientific evidence that Cr VI causes cancer in humans — specifically lung cancer, and nasal and sinus cancers — is definitive. There is no “safe” exposure level. OSHA sets a PEL to define the maximum legally permissible exposure, not a safe one.

02 Health Effects & Exposure Limits

▼

Health Effects of Cr VI Exposure

🫌 Lung Cancer

The primary and most serious Cr VI health effect. Studies of chromate workers show elevated lung cancer rates of 2–30 times the general population depending on exposure level and duration. Latency period typically 15–30 years after first significant exposure. Indistinguishable from other causes of lung cancer at time of diagnosis.

💨 Nasal & Sinus Cancer

Cr VI is causally linked to cancers of the nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses. Nasal septum perforation (a hole in the cartilage dividing the nasal passages) occurs from chronic exposure — a painless but permanent injury that serves as a marker of significant past Cr VI exposure.

🫘 Skin Effects

Cr VI can penetrate intact skin. Repeated skin contact causes chrome ulcers — painless, slow-healing lesions called “chrome holes” that develop at cut or abraded skin. Chrome allergy (contact dermatitis) causes severe itching, redness, and blistering on subsequent exposures even at low concentrations.

👁 Eye Irritation & Damage

Cr VI in welding fume causes eye irritation, conjunctivitis, and tearing. Direct contact with Cr VI solutions (chromate paints, chromic acid) causes severe chemical burns to the eyes. Splash from chromic acid can cause permanent damage or blindness.

OSHA Exposure Limits for Hexavalent Chromium

OSHA published dedicated hexavalent chromium standards in 2006 — 29 CFR 1910.1026 (General Industry) and 29 CFR 1926.1126 (Construction) — dramatically reducing the PEL from the previous 100 μg/m³ to the current limit:

2.5
μg/m³ — Action Level (AL)
8-hr TWA. At or above this level: air monitoring, medical surveillance, and Cr VI training required. Triggers OSHA 1910.1026 requirements.
5
μg/m³ — OSHA PEL
8-hr TWA. Maximum legally permissible 8-hour average exposure. Above this level: immediate engineering controls, respiratory protection, and regulated area required.
0.2
μg/m³ — NIOSH REL
8-hr TWA. NIOSH’s more protective recommendation — 25× lower than the OSHA PEL — reflecting the view that even the PEL poses a significant residual cancer risk.
⚠ What 5 μg/m³ Means in Practice. Five micrograms per cubic meter is a vanishingly small quantity — 5 millionths of a gram in 1,000 liters of air. You cannot see it, smell it, or taste it. The only reliable way to know whether Cr VI is within acceptable limits during welding operations is air monitoring with proper analytical methods. Workers who judge Cr VI exposure by visible fume, their own perception, or general gut feel are guessing. Engineers confirm.

03 Operations That Generate Cr VI at BBB Tank Services

▼

BBB Tank Services personnel perform a range of tasks that may generate Cr VI, primarily centered on welding, cutting, and grinding operations on stainless steel or chrome-containing alloy components at refineries and petrochemical facilities. Understanding which specific operations generate the highest exposures allows the correct controls to be applied at the right level of protection.

🔥 Stainless Steel Arc Welding

Very High Cr VI

SMAW, GMAW (MIG), and FCAW on stainless steel generate the highest Cr VI concentrations of any common BBB task. FCAW generates more fume per unit of weld than GMAW; both exceed OSHA PEL without controls at typical welding amperages.

💧 Stainless Steel TIG Welding

High Cr VI

GTAW (TIG) generates less fume than SMAW or GMAW but still produces significant Cr VI in confined or semi-enclosed conditions. At lower fume volumes, the Cr VI percentage by mass is higher than in MIG fume.

⚡ Plasma Cutting Stainless

Very High Cr VI

Plasma cutting stainless steel generates extremely high Cr VI concentrations — potentially orders of magnitude above the PEL without LEV controls. The cutting process vaporizes large quantities of base metal rapidly.

🔨 Grinding Stainless Steel

High Cr VI

Grinding and disc-cutting stainless generates Cr VI particles from friction heating of the base metal. Smaller particle sizes from grinding may penetrate deeper into the lung than welding fume particles.

🆕 Disturbing Chrome-Containing Coatings

High Cr VI

Sanding, blasting, or burning through chromate-containing primers and anti-corrosion coatings on older tank structures generates Cr VI dust. Chromate pigments (zinc chromate, strontium chromate) in industrial primers are a direct Cr VI source.

⚙ Oxy-Fuel Cutting Stainless

Moderate Cr VI

Oxy-fuel cutting is less effective on stainless steel than plasma or laser cutting; the lower efficiency means less Cr VI fume per minute than plasma cutting, but exposures above the PEL are common without controls.

Scenario — Underestimating the Hazard

The “Quick Grind” on a Stainless Nozzle

A BBB crew is completing a nozzle repair on a stainless steel heat exchanger. The final step is a 10-minute grinding pass to dress the weld profile. The crew lead says “it’s just a quick grind, we don’t need LEV for that.” No local exhaust ventilation is set up. The grinder is wearing a P100 half-face respirator and FRC. The air concentration during that 10-minute grind is later measured at 35 μg/m³.

What was wrong: At 35 μg/m³, the Cr VI concentration was 7× the OSHA PEL during those 10 minutes. Even a short-duration, high-concentration exposure contributes to cumulative cancer risk. The 10-minute assessment would yield an 8-hr TWA contribution that likely exceeded the AL. LEV is required for stainless steel grinding and must be positioned within 6–12 inches of the grinding point before the operation begins — the duration of the task does not reduce the obligation to control the hazard.

04 Engineering Controls & Work Practices

▼

OSHA 1910.1026 requires engineering and work practice controls as the primary means of reducing Cr VI exposure — not relying on PPE as the first line of defense. Engineering controls must be implemented to the extent feasible before respirators are used to address the remaining exposure.

Eliminate
Redesign or Substitute the Material Specify lower-chromium alloys for repairs where stainless steel’s corrosion resistance is not strictly necessary. Use mechanical joining (flanging, bolting) instead of welding where feasible. Discuss material substitution with the engineering team during the planning phase.
Substitute
Change the Welding Process Switch from SMAW or FCAW (high fume generation) to GTAW (TIG welding, lower fume) where the application permits. Use automated or robotic welding systems for repetitive stainless welds when available. Lower-fume processes reduce total Cr VI generated even if LEV is still required.
Engineering
Local Exhaust Ventilation (LEV) — Required for All Cr VI Operations Position the LEV capture hood 6–12 inches from the arc or grinding point, directed to capture the fume plume before it reaches the worker’s breathing zone. Use backdraft hoods, side-draft tables, or flexible arm LEV systems. For confined or semi-enclosed work, combine LEV with forced-air supply ventilation. At minimum 10,000 CFM general ventilation if LEV is not achievable; LEV is always preferred.
Admin
Work Practice Controls Position the welder upwind or with LEV capturing the fume; never weld with the face downwind into the fume plume. Rotate workers to reduce individual exposure duration. Schedule Cr VI work in isolated areas to prevent exposing non-welding crew members. Wet methods or HEPA vacuum for all Cr VI waste and surface cleanup — no dry sweeping of Cr VI-contaminated areas.
PPE
Respiratory Protection — Required in Addition to Engineering Controls A P100 (HEPA) particulate filter is the minimum for Cr VI work with LEV in place. Full-face APR with OV+P100 when Cr VI work involves other chemical hazards. Supplied-air respirator when concentrations exceed 25× the PEL (125 μg/m³) or in oxygen-deficient conditions. Fit-tested, NIOSH-approved respirator required — no exceptions.

Housekeeping — A Critical Cr VI Control

OSHA 1910.1026 includes specific housekeeping requirements for Cr VI work that go beyond general workplace cleanliness. These requirements exist because Cr VI-contaminated surfaces and settled fume particles continue to be an exposure pathway long after the welding operation is complete:

  • HEPA vacuum all Cr VI-contaminated surfaces before any cleanup. Standard vacuums pass Cr VI particles through their exhaust — only HEPA-rated vacuums capture particles at the size of Cr VI welding fume.
  • Wet wiping of surfaces after HEPA vacuuming to remove residual Cr VI particles. Never dry sweep or use compressed air to blow down Cr VI-contaminated surfaces — this re-suspends settled Cr VI particles into the breathing zone.
  • Dispose of Cr VI-contaminated waste (wipes, filters, PPE) as hazardous waste per the facility’s waste management procedures. Cr VI is a regulated hazardous substance.
  • Label waste containers with the Cr VI hazard warning; bag and seal contaminated PPE before removal from the work area.

Personal Hygiene Requirements

  • Wash hands and face thoroughly before eating, drinking, smoking, or applying cosmetics in any area where Cr VI work is performed or has been performed. Cr VI on the hands is easily ingested during eating.
  • Change into clean clothing before leaving the job site if Cr VI-contaminated fume has settled on work clothing. Do not launder Cr VI-contaminated clothing at home — it may contaminate household laundry and family members.
  • Shower before leaving the site when Cr VI exposure has been significant and Cr VI contamination of clothing or skin is likely.
  • No food, drink, or tobacco in Cr VI work areas. Ingested Cr VI is absorbed through the gastrointestinal tract and increases systemic exposure.

P100 Only for Cr VI — Not N95, Not P95. OSHA’s Cr VI standard requires a P100 (HEPA) particulate filter rated at 99.97% efficiency for airborne particles. N95 and P95 filters are rated at 95% efficiency — insufficient for a confirmed carcinogen where every particle that reaches the lung adds to cumulative risk. When welding stainless steel, ensure your cartridges are the orange-coded P100 filters, not the standard N95 dust masks sometimes issued for general nuisance dust.

Additional PPE for Cr VI Work

Skin Protection

  • Gloves: Leather welding gloves for arc protection; nitrile or neoprene chemical gloves underneath or as outer layer when handling Cr VI-containing solutions or concentrates
  • Ensure no skin on the wrists or forearms is exposed between glove cuff and sleeve — this is a common skin contact point for welding fume deposition
  • Change gloves when Cr VI-contaminated and before touching face or eating
  • For chrome ulcer prevention: cover any cut or abraded skin before working in Cr VI environments

Eye & Face Protection

  • Standard welding helmet protects the face during welding; safety glasses underneath are mandatory
  • Full-face APR (required at higher concentrations) also provides eye protection from Cr VI fume
  • Chemical splash goggles when handling chromate solutions, chromic acid, or chromate-containing paints
  • Face shield over goggles when splash risk from chromate solutions is high

Decontamination Sequence After Cr VI Work

  1. 1
    HEPA vacuum outer PPE (coveralls, jacket, boots) before removing to prevent Cr VI dust from becoming airborne during doffing.
  2. 2
    Remove outer PPE in the designated decontamination area. Fold items inward so the contaminated outer surface is contained. Place in labeled bags for laundry or disposal as appropriate.
  3. 3
    Remove gloves last before removing the respirator, using inside-out technique to avoid skin contact with the contaminated outer surface.
  4. 4
    Remove the respirator last — the same rule as for all toxic exposures. Wash hands before touching the respirator face seal area to avoid contaminating the inside of the facepiece.
  5. 5
    Wash hands and face thoroughly with soap and water. Do not use solvents or abrasive cleaners on skin. Proceed to shower if significant contamination occurred.

06 Medical Surveillance, Recordkeeping & Worker Rights

▼

Medical Surveillance Requirements

OSHA 1910.1026 requires medical surveillance for workers who perform Cr VI work at or above the Action Level (2.5 μg/m³) for 30 or more days per year. Given that stainless steel welding routinely generates Cr VI well above the AL without controls, BBB Tank Services workers who regularly weld, cut, or grind stainless steel are typically covered.

Initial Medical Examination

  • Medical and occupational history with emphasis on prior Cr VI, chromate, or stainless steel welding exposure
  • Respiratory symptoms questionnaire
  • Pulmonary function tests (spirometry)
  • Skin examination — looking for chrome ulcers, sensitization, or dermatitis
  • Nasal septum examination for perforation or mucosal damage
  • Baseline chest X-ray (at physician’s recommendation)
  • Completed prior to first assignment to Cr VI work above the AL

Periodic & Follow-Up Exams

  • Annual exam for all workers covered under the medical surveillance requirement
  • Medical removal protection if a physician recommends removal from Cr VI exposure — the worker must be moved to a comparable job without loss of pay, seniority, or benefits while under the MRP provision
  • Exit examination when leaving employment if covered under medical surveillance; record transfer required
  • Nasal septum examination at every annual visit — a perforation is a permanent indicator of past Cr VI overexposure

Exposure Records & Recordkeeping

  • Air monitoring records must be maintained for 30 years from the date of measurement
  • Medical records must be maintained for the duration of employment plus 30 years
  • Objective data (published industry data, exposure assessments) used to demonstrate exposure levels must be maintained for 30 years
  • Workers and former workers must be given access to their own exposure and medical records upon request — OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1020 right of access

Employee Rights Under OSHA 1910.1026

  • Right to be informed of the presence of Cr VI in the work area and the results of air monitoring within 15 working days of the monitoring being completed
  • Right to medical surveillance at no cost to the worker, performed at a reasonable time and place
  • Right to a written medical opinion following each medical examination
  • Right to medical removal protection — if removed from Cr VI work on a physician’s recommendation, the worker retains their pay rate, seniority, and benefits
  • Right to refuse unsafe work — a worker who is assigned to stainless steel welding without LEV, proper respiratory protection, or appropriate controls can exercise Stop Work Authority (Module 026)
✓ BBB Tank Services Cr VI Requirements Summary:
  • All stainless steel welding, cutting, and grinding requires LEV positioned 6–12 inches from the work point before starting
  • P100 (HEPA) filters required for all Cr VI operations — N95 and P95 are not acceptable
  • No dry sweeping or compressed air blowdown of Cr VI-contaminated areas — HEPA vacuum only
  • Wash hands and face before eating, drinking, or leaving the work area; do not launder Cr VI-contaminated clothing at home
  • Medical surveillance required for workers exposed above 2.5 μg/m³ for 30 or more days per year
  • Exposure and medical records maintained 30 years after end of employment
