Hand & Power Tool Safety Program

Safe selection, inspection, use, and storage of hand and power tools on BBB Tank Services job sites — including tank cleaning, maintenance, and field operations.

⏱ Est. 35 min 📋 6 Sections ✅ 10-Question Quiz 📅 Rev. Oct 2025

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Identify the leading causes of hand and power tool injuries
  • State OSHA general requirements for tool safety
  • Perform a proper pre-use inspection on hand and power tools
  • Apply safe operating practices for grinders, drills, saws, and pneumatic tools
  • Explain the purpose of guards and when they must be in place
  • Select the correct PPE for specific tool operations
  • Follow proper procedures for tool maintenance and defect reporting

01 Tool Hazards & OSHA Requirements

▼

Hand and power tools are involved in a significant percentage of all workplace injuries. At BBB Tank Services, workers routinely use grinders, drills, saws, impact wrenches, and hand tools in and around tanks — often in confined spaces, at elevation, or near flammable vapors. Understanding the hazards and OSHA requirements is the foundation of working safely.

Leading Causes of Tool-Related Injuries

Improper Use

  • Using the wrong tool for the job
  • Using a tool for which you are not trained
  • Bypassing or removing guards
  • Using excessive force or awkward posture
  • Operating tools while fatigued or distracted

Defective Equipment

  • Damaged cords, cracked housings, missing guards
  • Worn or dull cutting edges causing kick-back
  • Improperly mounted or cracked grinding wheels
  • Loose handles on chisels, hammers, and files
  • Air hoses with damaged fittings or couplings

OSHA Standards That Apply

StandardCovers
29 CFR 1926.300–307Construction: hand tools, power tools, abrasive wheels, pneumatic tools, powder-actuated tools, woodworking tools
29 CFR 1910.241–244General Industry: hand and portable powered tools, spray finishing, abrasive blasting
29 CFR 1910.212Machine guarding — guards must be in place whenever a tool is in use
29 CFR 1910.134 / 1926.103Respiratory protection for grinding, cutting, or sanding operations generating dust or fumes
Core OSHA Principle: Employers must provide the right tools for the job in safe working condition. Employees must use tools correctly, keep them in good repair, and never use a defective tool. Both share responsibility for tool safety.
⚠ Tank Services Specific Risk: Many BBB Tank Services jobs involve grinding, cutting, or power-brushing operations near residual flammable vapors. A spark from a grinder, saw, or impact tool can ignite vapors with explosive force. Always verify the atmosphere with a gas monitor before any spark-generating tool use near tanks. Hot-work permits are required — see the Hot Work program (Module 027).

02 Hand Tool Safety

▼

Hand tools include any non-powered tool: hammers, wrenches, screwdrivers, chisels, files, knives, pry bars, and more. They are involved in more injuries than many workers expect — often because their perceived simplicity leads to careless use.

General Rules for All Hand Tools

  • Use the right tool for the job. A wrench is not a hammer. A screwdriver is not a pry bar. Improvising with the wrong tool causes both tool damage and injury.
  • Inspect before every use. Check for cracked handles, loose heads, damaged tips, and corrosion. A hammer with a cracked handle can fail and send the head flying.
  • Keep cutting tools sharp. Dull blades require more force, increasing the chance of slipping. A sharp blade controlled with light pressure is safer than a dull blade that requires force.
  • Cut away from your body. Always direct cutting edges and striking force away from yourself and others nearby.
  • Never carry sharp tools in your pocket. Use sheaths, pouches, or tool belts. Hand injuries from pocket-carried tools are common and preventable.
  • Pass tools handle-first. Never toss or throw tools to another person.
  • Secure your work. Use clamps or a vise rather than holding material in your hand while working on it.

Common Hand Tool Hazards by Type

🔨 Hammers & Mallets

  • Check head is firmly seated on handle
  • Replace cracked or splintered handles
  • Never strike hardened steel with a steel hammer — use a brass or dead-blow mallet
  • Never use a hammer as a pry bar

🔧 Wrenches & Pliers

  • Use the correct size — a loose fit rounds fasteners and causes knuckle injuries when the tool slips
  • Pull toward you rather than pushing when possible
  • Never use a pipe extension on a wrench to increase torque
  • Never use pliers as a wrench

🔨 Chisels & Punches

  • Wear safety glasses — chips fly at high velocity
  • Remove mushroomed heads by grinding before use
  • Hold with a tool holder, not bare fingers, when struck
  • Keep edges sharp — dull chisels require more force and slip more easily

🔪 Knives & Cutting Tools

  • Use retractable or self-retracting blades
  • Cut on a stable surface, never in your lap or against your body
  • Store in a sheath when not actively in use
  • Replace dull blades immediately — never force a dull blade

🔩 Files & Rasps

  • Always use a handle — the tang is sharp and can pierce a palm
  • Never use as a pry bar or hammer
  • Clean with a file card, not another metal object
  • Never strike against a hard object to clean

🔧 Screwdrivers

  • Match tip type and size to the fastener exactly
  • Never use as a chisel or pry bar
  • Replace cracked or worn handles
  • Never hold material in your hand while driving screws
Mushroomed Tools: Chisels, punches, and drift pins develop mushroomed heads when repeatedly struck. Metal fragments from the mushroomed edge become high-velocity projectiles when struck again. Grind mushroomed heads before use — do not wrap them in tape as a substitute.

03 Power Tool Safety — General Requirements

▼

Power tools multiply force and speed far beyond hand capabilities — which makes them both more productive and more dangerous. The same rules apply whether the tool is electric, pneumatic, or hydraulic.

Pre-Use Inspection — Required Before Every Use

  1. 1
    Inspect the cord or air hose. Check for cuts, abrasions, kinks, or damaged connectors. For electric tools, verify the ground pin is present and the plug is undamaged (see Module 013 — GFCI).
  2. 2
    Check the housing and body. Look for cracks, missing screws, or damage that could expose internal parts. A cracked housing can collapse under load, contacting moving parts.
  3. 3
    Verify all guards are in place. Never operate a power tool with a guard removed or disabled. If a guard is missing or broken, remove the tool from service.
  4. 4
    Check the accessory (blade, wheel, bit, disc). Inspect for cracks, chips, or wear. Verify it is correctly installed, properly tightened, and rated for the tool's speed (RPM).
  5. 5
    Test before use. After installing any accessory, run the tool at full speed for 60 seconds in a safe direction before bringing it to the work. Listen for unusual vibration or noise.

General Safe Operating Rules — All Power Tools

  • Read the operator manual for every tool you use. Specific models have specific requirements.
  • Never carry a running tool by its cord or air hose, or with your finger on the trigger.
  • Disconnect power before adjusting, changing accessories, or clearing jams. For electric tools, unplug. For pneumatic tools, disconnect the air supply AND bleed the line.
  • Keep both hands on the tool when operating two-handle tools (grinders, core drills, large saws).
  • Secure workpieces with clamps or a vise — never hold material freehand while cutting or drilling.
  • Do not use power tools in wet conditions unless rated for wet use and a GFCI is in use.
  • Never leave a running tool unattended. Power down before setting a tool down.
  • Keep bystanders clear. Establish a safety zone — at a minimum, no one within 10 feet during grinding operations.

Required PPE for Power Tool Operations

OperationRequired PPE
Grinding, cutting, chippingFace shield over safety glasses, hearing protection, leather gloves, leather apron or sleeves for heavy grinding
Drilling (metal, concrete)Safety glasses or goggles, hearing protection; gloves for concrete drilling
Sawing (circular, reciprocating)Safety glasses, hearing protection, cut-resistant gloves when changing blades
Pneumatic impact toolsSafety glasses, hearing protection; face shield if working near rusty or brittle fasteners
Sanding / surface prepSafety glasses, N95 or better respirator for dry sanding; hearing protection
All power tool useSteel-toed boots, no loose clothing, no dangling jewelry, hair tied back

04 Angle Grinders, Saws & Drills

▼

These three power tool types are among the most commonly used — and most commonly involved in serious injuries — on BBB Tank Services job sites.

Angle Grinders

Angle grinders are versatile but high-risk. A 4.5-inch grinding disc spins at up to 11,000 RPM. At that speed, a disc failure does not chip — it shatters, sending fragments at hundreds of feet per second.

⚠ Grinder Disc Failures Kill. Never use a disc that is cracked, chipped, has been dropped, or has exceeded its maximum RPM rating. Never use a cut-off disc for side grinding or a grinding disc for cutting. The wrong use generates side loads the disc is not designed to withstand.
  • Match the disc to the tool RPM. The disc’s max RPM must equal or exceed the tool’s no-load RPM. Never use a lower-rated disc on a higher-speed tool.
  • Inspect every disc before mounting. Perform the ring test: hang the disc on a finger and tap it with a screwdriver handle. A good disc rings clearly; a cracked disc thuds.
  • The guard must cover at least 180° of the wheel and be positioned between the wheel and the operator.
  • Allow the disc to reach full speed before contacting the workpiece. Apply light, even pressure — never force or lever the grinder.
  • Never use a cut-off wheel to grind and never use a grinding disc to cut. Each is rated for its own load direction only.
  • Stand to the side, not directly behind the grinder, in case of disc failure or kickback.
  • Secure the work. A workpiece that shifts or grabs causes sudden kickback — the grinder snaps violently toward the operator.

Circular Saws & Reciprocating Saws

  • Lower guards must be in place on circular saws at all times. The lower guard retracts during the cut and springs back to cover the blade the moment the saw clears the material.
  • Never wedge or tie back the lower guard. This is a serious OSHA violation and a common cause of severe lacerations.
  • Support the workpiece so the cut portion does not fall and bind the blade, causing kickback. Support both sides near the cut.
  • Set the blade depth so it extends no more than ¼ inch below the material thickness. Excessive depth increases kickback risk.
  • For reciprocating saws, keep the shoe firmly on the material and allow the blade to reach full speed before contacting the work.
  • Change blades and adjust fences/guards only after disconnecting power.

Drills & Impact Drivers

  • Secure the workpiece with clamps before drilling. Drills can grab a workpiece suddenly and spin it, causing severe lacerations.
  • Use the correct bit for the material (masonry bits for concrete, twist bits for metal, spade or Forstner bits for wood).
  • Keep bits sharp. Dull drill bits require excessive pressure, generate heat, and are more likely to grab.
  • Reduce speed when breaking through the far side of material to prevent the bit from grabbing.
  • Clear chips and swarf regularly. Do not use your hand — use a brush.
  • Never use a drill as a mixer for paint or compounds unless fitted with a proper mixing paddle attachment rated for that purpose.
Scenario — Think It Through

The Missing Guard

A crew member picks up an angle grinder from the tool bin. He notices the disc guard has been removed — someone has taken it off to fit into a tight space and hasn’t replaced it. He needs to grind a weld bead inside a tank manway and thinks he can be careful. He is wearing a face shield and leather gloves.

Correct action: Do not use the grinder without the guard. The guard is not optional PPE — it is a required machine guard under OSHA 29 CFR 1910.212 and 1926.302. PPE is the last line of defense; it does not substitute for a machine guard. Locate the guard, reattach it, or get a different grinder. Report the missing guard to a supervisor so it can be tracked as a deficiency.

05 Pneumatic & Hydraulic Tools

▼

Pneumatic (air-powered) tools are widely used in tank services work — impact wrenches, air chisels, needle scalers, blow guns, and pneumatic drills. They present unique hazards related to compressed air and high-speed flying debris.

Pneumatic Tool Hazards

Compressed Air Injection

  • Compressed air directed at the skin can inject air under the skin — a life-threatening medical emergency
  • Never use air to clean yourself off — even at low pressure
  • OSHA limits dead-end air pressure for cleaning to 30 PSI when the nozzle is chipped or broken; this is NOT a license to blow air at people
  • Never horseplay with air hoses

Hose & Fitting Hazards

  • Air hoses under pressure that disconnect can whip violently, causing severe injury
  • Use safety wire, clips, or whip checks on all hose connections
  • Inspect hoses for cuts, bulges, and loose couplings before use
  • Always bleed air from the hose before disconnecting or adjusting tools

Pneumatic Tool Safe Practices

  • Use the correct air pressure. Check the tool’s nameplate for max inlet PSI. Over-pressurization causes tools to exceed their designed RPM — a leading cause of disc and wheel failures.
  • Install whip checks (safety cables) at every hose-to-hose and hose-to-tool connection. A whip check restrains the hose if a coupling fails under pressure.
  • Never kink an air hose to stop air flow. Use the shutoff valve at the manifold or compressor.
  • Disconnect air supply and bleed the hose before changing accessories, clearing jams, or performing any adjustment.
  • Keep hoses out of walkways and away from heat sources, sharp edges, and vehicle traffic.
  • Secure impact wrench sockets. Use impact-rated sockets only (black oxide, not chrome). Chrome sockets can shatter at impact tool torque levels. Use a retaining ring or pin to prevent socket ejection.

Needle Scalers & Air Chisels

Needle scalers and air chisels are common in tank interior prep work for removing scale, rust, and coating. These tools generate heavy vibration and flying debris.

  • Always wear a face shield over safety glasses — scale fragments are sharp and travel at high velocity
  • Wear hearing protection rated for the noise level — scalers routinely exceed 100 dB
  • Take vibration breaks to reduce hand-arm vibration syndrome (HAVS) risk: follow the 20-minutes-on / 10-minutes-off guideline when using scalers continuously
  • Inspect needle bundles before use. Replace if needles are bent, missing, or uneven
  • Never point a scaler at another person, even when not connected to air

Hydraulic Tools

Hydraulic torque wrenches and cutters may be used for large flange work on tank systems. Key rules:

  • Inspect hydraulic hoses for leaks, bulges, and damaged fittings before every use
  • Never attempt to locate a hydraulic leak by feel — use cardboard or paper. High-pressure hydraulic fluid injection into skin is a medical emergency
  • Release pressure from hydraulic lines before disconnecting any fitting
  • Stay clear of the work area when pressurizing a hydraulic circuit

06 Inspection, Maintenance, Storage & Defect Reporting

▼

Inspection Frequency

WhenWhat to Do
Before every useVisual inspection: cord/hose, housing, guard, accessory, trigger/switch. Run test for 60 seconds after mounting any new accessory.
After any incidentIf a tool is dropped, submerged, or involved in a near-miss or contact with energized equipment, it is removed from service until inspected by a supervisor or qualified person.
After any repairTools returned from repair are re-inspected and must pass a functional test before being returned to service.
Periodic (monthly)Full inventory inspection of all company tools: cords, housings, guards, accessories, storage condition. Results recorded in the tool inspection log.

When to Remove a Tool from Service

Remove any tool from service immediately if you observe any of the following:

Remove Hand Tools If:

  • Handle is cracked, splintered, or loose
  • Head is cracked or mushroomed
  • Cutting edge is excessively chipped or broken
  • Tool shows evidence of heat damage (blue/black discoloration)

Remove Power Tools If:

  • Guard is missing, damaged, or does not operate correctly
  • Cord or hose is cut, abraded, or has a missing ground pin
  • Housing is cracked or broken
  • Tool produces unusual noise, vibration, or sparks from the housing
  • Switch or trigger is faulty, sticks, or does not return to off

Defect Reporting Procedure

  1. 1
    Stop using the tool immediately. Do not attempt a field repair unless you are qualified and authorized to do so.
  2. 2
    Tag the tool “DEFECTIVE — DO NOT USE.” Use a red danger tag or defective equipment tag from the job site supply. Remove it from the work area.
  3. 3
    Report to your supervisor before the end of that work shift. Describe the defect and when it was discovered.
  4. 4
    Document it. Record the defect in the daily safety log or tool inspection log: date, tool description, defect found, your name.
  5. 5
    Do not return the tool to service until it has been repaired by a qualified person and passed a functional inspection.

Storage & Transport

  • Store tools clean and dry — moisture promotes rust and insulation degradation on electric tools
  • Store grinding discs and cutting wheels flat, in their original packaging, away from moisture and temperature extremes. Do not stack heavy items on top of discs.
  • Coil cords and hoses loosely — never wrap tightly around a tool or in a tight figure-8 that stresses the conductor
  • Transport sharp-edged tools in sheaths, rolls, or dedicated tool bags — not loose in a toolbox where they shift and are grabbed blindly
  • Lock up power tools when not in use — unauthorized use by untrained personnel is a real hazard on active job sites
Scenario — Think It Through

The Borrowed Grinder

While working inside a tank, a crew member runs out of grinding discs. Another worker loans him a grinder that was left in the gang box. It has no tag on it and he does not know when it was last inspected. He is behind schedule and wants to mount a disc and finish the weld prep quickly. The guard is present.

Correct action: Before using any tool, he must complete a pre-use inspection: check the cord, housing, guard, and verify the tool runs properly. He should check the tool log to confirm it has not been flagged as defective. A grinder that has been dropped, overloaded, or has an internal electrical fault may look fine but fail violently under load. Two minutes of inspection before use is a sound trade for the risk of a disc failure or electrocution inside a metal tank.
