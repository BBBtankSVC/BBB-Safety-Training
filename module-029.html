01 What Is Asbestos & Why It Is Dangerous

▼

Asbestos is a group of naturally occurring silicate minerals that were widely used in industrial applications throughout the 20th century for their remarkable heat resistance, tensile strength, and chemical inertness. Asbestos was considered an ideal material for insulation, fire protection, and chemical resistance — until it became clear that the microscopic fibers it releases when disturbed cause severe and fatal lung diseases.

What makes asbestos uniquely hazardous is that the fibers responsible for disease are invisible to the naked eye, have no odor, and produce no immediate symptoms. Workers exposed to high concentrations of asbestos fibers feel nothing at the time of exposure. The diseases caused by asbestos develop over a latency period of 10 to 50 years after initial exposure, meaning that workers exposed today may not develop symptoms until decades from now.

Asbestos-Related Diseases

🫌
Mesothelioma A rare and aggressive cancer of the lining of the lungs (pleura) or abdomen (peritoneum). Almost exclusively caused by asbestos exposure. Latency period of 20–50 years. No cure; median survival after diagnosis is 12–21 months. Even brief exposures have caused mesothelioma.
💤
Asbestosis Progressive scarring of lung tissue caused by accumulated asbestos fibers. Reduces lung capacity over time, causing shortness of breath, coughing, and eventually respiratory failure. No cure; typically requires high cumulative exposure. Latency period of 10–20 years.
💥
Lung Cancer Asbestos exposure significantly increases the risk of lung cancer, particularly in smokers (multiplicative, not additive risk). Latency period of 15–35 years. Asbestos-related lung cancer is indistinguishable from smoking-related lung cancer at diagnosis.
📋
Pleural Plaques & Effusion Thickening and calcification of the pleural lining, or fluid accumulation in the pleural space. Can cause chest pain and reduced lung function. A marker of asbestos exposure and an indicator of elevated cancer risk. Latency period of 20–30 years.

Types of Asbestos

There are six regulated types of asbestos, grouped into two families. All are hazardous; some are more potent than others:

Serpentine Family

  • Chrysotile (white asbestos) — by far the most commonly used; found in the majority of ACM in the U.S.; curly fibers; still highly hazardous despite being considered less potent than amphiboles

Amphibole Family (more potent)

  • Amosite (brown asbestos) — common in pipe and boiler insulation
  • Crocidolite (blue asbestos) — most hazardous type; needle-like fibers; strongly linked to mesothelioma
  • Tremolite, actinolite, anthophyllite — less common; found as contaminants in other materials
⚠ There Is No Safe Level of Asbestos Exposure. OSHA sets a PEL to define the maximum legally permissible exposure — not a safe level. No scientific study has established a threshold below which asbestos causes no harm. Every exposure adds to cumulative risk. BBB Tank Services policy is to treat all suspected ACM as if it contains the most hazardous type of asbestos until proven otherwise.

02 Where Asbestos Is Found at Tank Sites

▼

Petroleum storage tank facilities built or renovated before approximately 1980 are likely to contain asbestos-containing materials (ACM). Facilities built between 1980 and 1990 may also contain ACM in some applications. BBB Tank Services operates at refineries, terminals, and tank farms that may be decades old, and workers must recognize where ACM is typically found.

⚠ You Cannot Identify ACM by Looking at It. Asbestos-containing materials look identical to non-asbestos materials of the same type. White pipe insulation could contain 30% chrysotile or none at all. Gray floor tiles could be 20% amosite or completely ACM-free. The only reliable identification method is laboratory analysis of a sample taken by a qualified person. Never assume a material is ACM-free based on appearance alone.

Common ACM Locations at Petroleum Tank Facilities

Pipe Insulation

High Risk

Magnesia or calcium silicate pipe insulation on steam lines, heat tracing, and process piping. Commonly contains chrysotile or amosite. Highly friable when aged or damaged.

Valve & Flange Insulation

High Risk

Removable asbestos blankets or molded insulation covers on valves and flanged joints. Often deteriorated and friable. Commonly disturbed during maintenance.

Gaskets & Packing

High Risk

Asbestos-containing compressed sheet gaskets in older flanges; rope packing in valve stems. Releasing fibers during removal or grinding is a serious hazard specific to mechanical work.

Tank & Vessel Insulation

High Risk

Sprayed-on or pre-formed insulation on heated storage tanks and process vessels. May appear as a grey or white textured coating. Extremely hazardous when disturbed.

Boiler & Furnace Insulation

High Risk

Fire-rated insulation on heating equipment, furnace doors, and firebox linings in tank heating systems. Often contains multiple types of asbestos.

Building Materials

Moderate

Vinyl floor tiles, ceiling tiles, roof shingles, corrugated roofing panels, transite pipe (water supply), drywall joint compound, and exterior siding on older pump houses and operator buildings.

Fireproofing

Moderate

Sprayed-on fireproofing on structural steel, including pipe racks and structural members supporting tank farm infrastructure. Older sprayed fireproofing almost always contains asbestos.

Electrical Components

Moderate

Arc chutes in older switchgear, electrical panel boards, and motor starters. Wire insulation on very old electrical systems. Insulating tape and paper in older transformers.

Presumed Asbestos-Containing Material (PACM)

OSHA’s asbestos construction standard (29 CFR 1926.1101) defines Presumed Asbestos-Containing Material (PACM) as thermal system insulation (TSI) and surfacing material in buildings or structures constructed no later than 1980. This presumption means:

  • Any thermal insulation (pipe, vessel, tank) or surfacing material (sprayed fireproofing, floor tiles, ceiling tiles) in a pre-1980 facility must be treated as ACM unless sampling and analysis by a certified industrial hygienist definitively confirms otherwise
  • BBB Tank Services applies the PACM presumption broadly — if the age of the facility is unknown, treat as PACM
  • Facilities built after 1980 may still contain ACM; PACM presumption just does not automatically apply to post-1980 construction

03 OSHA Standards & Exposure Limits

▼

Applicable OSHA Standards

StandardScopeApplicability to BBB
29 CFR 1926.1101 Asbestos — Construction. Applies to all construction work involving ACM including removal, encapsulation, repair, and disturbance during renovation or demolition. Primary standard for BBB Tank Services work. Tank maintenance, insulation work, and mechanical work at facilities falls under construction standards.
29 CFR 1910.1001 Asbestos — General Industry. Applies to manufacturing operations and some maintenance work in general industry settings. May apply when BBB work is classified as maintenance rather than construction at a general industry facility.
EPA NESHAP
40 CFR Part 61, Subpart M		 National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants for asbestos. Controls asbestos emissions during demolition and renovation of structures containing friable ACM. Host facility responsibility, but BBB supervisors must be aware. Federally regulated asbestos removal requires notification to state environmental agency before work begins.

OSHA Exposure Limits

Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL)

  • 0.1 f/cc as an 8-hour time-weighted average (TWA)
  • f/cc = fibers per cubic centimeter of air
  • At this concentration, the fibers are invisible — there is no practical way to know you are at or near the PEL without air monitoring instruments
  • Exceeding the PEL triggers the full requirements of 1926.1101 including medical surveillance, respirator use, and regulated area controls

Excursion Limit (EL)

  • 1.0 f/cc as a 30-minute short-term exposure average
  • The short-term limit for brief high-exposure tasks
  • Exceeding the EL, even briefly, triggers immediate respirator use and engineering controls
  • Friable ACM disturbed without controls can generate concentrations thousands of times higher than the PEL in seconds

Asbestos Work Classifications

OSHA 1926.1101 defines four classes of asbestos work, each with different requirements based on the type and quantity of disturbance. BBB Tank Services workers must understand which class applies to any potential asbestos encounter.

Class I

Most hazardous — Licensed contractor only
  • Removal of thermal system insulation (TSI) and surfacing ACM
  • Removing pipe insulation, vessel insulation, or sprayed-on fireproofing
  • Requires licensed asbestos abatement contractor
  • Requires negative-pressure enclosure, HEPA air filtration, and air monitoring
  • BBB workers must NEVER perform Class I work

Class II

High risk — Licensed contractor only
  • Removal of ACM that is not TSI or surfacing material
  • Includes floor tiles, roofing materials, siding, and cementitious materials
  • Requires licensed abatement contractor and significant controls
  • Air monitoring, PPE, and regulated areas required
  • BBB workers must NEVER perform Class II work

Class III

Moderate risk — Trained workers only
  • Repair and maintenance activities that disturb ACM or PACM
  • Includes removing old gaskets, working around damaged pipe insulation
  • Requires 16-hour O&M (Operations & Maintenance) training minimum
  • Specific PPE, wet methods, HEPA vacuum, and glove-bag techniques required
  • BBB workers may perform Class III only with specific training and authorization

Class IV

Lower risk — 2-hour awareness training
  • Custodial activities adjacent to ACM that may contact but do not disturb it
  • Cleaning up dust or debris in an area where ACM is present but not being worked
  • Does not involve cutting, sanding, drilling, or scraping ACM
  • Requires HEPA vacuum, wet methods for cleanup, and half-face APR minimum
  • This training module qualifies BBB workers for Class IV awareness only

04 BBB Tank Services Asbestos Policy

▼

The Core Policy: Assume and Protect

BBB Tank Services operates under an “Assume and Protect” asbestos policy at all facilities built before 1990. This policy has three elements:

1. Assume ACM Is Present

  • Treat all thermal insulation, surfacing materials, gaskets, and building materials in pre-1990 facilities as if they contain asbestos until laboratory analysis proves otherwise
  • Do not rely on facility records, labels, or verbal assurances as confirmation that materials are ACM-free
  • When in doubt about a material’s age or composition, treat it as ACM

2. Do Not Disturb

  • Do not cut, drill, sand, grind, break, chip, or otherwise disturb any material suspected of containing asbestos
  • Do not remove suspected ACM gaskets or packing without authorization from a competent asbestos professional
  • Do not walk through or work in an area with visibly damaged insulation without respiratory protection

3. Stop Work and Report

  • If suspected ACM is encountered during work, stop immediately and move workers out of the area
  • Notify the BBB supervisor and the facility health and safety representative
  • Do not resume work in that area until the material has been assessed by a qualified industrial hygienist and proper controls are in place

What BBB Workers Are and Are Not Authorized to Do

Gasket Replacement — A Specific BBB Concern. Replacing flange gaskets is a routine BBB Tank Services task. In pre-1990 facilities, the gasket being removed may contain asbestos. Scraping or grinding an old asbestos gasket from a flange face releases fibers directly at the worker’s breathing zone. BBB policy requires that any gasket removal in a pre-1990 facility first be assessed for ACM. If ACM cannot be ruled out, wet methods, a glove bag or tent enclosure, and supplied-air respirator are required. This is a Class III task requiring specific training.

05 PPE & Response When ACM Is Encountered

▼

Minimum PPE When Working Near Intact Suspected ACM

When working in an area where intact (undamaged, not friable) suspected ACM is present but is not being disturbed, BBB Tank Services requires:

  • Half-face APR with P100 (HEPA) filters — protects against asbestos fibers. OV+P100 combination cartridges also provide protection. N95 filtering facepieces do NOT meet OSHA’s asbestos respirator requirements.
  • Disposable coveralls (Tyvek or equivalent) — prevent fiber contamination of street clothing; discarded as asbestos waste after use in the work area
  • Nitrile gloves — prevent skin contact with fibers
  • Safety glasses — standard eye protection; goggles if dust is anticipated

PPE for Incidental Contact / Class IV Work

  • Half-face or full-face APR with P100 filters (full-face if heavy dust or high contact potential)
  • Disposable coveralls with hood — fully sealed at wrists, ankles, and face opening
  • Disposable boot covers over safety boots to prevent tracking fibers out of the work area
  • Nitrile gloves, double-gloved when handling ACM debris
  • HEPA vacuum — standard vacuums spread asbestos fibers; only HEPA-rated vacuums trap asbestos. No compressed air blowdown.
  • Wet methods — amended water (water + surfactant) applied to suppress fiber release before and during cleanup

Decontamination — Leaving a Potentially ACM-Exposed Area

  1. 1
    HEPA vacuum all surfaces of your coveralls while still wearing them. Work top to bottom, covering all surfaces. This is the critical step — fibers clinging to coveralls will be released if not removed before doffing.
  2. 2
    Remove boot covers carefully — fold outward so the potentially contaminated outer surface is contained inside the folded cover. Place directly in an asbestos waste bag.
  3. 3
    Remove outer gloves using the standard inside-out technique. Place in the waste bag.
  4. 4
    Remove coveralls carefully — fold inward as you remove them so the potentially contaminated outer surface remains inside. Place directly in the asbestos waste bag.
  5. 5
    Remove the respirator last — this is the same rule as for toxic chemical work. The respirator protects you during the entire decontamination sequence. Remove it after all other potentially contaminated PPE is off and in the waste bag.
  6. 6
    Wash hands and face thoroughly before eating, drinking, or leaving the site. Never eat, drink, or smoke in areas where asbestos work has been performed.

Asbestos Waste Disposal

  • All asbestos waste must be placed in sealed, leak-tight containers (typically double polyethylene bags of at least 6 mil thickness) and labeled with the OSHA asbestos hazard warning label
  • Asbestos waste cannot be disposed of in regular trash — it must go to a licensed asbestos waste landfill
  • Used PPE (coveralls, boot covers, gloves) that contacted ACM are asbestos waste and must be bagged and disposed of accordingly
  • Contact the facility health and safety representative for asbestos waste handling at customer sites — the facility typically manages this process
Scenario — Unexpected Discovery

The Crumbling Pipe Insulation

A BBB pipefitter is removing a section of process piping at a 1965-era refinery. As he loosens a pipe support, he disturbs the adjacent pipe insulation, which crumbles and releases a visible dust cloud. He is wearing a half-face OV+P100 respirator, Tyvek coveralls, and safety glasses. He immediately steps back and holds his breath momentarily.

What he did right: He was wearing appropriate PPE, which provided immediate protection. He stopped immediately when ACM was disturbed. What must happen next: Stop all work in that area. Notify the BBB supervisor and the facility safety representative. Do not attempt to clean up the debris himself. The area must be assessed, air monitoring performed, and a licensed abatement contractor engaged before work in that area continues. The pipefitter’s name and the exposure event must be documented for his medical surveillance file. Even with proper PPE, an unexpected ACM disturbance must be reported and documented.

06 Medical Surveillance & Recordkeeping

▼

Medical Surveillance Requirements

OSHA requires medical surveillance for workers who perform Class I, II, or III asbestos work, or for any worker whose exposure is at or above the action level (0.1 f/cc). Medical surveillance consists of:

Initial Medical Exam

  • Comprehensive medical and work history
  • Standardized respiratory questionnaire
  • Pulmonary function tests (spirometry)
  • Chest X-ray (PA, 14×17 inches, read by a B-reader physician)
  • Completed prior to assignment to ACM work

Periodic Exams

  • Annual for workers performing Class I, II, or III work
  • Chest X-ray frequency based on exposure duration and B-reader recommendation
  • At termination of employment if the worker was exposed at or above the action level
  • All medical records maintained for 30 years plus the duration of employment

Asbestos Exposure Documentation

Any time a BBB Tank Services worker is potentially exposed to asbestos — even with appropriate PPE in place — the following must be documented:

  • Date, time, and location of the exposure event
  • Name and job title of the exposed worker(s)
  • Description of the ACM or suspected ACM involved
  • Description of the work being performed when exposure occurred
  • PPE worn at the time of exposure
  • Whether the event was planned or unexpected
  • Air monitoring results, if available

This documentation becomes part of the worker’s permanent exposure record, which must be maintained for 30 years after the end of employment per 29 CFR 1910.1001(m). Given the 20–50 year latency of asbestos diseases, these records are critical for future medical diagnosis and workers’ compensation claims.

Employee Rights Under Asbestos Standards

  • Right to know — OSHA requires that workers be informed of the presence of ACM or PACM in their work areas before beginning work
  • Right to air monitoring results — workers must have access to the results of any air monitoring performed in their work area within 15 days of receiving results
  • Right to medical records — workers have the right to access their own asbestos-related medical and exposure records
  • Right to refuse unsafe work — a worker who is assigned to disturb suspected ACM without proper training, PPE, or controls has the right to refuse the assignment without retaliation (see Module 026 — Stop Work Authority)
✓ BBB Tank Services Asbestos Summary — The Non-Negotiables:
  • Treat all insulation and surfacing materials in pre-1990 facilities as ACM until proven otherwise
  • Never disturb suspected ACM — stop work and report immediately if ACM is encountered unexpectedly
  • BBB workers are authorized for Class IV (awareness) only — never perform Class I, II, or III work without specific asbestos O&M or abatement training
  • Half-face APR with P100 filters minimum when working near suspected ACM
  • Decontaminate properly — HEPA vacuum coveralls before doffing; respirator comes off last
  • Document every asbestos exposure event; records kept 30 years
