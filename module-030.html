General Housekeeping

Orderly, clean worksites are not just professional — they are the foundation of safety. Poor housekeeping causes more injuries at petroleum tank sites than most workers realize.

⏱ Est. 30 min 📋 6 Sections ✅ 10-Question Quiz 📅 Rev. Oct 2025

  • Explain why housekeeping is a safety function, not just an aesthetic concern
  • Identify the OSHA standards that govern workplace housekeeping

  • Describe the requirements for walking surfaces, aisles, and access routes
  • Explain the spontaneous combustion hazard of oily rags and proper disposal

01 Why Housekeeping Is a Safety Function

▼

Housekeeping is the most consistently underrated safety practice in industrial work. It is often treated as a janitorial function — something done at the end of the day to make the site look presentable. This view is wrong. Poor housekeeping is a direct cause of workplace injuries and fatalities. Slips and trips from cluttered floors, fires from accumulated combustibles, chemical exposures from uncontained spills, and dropped objects from unsecured materials are all housekeeping failures with serious consequences.

OSHA data consistently shows that slips, trips, and falls account for approximately 15–20% of all occupational fatalities and a far larger share of disabling injuries. The overwhelming majority are preventable through basic housekeeping. At BBB Tank Services, good housekeeping is a non-negotiable daily responsibility for every worker on every job site — not just the newest member of the crew.

What Housekeeping Prevents

🤦

Slips, Trips & Falls

Debris, spilled liquids, electrical cords, and cluttered walkways are the primary causes of slip, trip, and fall injuries on tank sites.

🔥

Fire & Explosion

Accumulated combustibles near ignition sources, oily rags left in piles, and petroleum-soaked debris dramatically increase fire risk at tank facilities.

🔨

Dropped Objects

Unsecured tools, loose materials on elevated platforms, and improperly stored equipment become projectiles when disturbed or when work vibration causes them to shift.

⚗️

Chemical Exposure

Uncontained spills, improperly stored chemicals, and unwashed surfaces expose workers to skin contact and inhalation hazards long after the initial spill event.

🚨

Blocked Egress

Materials stored in aisles or near exits prevent rapid evacuation during emergencies and block access to fire extinguishers, eyewash stations, and emergency equipment.

🐛

Pest & Disease Vectors

Food waste, standing water, and accumulated debris attract pests. In outdoor tank farm environments, this includes venomous snakes and spiders as well as rodents.

02 Walking Surfaces, Aisles & Access Routes

▼

OSHA Walking Surface Requirements

Under 29 CFR 1910.22, OSHA requires that all places of employment be kept clean, orderly, and in a sanitary condition. Specifically for walking and working surfaces:

  • The floor of every workroom must be maintained in a clean and dry condition as far as reasonably practicable. Where wet processes are used, drainage must be maintained and false floors, platforms, mats, or other dry standing places must be provided where necessary.
  • Every floor, working place, and passageway must be kept free from protruding nails, splinters, loose boards, and unnecessary holes and openings
  • Aisles and passageways must be kept sufficiently clear — at least 28 inches wide for pedestrian access, wider where equipment operates
  • Permanent aisles and passageways must be appropriately marked

Specific Surface Hazards at BBB Tank Services Sites

Petroleum Spills on Metal Surfaces

  • Metal tank tops, catwalks, stair treads, and grating become extremely slippery when contaminated with oil, grease, or petroleum product
  • Oil contamination on metal grating is essentially invisible until a worker slips
  • Clean spills immediately with absorbent material; never leave petroleum contamination on any walking surface
  • Apply non-slip coatings or mats to high-traffic metal surfaces where spills are likely

Uneven Dike Terrain

  • Dike berms are sloped, often irregular, and may have soft spots after rain
  • Pipes, valve stems, and conduit at ground level create trip hazards in dike areas
  • Mark low-clearance obstacles and ground-level hazards with high-visibility paint or flags
  • Maintain clear walking paths through dike areas; do not store materials in the path of emergency egress routes

Cord and Hose Management

Extension cords, air hoses, water hoses, and welding cables laid across walkways are a leading cause of trip injuries on tank job sites. Requirements:

  • Route cords and hoses out of walkways wherever possible — run along walls, under grating, or overhead using cord hangers
  • Where cords or hoses must cross a walkway, secure them with a cord bridge or bright-yellow trip hazard cover visible from multiple angles
  • Never leave cords or hoses coiled on walking surfaces — a coiled cord is a guaranteed trip hazard
  • Tag damaged or frayed cords and remove them from service immediately; do not cover electrical damage with tape and continue using the cord
  • Retract and store hoses when not in active use; do not leave air hoses pressurized and unattended across walkways

Floor Openings, Grating & Covers

  • All floor openings (sumps, manholes, drains, inspection ports) must be covered or barricaded when not actively in use
  • Grating covers removed for maintenance must be immediately replaced when work in that area is complete; if the opening must remain open during work, barrier it with a guardrail system and post a warning
  • Never stand a removed grating cover against a wall or lean it against equipment — it can fall and strike a worker or create a secondary trip hazard
  • Covers for floor openings must be capable of supporting twice the maximum intended load

03 Waste Management & Combustible Hazards

▼

General Waste Disposal

Waste accumulation is one of the most common housekeeping failures on construction and maintenance job sites. OSHA 1926.25 requires that construction areas be kept free of unnecessary accumulation of material and that waste be disposed of at regular intervals. At BBB Tank Services, “at the end of the day” is the minimum; at active job sites, continuous waste management throughout the shift is required.

  • Use clearly marked and appropriately sized waste containers for general debris; never allow containers to overflow
  • Segregate waste by type: general debris, scrap metal, petroleum-contaminated materials, and hazardous waste each go in separate designated containers
  • Scrap lumber and form lumber must have protruding nails removed or bent flat before being placed in waste piles
  • Never burn trash or debris at a petroleum tank facility — open burning is prohibited
  • Dispose of petroleum-contaminated materials per the facility’s waste management procedures — oil-soaked rags, absorbent pads, and soil must be treated as petroleum waste, not general trash

🔥 Spontaneous Combustion of Oily Rags — A Real and Recurring Hazard

Rags, wipes, and absorbent materials soaked in petroleum products, paints, wood stains, or drying oils (linseed oil, tung oil) can undergo spontaneous combustion — they can ignite and catch fire without any external ignition source. This occurs when the oxidation of the oil in the rag generates heat faster than the heat can dissipate. A pile of oily rags left in a closed container or a bin in the corner can reach ignition temperature within hours.

  • Never pile oily rags in a corner, on a shelf, or in a closed trash bag — the confined space traps heat and dramatically increases combustion risk
  • Store oily rags in a metal container with a self-closing lid specifically designed for oily waste; the lid restricts oxygen when the container is closed and the metal body does not contribute fuel
  • Never mix oily rags with other combustible trash — they must be in their own dedicated metal container
  • Dispose of oily waste containers daily or before they are more than half full; do not accumulate oily rags over multiple days
  • At petroleum tank sites, all rags, wipes, and absorbent pads used to clean product spills must be treated as petroleum-contaminated waste

Spill Response & Cleanup

Spills of petroleum products, chemicals, or other hazardous materials must be cleaned up immediately. A spill left unaddressed is both a slip hazard and, at tank sites, a fire and environmental hazard.

  1. 1
    Stop the source. Before cleaning up, confirm the source of the spill is stopped. Cleaning up an ongoing spill is futile and exposes workers to continuous contamination.
  2. 2
    Eliminate ignition sources in the vicinity of any flammable spill. A petroleum spill is a fire hazard until it is fully contained and removed.
  3. 3
    Contain the spill using absorbent booms, drain covers, or berming to prevent it from spreading, reaching floor drains, or flowing into the dike perimeter drainage.
  4. 4
    Apply absorbent material (granular absorbent, absorbent pads) and allow it to fully absorb the product before sweeping up. Do not hose down petroleum spills to the drain.
  5. 5
    Bag and label the contaminated material and dispose of per the facility’s petroleum waste procedures. Report the spill per the BBB Tank Services environmental incident reporting protocol.
⚠ Never Hose Down Petroleum Spills to Floor Drains. Storm drains and process drains at tank facilities discharge to treatment systems, water bodies, or secondary containment. Washing petroleum product to a drain is an environmental violation under the Clean Water Act, triggers regulatory reporting, and can result in fines. Always contain and absorb petroleum spills; never dilute them with water and wash them away.

04 Material Storage Safety

▼

Improperly stored materials are a source of multiple hazard types simultaneously: tip-over injuries when stacked material falls on workers, dropped-object injuries when materials on elevated surfaces are dislodged, and fire hazards when combustible materials are stored near ignition sources. Correct storage is a housekeeping requirement, not just an organizational preference.

General Storage Principles

📚 Stacking Height

  • Stack materials no higher than they can be safely reached or removed without climbing on the stack
  • Limit stacking height for bags, drums, and irregular materials to prevent tip-over: bagged materials maximum 10 ft; drums on their sides maximum 2 tiers; drums upright with chocks
  • Never store heavy materials on elevated platforms without positive retention (chains, straps, or guardrails)

📄 Aisle Clearance

  • Maintain a minimum 28-inch clearance in all pedestrian aisles and 36 inches in frequently used aisles
  • Aisles to emergency equipment (fire extinguishers, eyewash, emergency shutoffs) must remain completely clear at all times
  • No material storage within 3 feet of electrical panels, fire suppression system heads, or emergency exits

⚙ Chemical Storage

  • Store chemicals per their SDS requirements: acids separated from bases, flammables in approved flammable storage cabinets, oxidizers separated from combustibles
  • No chemical storage directly on the floor where product leakage cannot be detected; use secondary containment trays
  • Label every container; never store chemicals in unlabeled or improperly labeled containers

🔨 Tool Storage

  • Return tools to designated storage areas at the end of each shift; do not leave tools on tank tops, scaffold platforms, or elevated work areas overnight
  • Store hand tools with cutting edges protected; never leave sharp tools exposed in tool bags or on work surfaces where workers could reach in without seeing the blade
  • Tag and segregate damaged tools from serviceable ones to prevent inadvertent use

💧 Flammables & Compressed Gas

  • Flammable liquids in quantities greater than one gallon must be stored in approved safety cans with self-closing lids and flame arrestors
  • Compressed gas cylinders must be secured upright at all times — see Module 027 (Welding Safety)
  • No flammable liquid storage within 20 feet of a petroleum tank unless in an approved flammable storage cabinet

👳 Material Elevation Risk

  • Any material stored on an elevated surface (scaffold platform, tank top, building roof) must be secured to prevent falling
  • Use toeboards, netting, or tool lanyards to retain small items on elevated platforms
  • Never leave loose materials on elevated surfaces during breaks; remove or positively secure everything before stepping away
Scenario — Storage Failure

The Drum That Didn’t Roll

A crew has staged six drums of cleaning solvent on a concrete pad adjacent to a tank. The drums are standing upright in two rows but are not chocked or secured. A passing forklift clips the edge of the pad, creating vibration that causes the outer row of three drums to tip. One drum strikes a worker on the lower leg, causing a fracture. When the drum impacts the ground and the lid is breached, solvent releases near welding activity 40 feet away.

What went wrong: Three housekeeping failures converged: drums not chocked or secured (storage failure), workers in the fall zone of a precarious stack (layout failure), and the staging location was too close to hot work activity (separation failure). All three are correctable with basic housekeeping planning. Drums must be chocked on both sides or secured with racking. Heavy materials must not be staged in areas where workers routinely pass. Flammable chemicals must be separated from ignition sources.

05 Petroleum Tank Site-Specific Housekeeping

▼

Tank sites present housekeeping challenges beyond those of general construction. The combination of flammable vapors, metal surfaces, confined dike areas, and the regulatory environment of active petroleum facilities requires a higher standard of housekeeping discipline than most other industrial settings.

Dike Area Housekeeping

Keep Dike Areas Clear

  • Dike interiors must have clear emergency egress routes at all times — no material staged across emergency walkways
  • No accumulation of debris, scrap material, or waste inside tank dikes — dike secondary containment must be functional and free of obstructions that could prevent it from holding a spill
  • Vegetation control: tall grass, weeds, and brush inside dikes are fire hazards and snake habitats; report overgrowth to the facility for control
  • Maintain clear access to dike drain valves, emergency shutoffs, and any firefighting infrastructure within the dike

Dike Surface Conditions

  • Petroleum-contaminated soil inside dikes must be reported to the facility environmental representative — it may be a reportable spill
  • Do not fill dike interior depressions with gravel or fill material without facility authorization — this can affect stormwater drainage and spill containment calculations
  • Water accumulation in dike areas after rain must be pumped to appropriate treatment; never pump contaminated dike water to storm drains without testing and authorization

Tank Tops & Roof Surfaces

  • Maintain a clean tank top. Petroleum product contamination on tank roofs creates extreme slip hazards. Clean any spills immediately with absorbent material. Do not leave product standing on a tank roof at the end of a shift.
  • No loose tools or materials left on tank tops overnight — wind, equipment vibration, and expansion/contraction of the tank roof can dislodge anything not positively secured. All tools must be retrieved before leaving the tank.
  • Tool accounting on tank tops. Before descending from a tank top, account for every tool taken up. Use a tool inventory list for elevated work; a dropped tool from a tank top is a serious struck-by hazard for workers below.
  • No food, drink, or tobacco on tank tops or inside tank structures — petroleum vapor contamination is present, and this is both a health issue and a potential ignition source.

06 BBB Tank Services Housekeeping Program

▼

Housekeeping Is Everyone’s Responsibility

A common and dangerous misconception is that housekeeping is the responsibility of a designated worker — the apprentice, the newest crew member, or whoever the supervisor assigns. At BBB Tank Services, every worker is responsible for the condition of their immediate work area at all times. This means:

  • You clean up as you go, not just at the end of the shift
  • You do not walk past a hazard created by poor housekeeping without addressing it or reporting it
  • You do not leave a spill for the next shift to deal with
  • You return every tool you use to its designated location before moving to the next task
  • You do not create housekeeping hazards and assume someone else will address them

Supervisor Responsibilities

  • Conduct a pre-shift housekeeping inspection of the entire work area and correct deficiencies before allowing crew to begin work
  • Ensure adequate waste containers of the correct type (including metal oily rag containers) are available and positioned in the work area before work begins
  • Include housekeeping in the daily tailgate/toolbox talk — identify specific housekeeping hazards from the previous shift or expected for the current shift
  • Enforce the end-of-shift housekeeping checklist daily without exception
  • Report repeated housekeeping violations to BBB Tank Services management for follow-up

✓ The Housekeeping Standard: If a customer site safety officer, a BBB supervisor, or an OSHA inspector walked through your work area right now, would the housekeeping pass inspection? If not, stop and fix it before continuing work. A clean, orderly worksite is a direct reflection of the crew’s safety culture — and it is the foundation that all other safety practices are built on.
