Discipline in Support of Safety Program

📅 Rev. 10/31/2025 🏢 BBB Tank Services, LLC ⏱ ~12 min read 📝 10-question quiz
⚖1. Role of the Disciplinary System
Purpose — Not Punishment The BBB Tank Services disciplinary system does NOT exist to punish employees. Its purpose is to help control the work environment so that workers are protected and accidents are prevented. It provides workers with opportunities to correct their behavior before an accident happens.

The disciplinary system is one of the keys to successfully implementing the company's safety and health program. It ensures that safety rules and safe working practices are taken seriously and followed. It lets employees know what is expected of them — and what actions the company will take if those expectations are not met.

The employee's supervisor and all members of management are responsible for the enforcement of this disciplinary program. On any job site, the Site Supervisor has specific responsibility for enforcing company safety rules, policies, and safe work procedures.

🚫3. Violations Resulting in Immediate Suspension or Termination

The following are grounds for immediate suspension and penalties up to and including termination of employment:

  • Fighting, provoking, or engaging in acts of violence against another person on company property.
  • Failure to follow written or verbal safe work procedures, company safety rules, or authorized posted safety instructions.
  • Willful damage to property.
  • Failure to wear required personal protective equipment (eye protection, hearing protection, safety helmets, etc.).
  • Not using safety harnesses and lanyards when fall protection is required.
  • Removing or making inoperative safety guards on tools and equipment.
  • Tampering with machine safeguards or removing machine tags or locks.
  • Removing barriers and/or guardrails and not replacing them.
  • Failure to follow recognized industry practices.
  • Major traffic violations while using a company vehicle.
  • Engaging in dangerous horseplay.
  • Failure to notify the company of a hazardous situation.
  • Theft.
  • Violation of company policies regarding alcohol, non-prescription drugs, and illegal drugs.
Managers & Supervisors Are Held Accountable Too When the company determines that a person with safety responsibilities has failed to perform those duties, this is also considered a disciplinary violation subject to the same penalties.
🤝4–6. Training, Supervision & Employee Involvement

Training: Employees must understand the disciplinary system and the consequences of deliberate, unacceptable behavior. Training allows employees to identify hazards and understand the standards the company expects.

Supervision: Supervision includes both training and corrective action. Effective supervision means correcting a problem before issuing punishment. When the relationship between employees and supervisors is open and interactive, problems are discussed and solutions are mutually agreed upon — reducing the need for formal disciplinary action.

Be Your Brother's Keeper Employees are encouraged to help informally enforce rules and practices — watching out for the safety and health of their colleagues. The goal is a safety culture in the field where employees look out for one another.

Two-Way Process: Once a problem is spotted, it must be discussed with the employee, who should be given at least 1 or 2 opportunities to change the behavior or correct the problem before formal disciplinary action is taken. Only after discussions — and possibly retraining — should disciplinary action be taken.

⚖7–8. Proportionality & Consistent Enforcement
  • Disciplinary actions must be proportionate to the seriousness of the offense and the frequency of its occurrence.
  • Supervisors must explain to the employee what behavior is unacceptable, why the corrective action is necessary, and how to prevent future violations.
  • Safe work practices are a condition of employment. Unsafe practices will not be tolerated.
  • The disciplinary system applies equally to everyone — including managers and supervisors, who may be subject to even more stringent disciplinary procedures.
  • For minor violations, supervisors meet with the employee to discuss the infraction, identify the rule that was violated, and describe the corrective action needed.
📝9. Documentation

Good recordkeeping is key to ensuring fairness and consistency. It is in the best interest of both the company and the employee to have written rules and documented disciplinary procedures.

  • All safety violations are documented using the Safety Hazard Citation format. A copy is placed in the employee's personnel file.
  • Instances of both good AND poor safety behavior should be documented.
  • Documentation tracks problem development, corrective actions taken, and the effectiveness of measures.
  • If warnings and corrective actions fail and the company decides to discharge an employee, documentation becomes critical legal protection.
Three Strikes Three citations for minor safety violations can be grounds for termination.

The company will conduct an annual review of personnel files of employees whose good overall safety records include only minor warnings.

🏆10. Positive Reinforcement
  • Supervisors should provide frequent reinforcement of safe work practice training through informal observation.
  • Supervisors should provide special recognition — including safety awards and swag — when they observe positive safety behavior.
  • When observing individual workers at their tasks, supervisors should give oral and/or written feedback on what was done safely.
OSHA Caution on Injury-Free Award Programs OSHA recommends award systems that recognize positive activities rather than the absence of injuries. Award programs with prizes for hours worked without injury may create heavy peer pressure on workers NOT to report injuries — which is the opposite of a safe workplace culture.
📊Appendix B — Five-Step Disciplinary System

The company uses this five-step system to correct minor "General Offenses":

StepAction
1st ViolationInstruction and discussion concerning the violation, proper procedures, and the hazards they control. Notation for the supervisor's file.
2nd ViolationRe-instruction with notation placed in the employee's personnel file.
3rd ViolationWritten warning describing the violation and actions that will be taken if it recurs.
4th ViolationFinal warning; may include suspension.
5th ViolationDischarge.

Additionally, all infractions are classified as Minor or Major:

⚠ Minor Infraction
Any infraction of government, corporate, or client rules that does NOT have the immediate potential of causing serious damage or injury.
1st offense: Verbal warning from supervisor
2nd offense: Written notice placed on file
3rd offense: Written notice + time off without pay
4th offense: Termination of employment
🚨 Major Infraction
Any infraction of government, corporate, or client rules that DOES have the potential to cause immediate serious damage or injury.
1st offense: Time off without pay, or termination
2nd offense: Termination of employment
