Bloodborne Pathogens Exposure Control Plan

📅 Rev. 10/31/2025 ⚖ OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030 ⏱ ~15 min read 📝 10-question quiz
📋1. Purpose & Universal Precautions

BBB Tank Services is committed to providing a safe and healthy work environment. This Exposure Control Plan (ECP) is designed to minimize occupational exposure to bloodborne pathogens for all employees who may encounter blood or body fluids in the course of their work.

Universal Precautions — The Core Principle Individuals cannot know whether blood, body fluids, or detached tissues are infected with bloodborne pathogens. Therefore, ALL blood and body fluids must be treated as infectious — every time, without exception. This is called Universal Precautions.

This program applies to all employees with potential occupational exposure. Training, PPE, and all other protections under this plan are provided at no cost to the employee. The Company Safety Director is responsible for effective implementation and management of this program.

Any employee may request a copy of this plan at any time. Copies will be provided at no charge within 15 days of the request.

🎯2. Employee Exposure Determination

Occupational exposure to blood and body fluids is limited to agency-designated first aid responders at worksites where professional emergency medical services are not readily available.

When professional emergency medical services ARE readily available, company personnel trained as first responders are not required as part of their employment to provide first aid or CPR.

Report After Any First Aid Incident In the event of a first aid incident where blood or other potentially infectious materials are present, the affected first aid responder must report to the Safety Director before the end of their work shift.

The Safety Director will maintain a report that includes the responder's name, and the date, time, and description of the incident. If the responder wants the HBV vaccine series after an incident, they must receive it within 24 hours.

🔧3. Engineering Controls & Work Practices
  • Handwashing facilities are available at all job sites. Employees must wash hands after administering first aid.
  • If handwashing facilities are not immediately available, disposable one-use disinfecting towelettes will be provided and used until proper handwashing is possible.
  • All equipment used or contaminated during first aid assistance must be properly decontaminated or discarded in appropriate containers.
  • Engineering controls and work practices will be reviewed annually to ensure continued effectiveness.
  • Any "near miss" involving potential bloodborne pathogen exposure will also trigger a review and revision of controls as needed.
🦺4. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

All job site first aid kits must contain the following for bloodborne pathogen protection:

Gloves
Latex medical-type gloves. Provided in multiple types and sizes.
Eye Protection
Splash goggles and face shields to prevent blood or fluid contact with eyes and face.
Resuscitation Bags
Bag-valve-mask devices to prevent direct mouth-to-mouth contact during CPR.
One-Way CPR Mouthpiece
Provides a barrier during rescue breathing to prevent fluid exchange.

PPE may also include body protection such as aprons depending on the anticipated situation. All PPE is provided at no cost to employees and must be replaced, decontaminated, or repaired as necessary.

Life-Threatening Emergency — Employee Choice In a life-threatening situation where PPE cannot be obtained immediately (e.g., starting CPR without a one-way mask, applying pressure to a hemorrhaging wound), it is always the employee's choice whether to render assistance without full PPE. In such situations, employees should use whatever barrier protection is immediately available — such as regular safety glasses and work gloves — to provide some level of protection.
⚠5. Biohazard Labeling
☣

Biohazard Warning Labels Required

All containers for waste that may be contaminated with blood or body fluids must display a biohazard warning label. Red leak-proof bags must also be used as required for contaminated materials.

🧹6. Housekeeping After an Incident
  • Clean up after a first aid incident must be performed only by certified personnel from a qualified company to disinfect and remove contaminated materials.
  • Contaminated broken glassware must be picked up using mechanical means (tongs, forceps, or brush and dustpan) — never by hand.
  • Contaminated waste must be placed in closable, labeled, or color-coded containers and discarded according to federal, state, and local regulations.
  • Contaminated clothing must be handled as little as possible and with minimum agitation, then placed in color-coded bags and taken by a commercial launderer with appropriate warnings.
🎓7. Training Requirements

All employees who volunteer as first aid responders will receive training from the Safety Director or a qualified instructor. Training must cover:

  • The OSHA standard and its explanation.
  • Epidemiology and symptoms of bloodborne diseases.
  • Modes of transmission of bloodborne pathogens.
  • The Exposure Control Plan and how to obtain a copy.
  • Methods to recognize tasks and activities involving exposure risk.
  • Use and limitations of engineering controls, safe work practices, and PPE.
  • PPE types, use, location, removal, handling, decontamination, and disposal.
  • The Hepatitis B Vaccine — offered free of charge, with training on safety, effectiveness, and administration before vaccination.
  • Emergency procedures and post-exposure evaluation and follow-up.
Annual
Training is conducted initially on hiring or assignment, then annually — within one year of initial training — for all occupationally exposed employees.
💉8. Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) Vaccination
Free HBV Vaccine Offered to All First Responders Employees who volunteer to provide first aid, emergency response, or routine medical care are offered the HBV vaccination series at company expense — free of charge.
  • Employees transferring into a role that includes bloodborne pathogen exposure will be offered HBV vaccination within 10 working days of transfer.
  • The vaccine is not mandatory. If an employee declines the initial offer, they may request vaccination at any time in the future.
  • The company will document the offer, acceptance or declination, and vaccination dates in writing as required by OSHA.
  • If an employee is exposed to blood in an incident, the vaccine series must be offered and received within 24 hours of the incident if the employee desires it.
🏥9–12. Post-Exposure Evaluation & Follow-Up
If an Exposure Incident Occurs Contact the Safety Director immediately. The company's designated medical provider will conduct a confidential medical evaluation and follow-up.

The post-exposure evaluation will include:

  • Documenting the routes of exposure and how it occurred.
  • Identifying and documenting the source individual (unless prohibited by law).
  • Obtaining consent and testing the source individual's blood; documenting results.
  • Providing the exposed employee with the source individual's test results.
  • Collecting the exposed employee's blood as soon as feasible and testing for HBV and HIV.
  • If the employee does not consent to HIV testing at the time of blood collection, the baseline blood sample will be preserved for at least 90 days.
15 days
The healthcare professional's written opinion must be provided to the employee within 15 days after completion of the post-exposure evaluation.
Confidentiality Protected The written opinion is limited to whether the employee received or requires the vaccine and whether they were informed of the results. All other diagnoses must remain confidential and are not included in the written report.
🗄13. Recordkeeping
Medical Records
Kept for the duration of employment plus 30 years minimum. Includes name, SSN, job title, training dates, vaccination records, exam results, and all healthcare professional opinions.
Training Records
Maintained for 3 years from the date of training. Includes training date, curriculum, instructor name, and names/job titles of all trained employees.
Confidentiality Medical records are kept strictly confidential. They will not be disclosed without the employee's express written consent, except as required by OSHA or other laws. Records must be provided to the employee within 15 working days of a written request.

Supplemental Information:

  • Designated First Aid Providers: Foreman or Site Supervisor for each work location.
  • Medical Evaluations Performed By: Local physician selected for the project location.
  • Designated Healthcare Professional: Nearest Emergency Facility.
