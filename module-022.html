Ladder Safety Program

Selecting, inspecting, setting up, and safely climbing portable and fixed ladders — including the shell stairways and fixed ladder systems common on BBB Tank Services job sites.

⏱ Est. 30 min 📋 6 Sections ✅ 10-Question Quiz 📅 Rev. Oct 2025

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • State the leading causes of ladder-related fatalities and injuries
  • Identify each ANSI duty rating and its load capacity
  • Select the correct ladder type for a given task
  • Perform a pre-use inspection on a portable ladder
  • Set up an extension ladder using the 4:1 angle rule
  • Demonstrate three-point contact climbing technique
  • Identify fixed ladder safety requirements including cages and fall arrest

01 Ladder Hazards & OSHA Requirements

▼

Falls are the leading cause of death in construction and a top cause in general industry. Ladders are involved in a significant number of those falls. According to OSHA, nearly 24,000 workers are injured and over 300 are killed in ladder-related incidents every year in the United States. Most of these incidents are preventable through proper selection, inspection, setup, and use.

Leading Causes of Ladder Incidents

Worker Behavior

  • Overreaching — leaning too far to the side
  • Climbing with tools or materials in both hands
  • Standing on the top cap or top rung
  • Mounting the wrong side of a stepladder
  • Moving a ladder while someone is on it
  • Using a ladder in high winds or wet conditions

Equipment & Setup Problems

  • Wrong ladder type or duty rating for the job
  • Defective ladder put back into service
  • Extension ladder set at incorrect angle
  • Ladder not secured at top or bottom
  • Set up on unstable or uneven ground
  • Ladder too short — worker overreaches at top

OSHA Standards That Apply

StandardScope
29 CFR 1926.1053Construction — portable ladders; use, construction, and care
29 CFR 1910.23General Industry — ladders (updated 2017); portable and fixed ladders
29 CFR 1910.28 / 1926.502Fall protection — applies to fixed ladders over 24 ft and when climbing unprotected fixed ladders
ANSI/ASC A14 SeriesPerformance standards for portable metal, wood, reinforced plastic, and fixed ladders — basis for duty ratings
Ladders at BBB Tank Services: Our crews use portable extension ladders for tank access, stepladders for equipment maintenance at height, and climb fixed tank shell ladders and stairways during in-service and out-of-service tank work. Every ladder type presents distinct hazards. This module covers all three.

02 Ladder Types & Duty Ratings

ANSI Duty Ratings

All portable ladders are rated by ANSI for maximum load capacity, which includes the combined weight of the worker, tools, equipment, and materials on the ladder at one time. BBB Tank Services requires workers to know their duty rating and never exceed it.

When Not to Use a Ladder

A ladder is not always the right access solution. OSHA requires that when tasks require extended work at height, a more stable work platform is the better choice. Consider a scaffold or aerial lift instead of a ladder when:

  • The task requires more than a few minutes of work at height
  • Both hands are needed to perform the work (leaving no hand for the ladder)
  • The work area requires lateral movement along the work surface
  • Heavy tools or materials must be used or held at height
  • Wind conditions make ladder stability questionable
  • The work height exceeds what can be safely reached from a properly positioned ladder

03 Pre-Use Inspection

▼

Every portable ladder must be inspected before each use. An inspection takes less than two minutes and can prevent a fatal fall. If any defect is found, the ladder must be immediately removed from service, tagged “DO NOT USE,” and reported to your supervisor. Never use a defective ladder.

✅ Pre-Use Inspection Checklist — All Portable Ladders

Rails (Side Stringers)No cracks, dents, bends, or corrosion. No paint covering potential damage (paint obscures cracks on metal ladders).
Rungs & StepsAll rungs present, tight, not bent, cracked, or corroded. No slippery coatings (grease, mud, paint). Non-slip surfaces intact.
Feet & PadsRubber or non-slip feet present, not cracked, hardened, or missing. Feet swivel freely to contact the ground flat.
Locking Devices (Extension Ladders)Both rung locks (dogs/pawls) engage fully and lock positively. No bent or cracked lock components. Locks hold under light load test.
Spreaders (Stepladders)Both spreader arms fully open and locked. No bent, cracked, or missing spreader components. Spreader hinge rivets tight.
Labels & RatingsDuty rating label present and legible. Manufacturer’s warning labels intact. Do not use a ladder with missing or illegible rating label.
Overall ConditionNo evidence of prior overload (bent rails, spread rungs). No previous repairs using tape, wire, or improvised materials. No evidence of heat or chemical damage.

Remove-from-Service Criteria

Remove the ladder immediately and tag “DEFECTIVE — DO NOT USE” if any of the following are found:

  • Any cracked, bent, or broken rail or rung
  • Missing, loose, or non-functioning rung lock on an extension ladder
  • Missing, cracked, or hardened rubber feet
  • Evidence of prior overloading (bent rails, spread rungs, twisted frame)
  • Previous repair with tape, wire, bolts, or any improvised material
  • Missing or illegible duty rating label
  • A ladder that has been dropped from height or involved in an incident
⚠ Never Paint a Metal Ladder. Paint fills cracks and hides corrosion on aluminum and steel ladders, allowing hidden damage to go undetected during inspection. Metal ladders must remain unpainted. If you find a painted metal ladder, remove it from service and report it.

04 Setup & Positioning

▼

The 4:1 Rule — Extension Ladder Angle

The most critical setup requirement for an extension ladder is the angle of inclination. The correct angle is 75.5° from horizontal, established by the 4:1 rule: for every 4 feet of working height, the base of the ladder is set 1 foot out from the wall or structure.

4:1 Rule Illustrated

H
B

Formula: B = H ÷ 4

If the ladder reaches 16 feet up the wall, the base must be 4 feet out from the wall.

If the ladder reaches 20 feet, the base must be 5 feet out.

Too steep (base too close): ladder tips backward — falls away from structure.

Too shallow (base too far): ladder slides down the wall — feet kick out.

A quick field check: stand at the base with toes touching the feet, arms extended horizontally — if your palms just reach a rung, the angle is approximately correct.

Extension Ladder Setup — Step by Step

  1. 1
    Inspect before setup. Complete the pre-use inspection before carrying the ladder to the work location.
  2. 2
    Choose a stable, level surface. The feet must rest on a firm, level base. On soft ground, use a mud sill (board) under the feet. On hard floors, use a ladder mat or have a second person foot the ladder.
  3. 3
    Extend to the correct length. The ladder must extend at least 3 feet (3 rungs) above the landing point (roof edge, tank top, platform). The overlap between sections must meet the manufacturer’s minimum requirement (typically 3 ft for ladders up to 36 ft; more for longer ladders).
  4. 4
    Set the angle using the 4:1 rule. Position the base 1 foot out for every 4 feet of height. Confirm both rung locks are fully engaged before climbing.
  5. 5
    Secure the ladder. Tie off or lash the top of the ladder to the structure before climbing, whenever possible. If top tie-off is not possible before initial ascent, have a second person hold the base firmly during the first climb. After reaching the top, secure before descending.
  6. 6
    Position away from doors and traffic. Barricade or lock doors that open toward the ladder base. Post warning cones if the ladder is in a traffic area. Notify others that the ladder is in use.

Stepladder Setup

  • Open all four feet fully and lock all spreader arms before use — never use a stepladder in the closed position leaned against a wall
  • Place on a firm, level surface — all four feet must contact the ground
  • Never use on a scaffold, in a vehicle, or on any other elevated surface without additional fall protection
  • Do not place at the top of a staircase without using a specially designed staircase ladder leveler
⚠ Ladder Near Energized Equipment: Do not use a metal (aluminum) ladder near overhead electrical lines or energized equipment. Maintain at least 10 feet of clearance from energized lines up to 50 kV. Use a fiberglass ladder when any electrical hazard is present. BBB Tank Services job sites may have powered equipment on and around tank structures — look up before setting up.

05 Safe Climbing & Working Practices

▼

Three Points of Contact

The most important climbing rule is three points of contact at all times: two hands and one foot, or two feet and one hand must be in contact with the ladder during all ascent and descent. This gives you two independent support points if one contact slips or fails. Three points of contact is not optional — it is the fundamental ladder safety rule.

How to Carry Tools & Materials: If both hands are needed for three-point contact, tools and materials cannot be hand-carried while climbing. Use a tool belt, tool bucket, or hand line to raise and lower tools. Never carry an item that requires both hands to hold. Never carry an item so large or heavy that it throws off your balance.

Climbing Rules — Always and Never

Always Do

  • Face the ladder when climbing and descending
  • Maintain three points of contact at all times
  • Center your body between the rails — keep your belt buckle between the rails
  • Wear slip-resistant footwear
  • Climb and descend slowly and deliberately
  • Descend the same way you ascended — facing the ladder

Never Do

  • Never stand on the top cap or top two rungs of a stepladder
  • Never reach so far to the side that your belt buckle passes outside the rails
  • Never carry items in both hands while climbing
  • Never allow two people on a portable ladder simultaneously
  • Never climb on the back (bracing) side of a stepladder
  • Never jump from a ladder

Working from a Ladder

Ladders are a means of access, not a work platform. Extended work at height from a ladder is a leading cause of overreaching and falls. When working from a ladder:

  • Keep your center of gravity between the rails. Your hips and belt buckle must remain within the width of the ladder rails at all times. If you need to reach farther, climb down and move the ladder.
  • Do not work at arm’s length. If you cannot comfortably reach the work while maintaining three points of contact, the ladder is in the wrong position. Reposition.
  • Secure the ladder before working. If you will be performing work that requires both hands (drilling, wrench work), the ladder must be tied off at the top before you begin. Do not rely on a footing partner for extended work.
  • Limit task duration on a ladder. Fatigue increases fall risk. For tasks requiring more than 20–30 minutes of continuous work at height, use a scaffold or aerial lift.
Scenario — The Overreach

Six Inches Too Far

A crew member is on an extension ladder painting the shell of a tank. He is almost finished with a section but there is a spot just out of his comfortable reach about 18 inches to his right. Rather than climb down and move the ladder, he leans out to reach it, extending his right arm and shifting his weight. The ladder rotates slightly and he grabs for the rung with his left hand, dropping his brush. He catches himself but his heart is pounding.

What should have happened: The moment the work is beyond comfortable reach, stop — climb down and move the ladder. The few minutes to reposition a ladder is a trivial cost compared to a fall from even 10 feet. There is no “just this once” on a ladder. Overreaching is the number one cause of ladder falls and the cause is always the same: the worker decided the shortcut was worth it.

06 Fixed Ladders & Tank Shell Access

▼

Fixed ladders are permanently attached to structures — including tank shells, platforms, and elevated equipment. At BBB Tank Services job sites, fixed ladders on storage tanks are one of the most frequently used means of access. They present distinct hazards from portable ladders and are governed by specific OSHA requirements.

OSHA Requirements for Fixed Ladders

RequirementSpecifics
Cage or WellRequired on fixed ladders extending more than 20 feet above a lower level. Begins no more than 8 feet above the base and extends to the top of the ladder.
Rest PlatformsRequired at no more than 150-foot intervals on ladders exceeding 24 feet. Ladders are offset at each platform.
Personal Fall ArrestRequired on fixed ladders extending 24 feet or more that are not equipped with a cage or well. A ladder safety system (self-retracting lifeline or fixed vertical lifeline with sleeve) must be provided.
Ladder WidthMinimum 16 inches between side rails; rungs spaced uniformly 12 inches apart (±3/8 inch).
Landing Platform ClearanceAt least 7 inches of toe clearance from the centerline of rungs to any obstacle. Through-the-hole exit: at least 24 × 24 inch opening.

Inspecting a Fixed Ladder Before Use

Fixed ladders on customer tanks may not be routinely maintained by the customer. Before climbing any fixed ladder on a job site, perform a visual inspection from ground level and at each level of the climb:

  • Inspect mounting brackets — look for corrosion, loose bolts, or separation from the tank shell
  • Check rungs for corrosion, damage, or missing anti-slip treatment
  • Verify cage or fall arrest system is intact before ascending ladders >20 ft
  • Check that the self-retracting lifeline or vertical lifeline is present, undamaged, and correctly anchored if a fall arrest system is required
  • Inspect the top landing — confirm there is a safe step-off platform and adequate handrail at the transition point
  • If any structural deficiency is observed, do not use the ladder — report to your supervisor and the customer

Using a Ladder Safety System (LSS)

Many tall tank fixed ladders are equipped with a ladder safety system — either a fixed vertical lifeline with a climbing sleeve, or an integrated self-retracting lifeline (SRL) track. Using it correctly is mandatory before ascending any fixed ladder requiring one:

  1. 1
    Don your full body harness and confirm it is correctly fitted before approaching the ladder. Check all buckles, leg straps, and the dorsal D-ring.
  2. 2
    Connect the sleeve or SRL to your harness D-ring at the bottom of the ladder before ascending. Never connect after you have already started to climb.
  3. 3
    Keep the sleeve or SRL above you as you climb — it should always be at chest to shoulder level, not below your feet. Letting it fall below you creates slack that converts a slip into a longer, harder fall arrest.
  4. 4
    Do not detach at intermediate rest platforms. Transfer to the next ladder section’s LSS before releasing from the current one — maintain continuous protection.
  5. 5
    Disconnect only at the designated top landing. Never detach from the LSS while still on the ladder, even to reposition.
⚠ Never Climb a Fixed Ladder Without Its Required Fall Protection. If a fixed ladder requires a cage, safety system, or fall arrest equipment and that equipment is missing, damaged, or non-functional, the ladder is out of service until repaired. Using an unprotected fixed ladder >24 feet is an OSHA violation and a potentially fatal decision. Report missing or damaged fixed ladder safety equipment to your supervisor and the facility immediately.

Tank Stairways vs. Ladders

Many larger storage tanks are equipped with spiral stairways rather than vertical fixed ladders. Stairways are always preferred over ladders for tank access when both are available. When using tank stairways:

  • Use handrails on both sides if available; grip the rail continuously
  • Do not run on tank stairways — open grating can catch boot toes
  • Check for wet, icy, or product-contaminated grating before stepping
  • Do not carry items in both hands on a stairway — use a tool bag
  • On floating roof tanks, be aware that stairways may articulate or pivot as the roof level changes
