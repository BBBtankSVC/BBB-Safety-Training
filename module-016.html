01 How Noise Damages Hearing

▼

Noise-induced hearing loss (NIHL) is one of the most common occupational injuries in the United States — and one of the most preventable. Unlike a broken bone, hearing loss is permanent and irreversible. There is no surgery or medication that restores noise-damaged hearing. Once it is gone, it is gone.

The Mechanism of Noise Damage

Inside the inner ear (cochlea) are thousands of microscopic hair cells that convert sound vibrations into nerve signals sent to the brain. These hair cells cannot regenerate. Excessive noise physically destroys them — bending, breaking, or tearing them from the basilar membrane. Early damage tends to occur in the 4,000 Hz frequency range, which is the range most important for understanding speech clearly.

⚠ Noise Damage Is Cumulative and Painless. Hearing loss builds up over years of repeated exposure without any warning pain. By the time a worker notices they are struggling to hear conversations, significant permanent damage has already occurred. Tinnitus (constant ringing or buzzing in the ears) is often the first sign — and it is usually permanent.

Symptoms of Noise-Induced Hearing Loss

Early Signs (Often Ignored)

  • Ringing, buzzing, or hissing in ears after work (tinnitus)
  • Muffled sounds for a few hours after a loud shift (temporary threshold shift)
  • Needing to turn up the TV or phone volume
  • Difficulty understanding speech in background noise
  • Asking people to repeat themselves frequently

Advanced Signs

  • Permanent high-pitched ringing (chronic tinnitus)
  • Inability to understand conversations without lip-reading
  • Difficulty hearing high-frequency sounds (birds, alarms, children’s voices)
  • Audiogram showing a notch at 4,000 Hz — the “NIHL notch”
  • Social withdrawal due to communication difficulty

02 OSHA Noise Standards & Exposure Limits

▼

OSHA regulates occupational noise exposure under 29 CFR 1910.95 (General Industry) and 29 CFR 1926.52 (Construction). These standards establish the maximum noise doses workers may receive and trigger requirements for hearing conservation programs.

Key Exposure Limits

LimitLevelWhat It Triggers
Action Level (AL) 85 dBA as an 8-hour TWA Enrollment in Hearing Conservation Program; noise monitoring; audiometric testing; hearing protection made available (mandatory use begins at PEL)
Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL) 90 dBA as an 8-hour TWA Mandatory engineering and administrative controls; mandatory hearing protection; feasible noise reduction required
Maximum Instantaneous Exposure 140 dBC peak sound pressure Never to be exceeded, even for an instant — applies to impact and impulse noise (gunshots, explosions, hammering on steel)

The 5-dB Exchange Rate (Time-Dose Relationship)

OSHA uses a 5 dB exchange rate: for every 5 dB increase in noise level, the permissible exposure time is cut in half. Starting from the 8-hour PEL of 90 dBA:

Sound Level (dBA)Maximum Exposure Time (Unprotected)
90 dBA8 hours
95 dBA4 hours
100 dBA2 hours
105 dBA1 hour
110 dBA30 minutes
115 dBA15 minutes (maximum, regardless)
NIOSH vs. OSHA Exchange Rate: NIOSH (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health) recommends a stricter 3 dB exchange rate and a 85 dBA PEL. Under NIOSH, exposure time halves with every 3 dB increase — meaning 88 dBA = 4 hours, 91 dBA = 2 hours, etc. BBB Tank Services follows the OSHA standard for compliance purposes but recommends workers treat NIOSH guidelines as best practice.

Time-Weighted Average (TWA) & Noise Dose

Because workers are exposed to different noise levels throughout a shift, OSHA uses a time-weighted average (TWA) to calculate total noise dose for the day. A noise dose of 100% equals the OSHA PEL. Exposures above 50% dose (the Action Level) trigger HCP requirements. Personal dosimeters worn on the worker’s collar collect this data during a full work shift.

⚠ Inside Tanks = Higher Exposure. Metal tank interiors reflect and amplify noise. A tool that measures 95 dBA in open air may measure 100–105 dBA inside a tank due to reverberant sound buildup. Always assume elevated noise exposure when working inside any metal-walled confined space.

03 Noise Monitoring & Audiometric Testing

▼

Noise Monitoring

When employee exposures may equal or exceed the Action Level (85 dBA TWA), BBB Tank Services is required to conduct noise monitoring to measure actual exposure levels. Monitoring results determine which employees must be enrolled in the Hearing Conservation Program.

Sound Level Meter (SLM)

  • Handheld device that measures noise level at a specific location and time
  • Used for area surveys and to identify noisy equipment
  • Good for comparing before/after engineering controls
  • Does not capture the worker’s full-shift exposure

Personal Noise Dosimeter

  • Worn on the worker’s collar near the ear for a full shift
  • Continuously records noise exposure and calculates TWA and dose %
  • Most accurate method for determining individual worker exposure
  • Required when exposures may exceed the Action Level

Audiometric Testing (Hearing Tests)

Audiometric testing measures a worker’s hearing ability across a range of frequencies. It is the only way to detect early noise-induced hearing loss before it becomes severe. Under OSHA 1910.95, audiometric testing is required for all employees whose noise exposures equal or exceed the Action Level.

TestWhen RequiredPurpose
Baseline Audiogram Within 6 months of first enrollment in the HCP (or within 1 year if a mobile test van is used) Establishes a baseline hearing threshold to compare all future tests against. Must be preceded by 14 hours of quiet (no noise exposure) before the test.
Annual Audiogram At least once per year while enrolled in the HCP Detects changes in hearing compared to the baseline. Identifies Standard Threshold Shifts (STS) that may indicate noise damage.
Revised Baseline When an STS is confirmed or when hearing improves Updated baseline used for future comparisons

Standard Threshold Shift (STS)

An STS occurs when a worker’s hearing worsens by an average of 10 dB or more at 2,000, 3,000, and 4,000 Hz in either ear compared to their baseline audiogram. When an STS is detected:

  • BBB Tank Services must notify the affected employee in writing within 21 days
  • The employee’s hearing protector use is evaluated — a higher-attenuation protector may be required
  • The employee is referred for additional audiological evaluation if the STS is work-related
  • The STS may be recordable on the OSHA 300 Log if it is work-related and meets severity criteria
✓ Your Right to Audiogram Results: You are entitled to a copy of your audiogram results. You also have the right to consult with a qualified audiologist or physician about your results. Ask your supervisor if you have questions about your hearing test.

04 Engineering & Administrative Controls

▼

OSHA’s hierarchy of controls requires that engineering and administrative controls be used to reduce noise exposure to the PEL before relying on hearing protection. Hearing protection is the last line of defense — not the first solution.

Engineering Controls

Engineering controls physically reduce the amount of noise generated or the amount that reaches the worker’s ear. They are the most effective and permanent solutions:

Noise Reduction at the Source

  • Selecting lower-noise tools and equipment where possible (e.g., electric vs. pneumatic)
  • Installing mufflers or silencers on pneumatic exhaust ports
  • Maintaining equipment — worn bearings, loose panels, and damaged components all add noise
  • Using rubber-lined or coated hoppers and chutes to reduce impact noise
  • Vibration isolation mounts on compressors and generators

Noise Reduction at the Path

  • Noise barriers and enclosures around compressors and generators
  • Distance — noise decreases by 6 dB for every doubling of distance from the source
  • Sound-absorbing materials inside tank enclosures where feasible
  • Remote positioning of loud equipment away from workers

Administrative Controls

Administrative controls reduce exposure by limiting time workers spend in noisy areas:

  • Job rotation — rotating workers through noisy tasks limits individual dose accumulation
  • Scheduling — performing the loudest operations when fewer workers are present
  • Quiet zones — designated rest areas away from noise where workers can reduce dose during breaks
  • Posting noise hazard areas — marking areas where noise exceeds 85 dBA so workers know to don hearing protection before entering
  • Remote operation — operating loud equipment from a distance using extended controls or remote systems
The 6 dB Rule: Doubling the distance between a worker and a noise source reduces exposure by approximately 6 dB. Moving from 5 feet to 10 feet away from a generator running at 100 dBA reduces exposure to roughly 94 dBA. This is a simple, no-cost administrative control.
Scenario — Controls in Action

The Noisy Generator

A portable generator powering a work site produces 100 dBA at the work position. A crew member will be working within 5 feet of it for 6 hours. At 100 dBA, the OSHA limit is 2 hours — this exposure is three times the limit.

Engineering control: Move the generator 40 feet away and run a longer power cable. At 40 feet, exposure drops to approximately 88 dBA — below the PEL for 8 hours. Administrative control: If relocation is not possible, rotate workers every 2 hours to limit individual dose. PPE as backup: Workers near the generator wear dual hearing protection (earplugs plus earmuffs) regardless of controls applied.

05 Hearing Protection Devices

▼

When engineering and administrative controls do not bring exposure below the PEL, or while those controls are being implemented, Hearing Protection Devices (HPDs) must be worn. BBB Tank Services requires HPD use in all areas posted as hearing protection zones and whenever workers use or are near the equipment identified in Section 1.

Types of Hearing Protection

Foam Earplugs (Disposable)

NRR: 25–33 dB
  • Most common and widely available
  • Must be rolled, inserted correctly, and allowed to expand
  • Ineffective when inserted incorrectly — proper technique is critical
  • Single-use; replace daily or when soiled
  • Not suitable for frequent insertion/removal during the shift

Pre-Molded Earplugs (Reusable)

NRR: 22–30 dB
  • Made of silicone or rubber; washable and reusable
  • Consistent fit — good for frequent removal during the shift
  • Must be fitted to individual ear canal size
  • Keep on a cord to prevent loss
  • Inspect regularly for hardening or deformation

Earmuffs

NRR: 20–30 dB
  • Hard cups with cushioned seals worn over the ears
  • Easier to put on and take off correctly than earplugs
  • Seal quality degrades with facial hair, glasses, or hard hat straps breaking the seal
  • Must be compatible with hard hat attachment
  • Inspect cushions — replace when hardened or cracked

Dual Protection (Plugs + Muffs)

NRR: Combined
  • Required when noise levels exceed 105 dBA
  • Combined NRR is NOT simply additive
  • OSHA method: take the higher NRR and add 5 dB
  • Provides maximum attenuation for highest-risk operations
  • Used during needle scaling, high-pressure water blasting, and confined space demolition

Understanding NRR — Noise Reduction Rating

Every hearing protector is tested and given a Noise Reduction Rating (NRR) measured in decibels. However, the NRR is measured under ideal laboratory conditions. In the real world, workers often insert earplugs incorrectly or have imperfect seal on earmuffs. OSHA requires a derating to account for this.

OSHA Derating Formula:
Effective Protection (dB) = (NRR − 7) ÷ 2

Example: Foam earplugs with NRR 29:
Effective protection = (29 − 7) ÷ 2 = 11 dB of real-world attenuation

If the noise level is 100 dBA and your earplugs provide 11 dB effective attenuation, your protected exposure is approximately 89 dBA — just below the OSHA PEL. This is why correct insertion technique is essential.

Correct Foam Earplug Insertion — Step by Step

  1. 1
    Wash your hands. Dirty hands contaminate the earplug and introduce bacteria into the ear canal, causing ear infections.
  2. 2
    Roll the earplug into a tight, thin cylinder. Roll between your fingers until it is compressed to roughly half its normal diameter. No creases.
  3. 3
    Pull the top of your ear up and back. Use your opposite hand to reach over your head and pull the ear upward and backward. This straightens the ear canal for a deep insertion.
  4. 4
    Insert the rolled earplug deeply into the canal. Push it in until it sits deep — not just at the entrance. Hold it in place for 20–30 seconds while it expands to fill the canal.
  5. 5
    Check the fit. Cup your hands over your ears and release. If sounds become noticeably more muffled when cupping, the earplugs are not inserted deeply enough — reinsert them.
⚠ Common Earplug Mistakes: Inserting only halfway (the most common error), not rolling small enough before inserting, not holding in place during expansion, and wearing earplugs with dirty hands. Any of these can reduce actual attenuation by 50% or more, turning a 29-NRR earplug into the equivalent of a 15-NRR protector.

06 BBB Tank Services Hearing Conservation Program

▼

Program Requirements

BBB Tank Services maintains a written Hearing Conservation Program in compliance with OSHA 29 CFR 1910.95. The program applies to all employees whose noise exposures equal or exceed the 85 dBA Action Level during any work shift. Key program elements:

Program ElementBBB Tank Services Requirement
Noise MonitoringConducted when exposures may reach the Action Level; repeated when work processes, equipment, or controls change significantly
Audiometric TestingBaseline within 6 months of enrollment; annual testing thereafter; follow-up testing when STS is detected
Hearing ProtectionAvailable to all employees at or above Action Level; mandatory at or above the PEL (90 dBA) and in all posted hearing protection zones
TrainingAnnual training for all HCP participants covering noise effects, exposure limits, HPD selection and use, and audiometric testing
RecordkeepingAudiometric test records retained for duration of employment; noise exposure records retained for 2 years; available to employees on request

Hearing Protection Zones at BBB Tank Services

Areas or operations requiring mandatory hearing protection are identified with orange-and-black “Hearing Protection Required” signs. You must don hearing protection before entering a posted zone — not after you are already inside. Common mandatory HPD zones include:

  • All areas where needle scalers, pneumatic chippers, or water blasters are in operation
  • Interior of tanks during any powered tool use (reverberant noise amplification)
  • Within 25 feet of operating generators, compressors, or vacuum trucks
  • All grinding and cutting operations
  • Any area posted with a hearing protection sign, regardless of perceived noise level

Your Responsibilities

  • Wear hearing protection whenever required — in posted zones, during designated operations, and at any time noise becomes uncomfortable
  • Attend your annual audiometric test during company-scheduled testing. Missing your test means an undetected shift in your hearing may go unaddressed
  • Inspect your hearing protectors before each use. Hardened or cracked earplug cushions, damaged earmuff cups, or stretched headbands reduce protection significantly
  • Report damaged or lost hearing protectors to your supervisor immediately. Never go without protection because yours are damaged — replacements are available at no cost
  • Report symptoms — if you notice ringing in your ears after a shift, muffled hearing, or difficulty understanding speech, report it to your supervisor and request a hearing evaluation
✓ Hearing Protection Is Free: BBB Tank Services provides hearing protection at no cost to all enrolled employees. You may request your preferred type of protector (foam plugs, pre-molded plugs, or earmuffs). You may also request a hearing protector fitting and insertion training session from your safety representative at any time.
Scenario — Know When to Act

The Ringing After the Shift

After a long day of needle scaling inside a crude oil storage tank, a crew member notices a persistent high-pitched ringing in both ears on the drive home. It is still there when he wakes up the next morning. He figures it will go away and does not mention it. Three months later, his annual audiogram shows a Standard Threshold Shift in both ears at 4,000 Hz.

What should have happened: Ringing in the ears after a work shift — even temporary — is a warning sign of noise overexposure and possible temporary threshold shift. He should have reported the symptoms to his supervisor that day. An immediate evaluation could have confirmed the HPDs were not being inserted correctly, triggering retraining and preventing cumulative damage. Early reporting gives the program a chance to intervene before the STS becomes permanent.
