01 What Is HazCom & Why It Matters

▼

OSHA’s Hazard Communication Standard (HCS), found at 29 CFR 1910.1200 (General Industry) and 29 CFR 1926.59 (Construction), is commonly known as “HazCom” or the “Right-to-Know Law.” It requires chemical manufacturers and employers to communicate chemical hazard information clearly so that workers can protect themselves before, during, and after chemical exposure.

In 2012, OSHA aligned the HCS with the Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals (GHS) — an international standard that standardizes how hazards are communicated using consistent labels and Safety Data Sheets worldwide. This means a BBB Tank Services employee who can read a GHS label can understand the hazards of a chemical regardless of where it was manufactured.

The Three Pillars of HazCom:
  • Labels — on every container of hazardous chemical in the workplace
  • Safety Data Sheets (SDS) — detailed technical documents for every hazardous chemical
  • Training — employees trained to understand labels and SDS before working with chemicals

Chemicals at BBB Tank Services Job Sites

BBB Tank Services employees are regularly exposed to a wide range of chemical hazards during tank cleaning, inspection, and maintenance. These include:

Residual Tank Substances

  • Crude oil and petroleum products
  • Gasoline, diesel, jet fuel
  • Asphalt and heavy fuel oils
  • Produced water and brine
  • Hydrogen sulfide (H&sub2;S) in vapor

Cleaning & Treatment Chemicals

  • Caustic soda (sodium hydroxide)
  • Acid cleaners (hydrochloric, citric)
  • Degreasers and solvents
  • Chemical neutralizers
  • Rust inhibitors and coatings

Operational Products

  • Lubricants and hydraulic fluids
  • Welding gases (oxygen, acetylene, argon)
  • Battery acid
  • Paints, primers, and coatings
  • Adhesives and sealants
✓ Your Rights Under HazCom: You have the legal right to know the identity and hazards of every chemical you work with. You have the right to access the SDS for any chemical at your job site before you work with it — at any time, on any shift. You cannot be disciplined for requesting this information. If you cannot locate an SDS, stop work and notify your supervisor.

02 GHS Chemical Labels

▼

Every container of hazardous chemical — from the original manufacturer’s container to a smaller transferred container — must bear a GHS-compliant label. GHS labels must contain exactly six required elements. Knowing these elements lets you quickly assess any chemical hazard before you open the container.

The 6 Required GHS Label Elements
Element 1
Product Identifier The chemical name, code, or batch number that matches the SDS. Must be the same identifier used on the SDS.
Element 2
Signal Word Either DANGER (more severe hazard) or WARNING (less severe hazard). Only one signal word appears per label.
Element 3
Hazard Statements Standard phrases describing the nature and degree of the hazard. Example: “Causes serious eye damage.” or “May cause cancer.”
Element 4
Precautionary Statements Recommended measures to minimize or prevent adverse effects. Covers: Prevention, Response, Storage, and Disposal.
Element 5
Pictogram(s) One or more GHS hazard pictograms — black symbols in a red diamond border — that visually communicate the type of hazard.
Element 6
Supplier Information Name, address, and phone number of the chemical manufacturer, importer, or distributor. This is where you call in an emergency.

Signal Words

DANGER More severe hazard category
Used for the highest hazard levels
Take all precautions immediately
WARNING Less severe hazard category
Used for moderate hazard levels
Precautions still required

Workplace Labels & Secondary Containers

When a chemical is transferred from its original container into a smaller container for use (a spray bottle, a bucket, a drum), that secondary container must also be labeled. The label must include at minimum:

  • The product identifier (chemical name)
  • The hazard information (at minimum: signal word and hazard statements, or the full label)
⚠ Never Transfer to an Unlabeled Container. Unlabeled containers are an OSHA violation and a serious hazard. Coworkers on the next shift may not know what is in an unlabeled container. Never use food or drink containers to store chemicals — even temporarily.

03 GHS Pictograms

▼

GHS pictograms are standardized symbols printed inside a red diamond border. There are nine GHS pictograms. You must be able to recognize each one and understand what hazard it communicates. A single chemical may display multiple pictograms if it presents multiple hazard types.

🔥 Flame Flammable, self-reactive, pyrophoric, self-heating, emits flammable gas, organic peroxides
🔴 Flame Over Circle Oxidizers — substances that intensify fire; do not store near flammables
💥 Exploding Bomb Explosives, self-reactive substances, organic peroxides that can detonate or deflagrate
☢️ Skull & Crossbones Acute toxicity (fatal or toxic) — can cause death or serious injury from a single exposure
☣️ Corrosion Corrosive to skin, eyes, or metals — causes irreversible tissue destruction on contact
⚠️ Exclamation Mark Irritant, skin sensitizer, harmful, narcotic effects, respiratory irritant, hazardous to ozone layer
🏥 Health Hazard Carcinogen, respiratory sensitizer, reproductive toxin, target organ toxin, aspiration hazard
⛈️ Environment Aquatic toxicity (acute or chronic) — harmful to fish, invertebrates, or aquatic ecosystems
💧 Gas Cylinder Gases under pressure — compressed, liquefied, dissolved, or refrigerated liquefied gases
Memorization Tip: Group the pictograms by hazard type: Physical hazards (Flame, Flame over Circle, Exploding Bomb, Gas Cylinder) — Health hazards (Skull & Crossbones, Health Hazard, Exclamation Mark) — Reactive/Corrosive hazards (Corrosion) — Environmental hazards (Environment).

Pictograms Common in Tank Services Work

Given the nature of BBB Tank Services operations, the pictograms you will encounter most often are:

  • Flame — on petroleum products, solvents, and flammable cleaning chemicals
  • Skull & Crossbones — on concentrated acids and caustics; certain industrial solvents
  • Corrosion — on caustic soda (NaOH), hydrochloric acid, and battery acid
  • Health Hazard — on petroleum distillates (benzene content), many solvents and coatings
  • Exclamation Mark — on degreasers, lubricants, and mild irritants
  • Gas Cylinder — on compressed gas cylinders (oxygen, acetylene, argon, nitrogen)

04 Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

▼

A Safety Data Sheet (SDS) is a standardized technical document that provides comprehensive information about a hazardous chemical. Under GHS, all SDS are organized into exactly 16 sections in the same order, regardless of manufacturer. This standardization means that once you know the 16-section format, you can find critical information on any SDS quickly — even in an emergency.

✓ Where to Find SDS at BBB Tank Services: SDS must be accessible to all employees on every shift without having to ask a supervisor. BBB Tank Services maintains SDS in both digital format (accessible on company tablets/laptops at the job site) and in a physical binder kept in the crew truck or job site trailer. Know where the binder is before you start work.

The 16 SDS Sections

1
IdentificationProduct name, uses, manufacturer contact info
2
Hazard(s) IdentificationGHS classification, signal word, pictograms, hazard & precautionary statements
3
Composition / IngredientsChemical identity, CAS numbers, concentration ranges
4
First-Aid MeasuresSymptoms and treatment by route of exposure (skin, eyes, inhalation, ingestion)
5
Fire-Fighting MeasuresSuitable extinguishants, special hazards, protective equipment for firefighters
6
Accidental Release MeasuresSpill containment, cleanup procedures, environmental precautions
7
Handling & StorageSafe use precautions, incompatible materials, storage conditions
8
Exposure Controls / PPEOSHA PEL, NIOSH REL, ACGIH TLV, required PPE, ventilation needs
9
Physical & Chemical PropertiesAppearance, odor, pH, boiling point, flash point, flammability limits
10
Stability & ReactivityConditions to avoid, incompatible materials, hazardous decomposition products
11
Toxicological InformationRoutes of exposure, acute & chronic health effects, carcinogenicity, LD50/LC50
12
Ecological InformationEnvironmental fate, aquatic toxicity, persistence and biodegradability
13
Disposal ConsiderationsWaste disposal methods, regulatory classification
14
Transport InformationUN number, proper shipping name, hazard class, packing group
15
Regulatory InformationSARA, CERCLA, state regulations, inventory listings
16
Other InformationRevision date, changes from previous version, preparer information

The Four Sections You Must Know Cold

In an emergency or before starting a job, four SDS sections give you the most critical information:

SectionWhen You Use ItWhat You Need From It
Section 2 — Hazard IdentificationBefore any chemical work beginsThe GHS hazard class, signal word, and all hazard & precautionary statements. Tells you what can happen and what to do to prevent it.
Section 4 — First AidImmediately after an exposureSpecific first-aid steps for each route of exposure: skin contact, eye contact, inhalation, and ingestion. Share with emergency responders.
Section 6 — Accidental ReleaseDuring a spill or leakHow to contain and clean up the spill, personal protective precautions during cleanup, and environmental notifications required.
Section 8 — Exposure Controls / PPEBefore starting work with the chemicalWhat PPE is required (gloves, respirator, eye protection), exposure limits (OSHA PEL, NIOSH REL), and ventilation requirements.
Scenario — Using an SDS in the Field

Chemical Spill During Tank Cleaning

Your crew is using a caustic degreaser to clean the interior of a fuel storage tank. A hose fitting fails and approximately 5 gallons of concentrate spills on the tank floor. A crew member’s arms and legs are splashed. What do you do?

Step 1: Have the exposed worker move to fresh air and begin flushing the affected skin with large amounts of water immediately — do not wait to read the SDS first for skin/eye contact. Step 2: Retrieve the SDS for the degreaser. Go to Section 4 (First Aid) for specific decontamination instructions and Section 6 (Accidental Release) for spill cleanup steps. Step 3: Call 911 if Section 4 indicates medical attention is needed for caustic exposure — concentrated caustic soda can cause serious chemical burns that may not be immediately painful. Section 8 tells the cleanup crew what PPE they need before entering the spill area.

05 Chemical Hazard Classification

▼

Under GHS, chemicals are classified into hazard classes (the type of hazard) and hazard categories (the severity within that class). Category 1 is always the most severe; higher category numbers indicate lower severity. This is the opposite of some older rating systems — be careful not to confuse them.

Major Hazard Classes

Physical Hazards

  • Flammable liquids (Cat. 1–4 by flash point)
  • Flammable gases
  • Oxidizing liquids and solids
  • Explosives
  • Compressed gases
  • Self-reactive substances
  • Pyrophoric liquids and solids

Health Hazards

  • Acute toxicity (oral, dermal, inhalation)
  • Skin corrosion/irritation
  • Serious eye damage/irritation
  • Respiratory or skin sensitization
  • Germ cell mutagenicity
  • Carcinogenicity
  • Reproductive toxicity
  • Target organ toxicity (single/repeated)

Flammable Liquid Categories — Relevant to Tank Work

⚠ GHS vs. HMIS/NFPA: Older chemical labeling systems like HMIS (Hazardous Materials Identification System) and NFPA 704 “Fire Diamond” use a 0–4 numerical scale where 4 = most hazardous. GHS uses Category 1 = most hazardous. You may encounter both systems at customer facilities. Know the difference so you do not misread a hazard level.

Exposure Routes — How Chemicals Enter the Body

Understanding how a chemical can enter the body helps you select the right protective equipment:

  • Inhalation — breathing vapors, gases, mists, fumes, or dusts. The most common route of occupational exposure. Respiratory protection addresses this route.
  • Skin absorption — some chemicals (e.g., benzene, many pesticides) pass through intact skin. Gloves and coveralls address this route.
  • Skin/eye contact — corrosives, irritants, and sensitizers cause local damage on contact. Chemical splash goggles and face shields address this route.
  • Ingestion — swallowing chemicals, often from contaminated hands. Prevented by washing hands before eating, drinking, or using tobacco, and by not eating in chemical work areas.

06 Your Responsibilities & BBB Tank Services HazCom Program

▼

What BBB Tank Services Is Required to Provide

  • A written HazCom program available for review by any employee at any time
  • An up-to-date chemical inventory listing all hazardous chemicals in the workplace
  • An SDS for every hazardous chemical on the inventory, accessible on all shifts
  • GHS-compliant labels on all containers of hazardous chemicals
  • HazCom training at the time of initial assignment and when new chemical hazards are introduced

What You Are Required to Do

  • Read the label on every chemical container before opening or using it. Do not skip this step even for chemicals you use regularly — formulations change.
  • Locate and review the SDS before working with any chemical you are unfamiliar with, or before any task with significant exposure potential.
  • Use the required PPE specified in Section 8 of the SDS and on the label precautionary statements.
  • Never remove or deface a label from any chemical container. Unlabeled containers are a hazard and an OSHA violation.
  • Label all secondary containers when transferring chemicals from original containers.
  • Report missing or damaged labels and missing SDS to your supervisor immediately.
  • Report any chemical exposure — skin contact, splash, or inhalation — to your supervisor and seek first aid or medical attention per the SDS.

Before Starting Any Chemical Task — Pre-Job Steps

  1. 1
    Identify the chemical. Read the product identifier on the label. Make sure you have the right product for the job and confirm you understand what it is.
  2. 2
    Review the label. Note the signal word, pictograms, hazard statements, and precautionary statements. What hazards are present? What should you avoid?
  3. 3
    Locate and review the SDS. Focus on Sections 2, 4, 6, and 8. Confirm you know: what PPE is required, what to do if exposed, and how to respond to a spill.
  4. 4
    Don required PPE before opening or handling the chemical. PPE goes on before exposure occurs — not after.
  5. 5
    Verify ventilation and emergency equipment. Confirm there is adequate ventilation. Know where the nearest eyewash station, safety shower, and fire extinguisher are located before starting.
⚠ Non-Routine Tasks and New Chemicals: If you are asked to work with a chemical you have never used before, or to perform a non-routine task involving chemical exposure, you have the right — and the responsibility — to review the SDS and receive adequate training before beginning work. Do not allow time pressure to bypass this step. A chemical exposure that causes injury or illness cannot be undone after the fact.

Chemical Exposure — Immediate Actions

Skin or Eye Contact

  • Remove contaminated clothing immediately
  • Flush with large amounts of water for 15–20 minutes
  • For eye contact: use the eyewash station; hold eyelids open and flush continuously
  • Do not rub eyes or skin
  • Notify supervisor and refer to SDS Section 4
  • Seek medical evaluation for any corrosive or sensitizer exposure

Inhalation Exposure

  • Remove to fresh air immediately
  • Position conscious victim sitting upright
  • Loosen tight clothing around the chest
  • Call 911 for significant exposures or any loss of consciousness
  • Provide SDS to emergency responders
  • Do not re-enter the area without proper respiratory protection
