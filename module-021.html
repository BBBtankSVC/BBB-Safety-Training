01 Hazards, Regulations & Qualified Persons

▼

Crane and rigging operations are among the most hazardous activities in industrial work. A suspended load that falls, swings, or drops can kill instantly — and the consequences extend beyond the rigging crew to anyone in the surrounding area. At BBB Tank Services, lifting operations occur frequently: setting pump housings, moving manway covers, positioning heavy equipment for tank work, and coordinating crane lifts for tank components.

Leading Causes of Crane & Rigging Fatalities

Load-Related Causes

  • Overloading — exceeding the crane or rigging WLL
  • Load shifting or slipping from improperly rigged slings
  • Two-blocking (hook meets drum — line snaps)
  • Boom collapse from side loading or overloading
  • Load swing striking workers or structures

Operational Causes

  • Contact with overhead power lines (electrocution)
  • Workers in the load path or under suspended load
  • Crane upset / tip-over from soft ground or improper outrigger setup
  • Defective rigging hardware (worn slings, cracked shackles)
  • Communication failure between rigger and operator

Applicable Standards

Qualified Rigger & Signal Person Requirements

OSHA 1926.1401 defines specific qualification requirements for personnel involved in crane operations:

Qualified Rigger

  • Must be a qualified person based on training, knowledge, and experience in rigging equipment and operations
  • Understands sling types, hardware, load angles, and rigging configurations
  • Can identify unsafe conditions and knows the load limits of all rigging used
  • Required when making or releasing rigging attachments for crane lifts

Signal Person

  • Qualification required when the operator cannot see the load, landing area, or rigging crew
  • Must know and use standard hand signals or agreed-upon signals
  • Only one designated signal person directs the operator at any time
  • Signal persons must be evaluated by a third-party qualified evaluator or by the employer’s own qualified evaluator
⚠ Power Line Contact — The Leading Crane Fatality Cause. Contact with energized overhead power lines accounts for more crane-related deaths than any other cause. Before any crane or boom truck operates on a BBB Tank Services job site, overhead electrical lines must be identified. If the crane or its load could contact a line, the utility must de-energize it, or minimum safe clearance distances must be established and enforced. The minimum clearance for lines up to 50 kV is 10 feet. More clearance is always better.

02 Slings — Types, Inspection & Removal Criteria

▼

Slings are the primary connection between the crane hook and the load. Selecting the correct sling type, verifying its Working Load Limit (WLL), inspecting it before every use, and removing it from service when damaged are the most critical rigging skills a BBB Tank Services worker can have.

Sling Types Used at BBB Tank Services

TypeConstructionStrengthsLimitations
Wire Rope Sling Multiple strands of steel wire twisted together; available with eyes, hooks, or shackle ends High strength-to-weight ratio; resistant to abrasion and heat; durable in harsh industrial environments Heavy; can damage soft or finished surfaces; stiffness limits conformation to irregular loads; must be inspected carefully for broken wires
Chain Sling Alloy steel chain links with master link and hooks; available in grades 80 and 100 Excellent for high-temperature lifts; adjustable length; highly resistant to abrasion and cutting; durable for rough service Heaviest sling type; can damage soft surfaces; must be Grade 80 or Grade 100 alloy only — never use hardware store or transport chain for lifting
Synthetic Web Sling Woven nylon or polyester webbing with sewn eyes; flat or tubular Lightweight; does not damage finished surfaces; flexible and easy to rig around irregular shapes; absorbs shock loads Susceptible to cuts, abrasion, chemical damage, and UV degradation; lower working temperature limit (<180°F); inspect for cuts in the webbing surface
Round Sling (Endless) Continuous nylon or polyester core wrapped in a woven cover; color-coded by capacity Extremely flexible; distributes load over a wider bearing surface; gentle on delicate loads; lightweight and easy to handle Core damage may not be visible through the cover — use a round sling cover inspection light; sensitive to abrasion and cuts; lower temperature limits

Wire Rope Sling — Removal from Service Criteria

Inspect wire rope slings before every use. Remove from service immediately if any of the following are found:

  • 10 randomly distributed broken wires in one rope lay, or 5 broken wires in one strand within one lay
  • Kinking, crushing, bird-caging, or any other distortion of the rope structure
  • Heat damage — discoloration, loss of lubrication, or fused wires
  • Corrosion of the wire rope or end fittings
  • Cracked, deformed, or worn end fittings (hooks, thimbles, swaged sleeves)
  • Any reduction in rope diameter of more than 1/3 of the original diameter
  • Missing or illegible tag (capacity tag must be attached and readable)

Synthetic Sling — Removal from Service Criteria

  • Acid or caustic burns; heat damage or melting
  • Holes, tears, cuts, or snags exposing core yarns
  • Broken or worn stitching in the sling body
  • Distortion of the metal fittings (eyes, hooks)
  • Missing or illegible identification tag
  • Evidence of UV degradation (chalky, brittle surface texture)
⚠ Never Use a Sling Without a Legible Tag. The identification tag on a sling contains its rated capacity, size, and material. A sling without a readable tag is an unknown capacity — remove it from service. Tags that are worn to illegibility cannot be replaced by writing capacity information with a marker on the sling body. Replace the sling.

03 Sling Angles & Load Calculations

▼

One of the most critical — and most misunderstood — aspects of rigging is the effect of sling angle on the actual load each sling leg carries. When two sling legs are used in a bridle configuration, the angle between each sling and the horizontal has a dramatic effect on the tension each leg experiences. As the angle decreases (slings become more horizontal), the tension in each leg increases far beyond the weight of the load alone.

Sling Angle Effect on Rated Capacity

The table below shows the percentage of rated vertical capacity that remains usable as the sling angle changes. The angle is always measured from the horizontal, not from the vertical.

Sling Angle from Horizontal % of Vertical Rated Capacity Usable Tension in Each Leg (for 1,000 lb load)
90° (vertical)100%500 lbs each
60°87%577 lbs each
45°71%707 lbs each
30°50%1,000 lbs each
15°25%1,932 lbs each
⚠ The 30° Danger Zone. At a 30° sling angle, each sling leg carries a tension equal to the full weight of the load — double what a vertical sling would carry. At 15°, each leg carries nearly twice the weight of the load. Slings that appear more than adequate for the load can fail catastrophically when rigged at a low angle. BBB Tank Services requires a minimum sling angle of 45° from horizontal for all standard lifts. Angles less than 45° require a Qualified Rigger review and may require a Critical Lift designation.

Determining the WLL for a Lift

  1. 1
    Determine load weight. Obtain the actual weight from engineering drawings, equipment nameplates, or manufacturer data. Never estimate weight for crane lifts — a reasonable-looking object may weigh far more than it appears. Tank components (flanged heads, internals, pump housings) are frequently heavier than they look.
  2. 2
    Identify the rigging configuration. Determine how many sling legs will be used and at what angle. Measure or calculate the sling angle from the horizontal.
  3. 3
    Calculate the tension per leg. Use the sling angle factor from the table above. Multiply load weight by the sling angle factor to get the actual tension on each leg.
  4. 4
    Verify each sling’s WLL exceeds the calculated tension. Each sling leg must be rated at or above the tension it will experience in the rigged configuration — not just the load weight.
  5. 5
    Verify the crane or hoist capacity. The total load (load weight plus rigging weight) must not exceed the crane or hoist capacity at the operating radius and configuration. Use the crane’s load chart — never rely on a verbal capacity number.

Common Rigging Configurations

Straight (Vertical) Hitch

  • Single sling, vertical from hook to load
  • Full WLL of sling applies
  • Load can rotate unless tag line is used
  • Use when load has a single lift point directly above COG

Choker Hitch

  • Sling loops around load and through its own eye
  • Reduces capacity to approximately 75% of rated vertical WLL
  • Tightens as load is lifted — secures the load better than vertical
  • Do not use choker hitch on loads with sharp edges without corner protectors

Basket Hitch

  • Sling runs under load with both eyes on the hook
  • Doubles capacity when load is balanced and angle is 90°
  • Load may shift or roll — use only on stable, balanced loads
  • Never use basket hitch without securing both legs so load cannot roll out

04 Rigging Hardware Inspection

▼

Rigging hardware — shackles, hooks, rings, and eye bolts — is only as reliable as its condition at the time of use. Hardware that has been overloaded, corroded, improperly modified, or not rated for the application can fail without warning. Inspect all hardware before every lift.

Shackles

Inspection Points

  • Verify WLL is stamped or embossed on the body — not just written in marker
  • Check for cracks, gouges, or deformation in the bow and pin
  • Verify the pin is the correct pin for that shackle body (not a bolt or rod substitute)
  • Pin must be fully seated and secured (cotter pin or wire moused through the pin hole)
  • Check for corrosion that could have reduced cross-section

Common Errors

  • Using a bolt instead of the rated shackle pin
  • Side-loading the shackle (load applied to the pin rather than the bow)
  • Using a shackle without mousing the pin — pin can unscrew under load rotation
  • Using the wrong shackle type (screw pin shackle in a rotating load application)
  • Exceeding the shackle WLL because “it’s only a little over”

Hooks

  • Safety latch must be present and functional — a hook without a functional safety latch must be removed from service
  • Inspect the throat opening — if it has opened more than 15% of its normal throat width, the hook is overloaded and must be removed from service
  • Inspect the hook tip for twisting — a twist of more than 10° from the plane of the hook indicates overloading
  • Never repair a cracked or bent hook by welding or heat application
  • Load must sit in the bowl (saddle) of the hook — never on the tip or the latch

Eye Bolts & Hoist Rings

Eye Bolts

  • Shouldered eye bolts must be fully seated against the work surface with zero gap
  • Plain (un-shouldered) eye bolts are rated for vertical lift only — any angular loading dramatically reduces WLL
  • Shouldered eye bolts: angular loading at 45° reduces WLL by 30%; at 90° (horizontal) reduces WLL by 75%
  • Inspect threads for stripping or cross-threading before installing

Swivel Hoist Rings

  • Preferred over eye bolts for angular rigging applications
  • Swivel and pivot to maintain rated WLL at any load angle
  • Inspect swivel action — must rotate freely; binding swivels indicate damage
  • Verify mounting bolt is fully torqued per manufacturer specification
Remove Sling If:Any visible defect, missing tag, or WLL not clearly identifiable
Remove Hook If:Safety latch missing; throat opened >15%; twisted >10°; any cracks
Remove Shackle If:Deformed bow/pin; pin not original; no WLL marking; corrosion
Remove Chain If:Link elongated; twisted links; cracks; not Grade 80 or 100 alloy
Never Use:Hardware store chain, galvanized chain, or unmarked rigging hardware for lifting
Always Verify:Total rigging weight included in load calculation; never exceed any single component’s WLL

05 Pre-Lift Planning & Critical Lifts

▼

Every lift at BBB Tank Services — regardless of how “routine” it appears — requires planning before the hook is attached. Rushing into a lift without proper preparation is one of the most common causes of rigging incidents. For larger or more complex lifts, a formal Pre-Lift Plan or Critical Lift Plan is required.

Pre-Lift Checklist — Every Lift

  1. 1
    Know the load weight. Verify actual weight from documentation. If unknown, weigh the load or contact engineering. Never guess.
  2. 2
    Identify the center of gravity (COG). Determine where the load will balance. An off-center COG causes the load to tilt when lifted. Adjust rigging attachment points to center the load over the COG before lifting.
  3. 3
    Select and inspect all rigging. Choose slings, shackles, and hardware rated for the load at the planned rigging configuration (including sling angle). Inspect every component before use.
  4. 4
    Verify crane/hoist capacity at the operating radius. Use the load chart for the actual boom length and operating radius. Crane capacity decreases significantly at longer radii.
  5. 5
    Check the lift path. Walk the entire path the load will travel. Look for overhead obstructions, power lines, weak floor areas, and other equipment or personnel that need to be cleared.
  6. 6
    Establish the exclusion zone. Mark and barricade the area beneath and around the lift path. Post a spotter. No unauthorized personnel in the exclusion zone during the lift.
  7. 7
    Assign and brief all roles. Designate the Qualified Rigger, Signal Person, and any tag line tenders. Brief all crew on the lift plan, signals to be used, and emergency stop procedure.

What Makes a Lift “Critical”?

A Critical Lift requires a formal written plan, engineering review, and additional supervision. BBB Tank Services designates a lift as critical when any of the following apply:

  • The load exceeds 75% of the crane’s rated capacity at the operating radius
  • The lift involves two cranes sharing the load (tandem lift)
  • The load is being lifted over or adjacent to energized equipment, process lines, or occupied structures
  • The load is personnel (man-basket lift)
  • The load is extremely difficult to rig due to size, shape, fragility, or unidentifiable COG
  • Environmental conditions are unusual (high wind, extreme heat, night operation)
When in Doubt, Designate as Critical. There is no downside to treating a lift as critical when you are uncertain. The extra planning and review add time but eliminate the conditions where catastrophic failures occur. If any crew member is uncertain about a lift, that is sufficient reason to pause and re-evaluate.

Tag Lines & Load Control

Tag lines are ropes or lines attached to the load to control swing and rotation during the lift. They are not optional for BBB Tank Services lifts:

  • Attach tag lines to the load before the lift begins — not once the load is in the air and swinging
  • Tag line tenders must stand clear of the load path and use the tag line to guide, not to hold the load against crane movement
  • Never wrap a tag line around your hand, wrist, or body — a load swing can pull a person off their feet or drag them under the load
  • Tag lines must be long enough that tenders remain outside the exclusion zone while controlling the load
  • In areas where tag lines could contact electrical lines, use non-conductive (synthetic) tag line material
Scenario — Pre-Lift Failure

The Unmarked Tank Component

A crew needs to lift a large flanged nozzle assembly off a flatbed and position it near a tank opening. The rigging crew estimates the piece at “probably 800 lbs” based on its size and rigs a 1-ton chain fall with a 3/4-inch wire rope sling in a choker configuration. As they lift, the wire rope sling snaps and the piece drops, narrowly missing two workers.

Investigation reveals: The nozzle assembly was a solid forged steel component weighing 2,400 lbs — three times the estimated weight. The 3/4-inch wire rope sling in choker configuration (75% of rated capacity) had a WLL of approximately 1,400 lbs — far less than the actual load. The chain fall was also overloaded. The failure was entirely caused by not verifying load weight before rigging. Manufacturer documentation for the component was on the flatbed driver’s paperwork the entire time.

06 Hand Signals, Exclusion Zones & Never-Do Rules

▼

Standard Hand Signals

OSHA requires the use of standard hand signals unless alternative signals are agreed upon before the lift begins. All crew members involved in the lift must know and understand the signals being used. Only one designated signal person communicates with the operator at any time.

👍

Hoist / Raise Load

Forearm vertical, forefinger pointing up, move hand in small horizontal circles

👎

Lower Load

Arm extended downward, forefinger pointing down, move hand in small horizontal circles

✊

Stop

Arm extended palm down, move arm back and forth horizontally

✋

Emergency Stop

Both arms extended palm down, move arms back and forth horizontally (urgent)

👈

Swing / Travel

Point finger in direction of travel for boom or crane travel movement

👋

Dog Everything

Clasp hands in front of body — stop all crane movement; hold position

🖐

Raise Boom

Arm extended, fingers closed, thumb pointing upward

🖐

Lower Boom

Arm extended, fingers closed, thumb pointing downward

👌

Creep / Slowly

Give any signal with the other hand held motionless in front of signal hand; all movements slow

Exclusion Zone Requirements

An exclusion zone is the area beneath and around the load path where no unauthorized personnel are permitted during a lift. BBB Tank Services requires:

  • The exclusion zone encompasses the area beneath the entire load travel path, plus a radius equal to the load height from each side
  • The zone is established and barricaded or flagged before the lift begins — not after the load is in the air
  • A spotter is posted to maintain the zone and alert the signal person if anyone approaches
  • In areas where barricading is impractical, a safety watch must clear and hold the area before and during all lifts
  • Customer site exclusion zone requirements may be more stringent than BBB minimums — always follow the more protective requirement

Absolute Never-Do Rules for Rigging & Crane Work

These rules have no exceptions at BBB Tank Services:
  • Never stand, walk, or work under a suspended load — if the rigging fails, nothing will protect you. Not a hard hat. Not a safety harness.
  • Never exceed any component’s WLL — there is no “just a little over”. Every WLL is set with factors of safety that account for dynamic loading, wear, and service conditions.
  • Never use unmarked or unrated rigging hardware — includes hardware store chain, eye hooks from the electrical section, or any hardware whose WLL cannot be confirmed by the marking on the device.
  • Never leave a load suspended unattended — if work stops, the load must be landed on a stable surface before the rigging crew leaves the area.
  • Never allow tag line tenders to wrap lines around their hands or bodies — loop the line in the hands; do not wrap.
  • Never operate a crane near overhead power lines without established clearance — minimum 10 feet from lines up to 50 kV.
