First Aid, CPR & Emergency Medical Response

📅 Rev. 10/31/2025 ⚖ OSHA 29 CFR 1926.50 ⏱ ~15 min read 📝 10-question quiz
🏥2–3. General Requirements & Emergency Response
  • The Company ensures the availability of medical personnel for advice on occupational health matters.
  • Provisions for prompt medical attention in case of severe injury must be made before a project commences.
  • Where an infirmary, clinic, or hospital is not reasonably accessible in terms of time and distance, a person with a valid first aid certificate (U.S. Bureau of Mines, American Red Cross, or equivalent) must be available at the worksite.
  • The Site Supervisor is responsible for confirming availability of emergency medical services before work begins, and ensuring transportation arrangements are in place for injured workers.
  • In areas where 911 is not available, the Site Supervisor must post the phone numbers of physicians, hospitals, and ambulances.
Post Names of CPR-Trained Employees The names of all jobsite employees trained in CPR and first aid must be posted by the telephone or other communications method, or in another prominent place at the worksite.

When responding to an emergency:

  • The first person at the scene should react quickly but calmly and in a reassuring manner.
  • Immediately summon medical help — be explicit: state the suspected type of injury or illness, the location of the victim, and the type of assistance required.
  • Send people to highway intersections or entrance roadways to direct EMS to the scene.
  • The injured person should NOT be moved except where necessary to prevent further injury.
  • If using a communication system where location is not automatically provided to 911, post the worksite latitude/longitude or location-identification information in a conspicuous location.
🩹4. General First Aid for Minor Injuries

General first aid is defined as any one-time treatment — or follow-up visit for observation — of minor scratches, cuts, burns, or splinters that do not ordinarily require medical care.

  • Minor injuries should be initially treated with self-administered first aid unless assistance is required. This limits the exposure of others to potential pathogens.
  • All minor injuries must be reported to a supervisor and recorded on the First Aid Report form at each work location. A minor injury may indicate a hazardous condition that could cause serious future injuries.
  • Documentation is critical — a minor injury should be recorded as "work related" in case it later leads to serious complications such as infection.
Employee Refusing First Aid — Cannot Return to Work If an employee declines first aid and/or medical treatment recommended by the Site Supervisor, the employee will NOT be allowed to continue work. The Site Supervisor must discuss the situation with the Company Safety Representative or Personnel Department before allowing the employee to return to duty.
🦺5. Personal Protection During First Aid

OSHA requires adherence to Universal Precautions when responding to emergencies. All patients must be treated as potentially infectious for HIV and other bloodborne pathogens.

SituationRequired PPE
Minimal bleeding or handling contaminated instrumentsDisposable gloves only
Spurting blood / significant blood exposureDisposable gloves + gown + mask + protective eye wear
Taking temperature or blood pressureNo protection required
Wash Hands Immediately After After emergency care, wash hands and all skin surfaces with warm water and soap if contaminated with blood or body fluids. Always wash hands after removing gloves — even if the gloves appear to be intact.
📋6–7. Reporting Requirements & First Aid Station
  • All work-related injuries and illnesses must be reported immediately to management and treated as soon as possible.
  • A First Aid Station is located at each work location and jobsite, stocked with specified supplies and First Aid Report forms.
  • Supplies are checked by the appointed supervisor before each job and at least weekly on each job by the Site Supervisor to ensure expended items are replaced.
Required First Aid Kit Contents Include: Protective rubber gloves (2 pair) · CPR mask with one-way valve · Antiseptic soap · Absorbent gauze · Adhesive bandages · Bandage compresses · Eye dressing · Bandage scissors · Tweezers · Antiseptic pads · Burn ointment · Sterile eye wash · Cool packs (2) · Sterile burn sheet · Bio-hazard bags (red) · First Aid Report forms
🌡8. Heat-Related Illnesses — In Order of Severity

Heat is a serious hazard outdoors in hot weather and indoors in high-temperature environments. Too much heat can cause injury and, at its worst, death. Know each stage and what to do.

Heat Rash
Signs: Tiny red blister-like eruptions; prickly, itchy, burning sensation.
First Aid: Bathe skin to prevent infection, put on dry clothes.
Sunburn
Signs: Red, painful skin (1st degree); blistering (2nd degree).
First Aid: Topical anesthetics; move to shaded area.
Heat Cramps
Signs: Painful muscle spasms, may occur even after leaving work.
First Aid: Water and/or electrolyte replacement beverage. Get medical assistance.
Heat Exhaustion
Signs: Cool, moist skin; obvious sweating; rapid pulse (more than 150 BPM); fatigue, nausea, headache, dizziness; possible fainting.
First Aid: Water and/or electrolyte replacement beverage. Rest in cool area.
🚨 HEAT STROKE
Signs: Hot, DRY skin (sweating has stopped); body temperature 105°F or higher; rapid pulse; confusion, delirium, or unconsciousness; possible convulsions.

First Aid — LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY:
1. Remove victim from heat source immediately.
2. Lower body temperature as fast as possible — immerse in water (hose, shower, bathtub) or cover with cool wet towels.
3. DO NOT give liquids to an unconscious person.
4. Call for emergency medical assistance immediately.
💧8.6. Preventing Heat Stress
15 min
Drink cool water every 15 minutes — approximately 5 to 7 ounces per cup. Don't wait until you feel thirsty — thirst means you're already behind on hydration.
  • Take rest breaks in a cool, shady spot. Use fans.
  • Wear light-colored cotton clothing.
  • Schedule the heaviest work during the coolest part of the day.
  • Work in the shade whenever possible.
  • For heavy work in hot areas, rotate with other workers so some can rest.
  • If transferring to a warm area for a new job, allow at least two weeks for your body to acclimate — be extra careful during that period.
  • Employees working in protective clothing require more rest breaks and may need temperature and heart rate monitoring.
OSHA General Duty Clause OSHA does not have a specific heat standard, but heat stress is a recognized serious hazard. Employers are required under the General Duty Clause to provide employment free from recognized hazards causing or likely to cause physical harm.
