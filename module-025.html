Scaffold Safety Program

Safe erection, use, inspection, and dismantling of scaffolds used for BBB Tank Services tank maintenance, painting, and external inspection operations.

⏱ Est. 35 min 📋 6 Sections ✅ 10-Question Quiz 📅 Rev. Oct 2025

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Identify the leading causes of scaffold-related fatalities
  • Distinguish between a Qualified Person and a Competent Person for scaffolding
  • Identify the load capacity requirements for light-, medium-, and heavy-duty scaffolds
  • State the platform width, planking, and gap requirements
  • Identify guardrail dimensions and toeboard requirements
  • Describe fall protection requirements for scaffold workers

01 Scaffold Hazards, Statistics & OSHA Requirements

▼

Scaffolding is involved in approximately 4,500 injuries and 60 fatalities every year in the United States. OSHA consistently ranks scaffolding as one of the top ten most frequently cited construction standards. At BBB Tank Services, scaffolding is erected on and around storage tanks for painting, inspection, nozzle maintenance, and insulation work — often on uneven dike surfaces, near flammable vapors, and in high-wind conditions typical of Texas Gulf Coast sites.

Leading Causes of Scaffold Incidents

Worker Falls (43% of fatalities)

  • Missing or inadequate guardrails
  • Improperly planked or cross-braced platforms
  • Accessing scaffold from the wrong point
  • Overreaching beyond the guardrail
  • Slippery platforms (wet, icy, oil-contaminated)

Scaffold Collapse & Falling Objects

  • Overloaded scaffold (exceeding capacity)
  • Unstable base or inadequate mudsills
  • Improperly connected or missing bracing
  • Dropped tools and materials striking workers below
  • Scaffold struck by vehicles or equipment

Applicable OSHA Standards

StandardScope
29 CFR 1926.451General scaffold requirements — capacity, platform construction, access, fall protection, falling object protection
29 CFR 1926.452Additional requirements for specific scaffold types (tube and coupler, frame, system, suspended)
29 CFR 1926.453Aerial lifts (scissor lifts, boom lifts, boom trucks used as work platforms)
29 CFR 1926.454Training requirements for scaffold erectors, users, and inspectors
ANSI/SSFI A10.8Scaffolding — safety requirements (industry standard referenced by OSHA)

Qualified Person vs. Competent Person

Qualified Person

  • Has recognized degree, certificate, or professional standing in scaffold design
  • Extensive knowledge and experience in scaffold engineering
  • Must design any scaffold that requires engineering calculations
  • Required for scaffolds >125 feet high, suspended scaffolds, and non-standard configurations
  • Signs and seals scaffold drawings where required

Competent Person

  • Capable of identifying existing and predictable scaffold hazards
  • Has authority to take prompt corrective action
  • Must supervise erection, alteration, and dismantling
  • Must inspect scaffolding before each work shift and after any event that could affect structural integrity (high wind, overload, impact)
  • BBB Tank Services designates competent persons on each job requiring scaffold use

02 Scaffold Types & Load Capacities

▼

Scaffold Types Used at BBB Tank Services

Frame / Sectional Scaffold

Light to Heavy Duty
  • Pre-fabricated welded steel frames; most common type
  • Quick to erect and dismantle
  • Used for tank shell access, painting, inspection
  • Must be cross-braced in both directions
  • Legs must be on mudsills on soft or uneven ground

Tube & Coupler Scaffold

Most Flexible
  • Individual steel tubes connected with swivel or fixed couplers
  • Can be built around curved tank shells and irregular geometries
  • Preferred for complex tank profiles
  • Requires more skilled erection
  • Each coupler must be inspected for correct engagement

System Scaffold (Modular)

Standard & Custom
  • Proprietary components (Saflock, Layher, Kwik-Stage)
  • Pre-engineered connection points — faster than tube & coupler
  • Common on large refineries and petrochemical sites
  • Components must be matched — no mixing of incompatible systems

Rolling / Mobile Scaffold

Light Duty
  • Frame scaffold on castors; moveable without dismantling
  • Height-to-base ratio must not exceed 4:1 (indoors) or 3:1 (outdoors)
  • All casters must be locked before anyone ascends
  • No workers on rolling scaffold while it is being moved
  • Not appropriate for use on tank dike surfaces with drainage slopes

Suspended Scaffold

Special Requirements
  • Platform suspended from overhead by wire ropes or chains
  • Used for tank dome interiors or structures too high for supported scaffold
  • Requires Qualified Person design
  • Suspension system inspected before each use
  • Personal fall arrest required for all workers

Aerial Work Platforms (AWP)

Scissor & Boom Lifts
  • Self-propelled; classified as aerial lifts under 1926.453
  • Workers must be in harness attached to the basket anchor
  • Do not use as crane or extend reach beyond the basket
  • Operator must be trained per manufacturer requirements
  • Check ground bearing capacity before positioning

Scaffold Duty Ratings & Load Limits

Every scaffold is classified by the load it is designed to carry. The total load includes workers, tools, and materials simultaneously on the platform. The scaffold must support 4 times the maximum intended load without failure.

Duty RatingCapacity per Platform LevelTypical Use
Light Duty25 lbs per sq ft (max ~375 lbs on a standard bay)Inspection, painting with hand tools, touch-up work — no heavy equipment or stockpiled materials
Medium Duty50 lbs per sq ftGeneral tank maintenance, coating application, light power tool use
Heavy Duty75 lbs per sq ftConcrete, masonry, or heavy material handling — rare in tank services work
⚠ Never Overload a Scaffold. A scaffold rated for light duty cannot become medium duty by “being careful.” The duty rating is an engineering determination. Placing a 5-gallon bucket of coating on a light-duty scaffold that already has two workers on it may exceed the rated capacity. Before placing any significant load on a scaffold, confirm the duty rating with the competent person.

03 Platform Requirements & Guardrails

▼

Platform Construction Requirements

The scaffold platform is where workers stand and work. OSHA has specific dimensional requirements for scaffold platforms that must be met before any worker accesses the scaffold:

RequirementOSHA SpecificationNotes
Minimum Width18 inches for most scaffolds; 14 inches for two-point (swing stage) scaffolds; 36 inches where used as a passagewayBBB Tank Services standard: 18 inches minimum for all work platforms
Platform PlanksMust be laid with no gaps greater than 1 inch between planks (except at the scaffold frame, where up to 9.5 inches is permitted for system scaffolds)Gaps allow tools and material to fall through, striking workers below
Plank OverhangMinimum 6 inches beyond supports; maximum 12 inches for planks ≤10 ft; maximum 18 inches for planks >10 ftUnder-overhang creates trip hazard; over-overhang creates cantilever tip-up
End Plank GapNo more than 14 inches between the end of the platform and the wall or structure being worked onLarger gaps allow workers to fall between the platform and the wall
Platform ConditionFree of mud, ice, grease, oil, debris, or any material that creates a slip or trip hazardOn tank sites, petroleum product contamination is a serious slip hazard

When Guardrails Are Required

Guardrails must be installed on all open sides and ends of scaffold platforms that are 10 feet or more above a lower level. This includes:

  • All open sides of the platform (not against a structure)
  • Both ends of the platform unless the end is against a solid wall
  • The entrance to the scaffold if it is on a side rather than at the end
⚠ Guardrail Removal for Work Tasks. Sometimes a guardrail section must be temporarily removed to land material or perform a specific task. When a guardrail is removed, the worker in that area must use personal fall arrest equipment (full body harness and lanyard) attached to an independent anchor point — not the scaffold itself — for the entire time the guardrail is absent. The guardrail must be replaced immediately after the task is complete. Never leave a platform open-sided without either a guardrail or personal fall arrest in place.

Toeboards & Falling Object Protection

  • Toeboards (minimum 3.5 inches high) are required on all edges of scaffold platforms where workers below could be struck by falling tools or materials
  • If the scaffold is adjacent to a passageway, a barricade or debris net below the scaffold level may be required in addition to toeboards
  • All workers below a scaffold where work is being performed must wear hard hats
  • Establish a barricaded exclusion zone below the scaffold to keep unauthorized workers out of the fall zone
  • Never drop tools, materials, or debris from scaffold platforms — use hand lines or material hoists

04 Access, Fall Protection & Base Requirements

▼

Scaffold Access

Workers must have safe access to and from every scaffold platform level. Acceptable access methods include:

Scaffold Stairway

  • Preferred access for regularly-used scaffold >24 feet
  • Handrails required on both sides
  • Uniform rise and run
  • Self-closing gate at top landing

Ladder

  • Must extend at least 3 ft above the platform
  • Secured at top and bottom
  • Positioned so workers do not have to step over guardrails to access the platform
  • Through-hole access preferred over climbing over guardrail

Frame Rungs (Climb-Through)

  • Frame scaffold cross-braces may be used as ladders only if specifically designed for climbing
  • Maximum 18-inch rung spacing
  • Minimum 4.5-inch clearance between rung and cross-brace
⚠ Never Climb Scaffold Cross-Braces Unless Designed for Climbing. Standard X-braces are structural members — not ladders. Climbing on cross-braces that are not specifically rated for climbing can cause them to buckle or disengage, leading to scaffold collapse. If a designated access point is not available, stop work and notify the competent person to arrange proper access.

Fall Protection on Scaffolds

OSHA provides two options for fall protection on scaffolds at or above 10 feet:

Option 1: Guardrail System

  • Top rail, midrail, and toeboard installed per specifications
  • No personal fall arrest equipment required while the guardrail is intact
  • Most common method for supported scaffolding
  • Guardrail must be in place before workers access the platform

Option 2: Personal Fall Arrest System (PFAS)

  • Full body harness + shock-absorbing lanyard attached to an independent anchor
  • Required when guardrails are removed, for suspended scaffolds, and for aerial lifts
  • Anchor must be capable of supporting 5,000 lbs per attached worker
  • The scaffold frame or guardrail is not an acceptable anchor point for PFAS

Base & Foundation Requirements

Scaffold stability begins at the ground. The base of any scaffold must be stable, level, and capable of supporting the full intended load without settlement or movement.

  1. 1
    Mudsills on all soft or uneven ground. Place lumber mudsills (minimum 2×10, spanning at least two base plates) under all scaffold base plates when the ground is soft, sloped, or could settle. Dike areas at petroleum tank sites frequently have compacted but uneven surfaces — mudsills are standard practice.
  2. 2
    Adjustable base plates. Use adjustable screw-type base plates to level the scaffold on sloped surfaces. Do not raise a base plate more than 12 inches from its lowest position without additional engineering review.
  3. 3
    Tying and bracing at height. Scaffold sections must be tied to the structure being worked on at specified intervals to prevent overturning. Consult the manufacturer’s guidelines or a Qualified Person for tie-in requirements for specific scaffold heights.
  4. 4
    Outriggers or extended base. If the scaffold cannot be tied to the structure (e.g., free-standing scaffold on a tank dike), outriggers or an extended base frame may be required to achieve the necessary stability. A Qualified Person must determine the configuration.
Scenario — Base Failure

The Settling Dike

A crew erects a frame scaffold against a tank shell on a compacted clay dike. They place base plates directly on the clay without mudsills. On day 2 of a painting job, after overnight rain, the clay softens and one leg settles 2 inches. The scaffold tilts noticeably toward the tank shell. Workers on the third level feel the platform shift.

Correct response: Evacuate the scaffold immediately. Do not attempt to re-level it while workers are on it. Once clear, lower the scaffold level by level after installing mudsills and re-establishing a level base. The competent person must re-inspect the entire scaffold before work resumes. Rain-softened soil at dike areas is an extremely common cause of scaffold instability at tank sites — always use mudsills, especially when rain is expected.

05 Inspection, Tag System & Out-of-Service Procedures

▼

Competent Person Inspection Requirements

OSHA requires the competent person to inspect scaffolding before each work shift and after any occurrence that could affect structural integrity. This includes:

  • High winds (gusts above 25 mph typically require evacuation and post-wind inspection)
  • Any impact by a vehicle, equipment, or falling load
  • Any component failure, settlement, or unexpected movement
  • Following overnight rain or ice events
  • After any alteration (adding, removing, or repositioning components)

Pre-Shift Inspection Checklist (Authorized Users)

Workers using a scaffold should also perform a basic visual inspection before ascending each shift, even when the competent person has already completed a formal inspection. Look for:

Platform & Structure

  • Planks in place, overlapping properly, no excessive gaps
  • No loose, missing, or displaced planks
  • Platform surface free of debris, spills, and slip hazards
  • All guardrails present, in position, and secure
  • Toeboards in place on all open edges
  • Frame, bracing, and couplers visually secure and undisturbed

Base & Stability

  • Mudsills undisturbed and still bearing evenly
  • Base plates seated on mudsills; no uplift visible
  • Scaffold plumb (vertical) — no noticeable lean
  • Tie-ins to structure still in place and secure
  • No unauthorized changes or missing components since last inspection
  • Scaffold tag present and current (see tag system below)

Scaffold Tag System

BBB Tank Services uses a three-color scaffold tagging system to communicate the status of each scaffold to all workers on site at a glance. Tags are posted at every point of access to the scaffold.

🟢

GREEN — Safe to Use

Scaffold has been inspected by the competent person this shift. All components are in place. All required fall protection and guardrails are installed. Authorized users may access.

🟡

YELLOW — Caution / Restricted

Scaffold may be used only with specific restrictions noted on the tag (e.g., maximum number of workers, personal fall arrest required, certain levels restricted). Read the tag before ascending.

🔴

RED — Do Not Use

Scaffold is out of service. Missing components, structural deficiency, failed inspection, or pending alterations. No one may access this scaffold for any reason until the tag is changed by the competent person after all deficiencies are corrected.

⚠ Never Remove or Ignore a Red Tag. A red-tagged scaffold has been taken out of service by the competent person because a hazard was identified. Removing the red tag, bypassing the tag, or accessing the scaffold “just for a moment” while red-tagged is prohibited under any circumstances. The deficiency that caused the red tag may be invisible to an untrained observer — trust the system.

Scaffold Alterations & Dismantling

  • Any modification to an erected scaffold — adding height, removing braces, relocating planks — must be done under the direct supervision of the competent person
  • The scaffold must be re-inspected and re-tagged by the competent person after any alteration before workers re-access it
  • Dismantling is performed under the competent person’s supervision, working from the top down — never dismantle lower levels while upper levels still carry material or workers
  • Scaffold components must be controlled during dismantling and not dropped or thrown to the ground
  • Inspect all components after dismantling; damaged or deformed components must be tagged and segregated from serviceable components before being returned to storage

06 Scaffold Safety at Petroleum Tank Sites

▼

Petroleum tank sites present scaffold hazards beyond those found on typical construction sites. Every BBB Tank Services supervisor and worker must account for these site-specific factors when planning and using scaffold systems at customer tank facilities.

Flammable Vapor Hazards Near Scaffolding

Ignition Source Control

  • Scaffold erection and dismantling generates metal-on-metal sparks. On in-service tanks with flammable vapor potential, atmospheric monitoring is required before and during scaffold erection near tank openings and in the dike area
  • Use non-sparking tools (brass hammers, bronze mallets) for scaffold assembly in classified areas
  • Grounding the scaffold to the tank or to a dedicated ground rod is required to prevent static buildup from wind on large scaffold structures adjacent to tanks

Vapor Accumulation on Platforms

  • Petroleum vapors are heavier than air and can accumulate on scaffold platforms, especially at lower levels inside dike walls
  • Workers on scaffold platforms near in-service tanks must wear personal 4-gas monitors
  • Ensure adequate natural ventilation on platforms; avoid working in enclosed scaffold areas near tank vents during product transfer operations

Environmental Conditions Specific to Tank Sites

  • Wind: Tank dike areas are often exposed with no windbreak. Wind speeds above 25 mph can make scaffold work unsafe and may require work stoppage. Taller scaffolds are more susceptible. Monitor wind conditions throughout the shift, not just at setup.
  • Heat: In Texas summers, metal scaffold components can reach temperatures exceeding 150°F in direct sunlight. Workers can suffer burns from bare-hand contact with scaffold tubes. Wear gloves when working on sun-exposed scaffold. Use shade and hydration breaks per the BBB Heat Illness Prevention Program (Module 031).
  • Dike Surface Conditions: Dike berms are often sloped, may have contaminated soil from minor spills, and can become extremely soft after rain. Inspect base conditions before and after any significant rainfall. Mudsills are mandatory in all dike areas at BBB Tank Services.
  • Chemical Contamination: Scaffold planks and platforms exposed to petroleum product spray or spills become immediately slip-hazardous. Clean contaminated platforms before allowing workers to access them. Remove and replace planks that cannot be adequately decontaminated.

Aerial Work Platforms at Tank Sites

Scissor lifts and boom lifts are commonly used at tank facilities for inspection and maintenance tasks. Additional tank-site specific rules apply:

  • Ground bearing capacity: AWPs are heavy — verify the dike surface can support the outrigger loads before positioning. Do not assume a compacted dike can support a fully loaded boom lift at maximum reach
  • Classified areas: Use only explosion-proof or intrinsically safe AWPs in areas classified for flammable vapors. Verify the equipment rating against the area classification before operating
  • Harness and anchor: All workers in AWP baskets must wear a full body harness anchored to the manufacturer-designated anchor point in the basket. Do not use a self-retracting lifeline (SRL) to an external structure from inside an AWP
  • No climbing on rails: Workers must never stand on the mid- or top rail of an AWP basket to reach higher. This shifts the center of gravity and can tip the lift
  • Overhead hazards: Tank sites have process lines, electrical conduit, and instrumentation at various elevations. Survey the work area for overhead obstructions before operating the lift
