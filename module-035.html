01 What Is Risk Assessment & Why It Matters

▼

A risk assessment is a systematic process of identifying hazards in a task or work environment, evaluating the likelihood and severity of harm those hazards could cause, and determining what controls are needed to reduce that risk to an acceptable level. It is the foundational thinking process behind every safe work permit, Job Safety Analysis, toolbox talk, and pre-task briefing.

Risk assessment is not paperwork. It is not a form filled out to satisfy a supervisor. It is the deliberate mental process of asking: “What could go wrong here, how bad could it be, and what do we do about it before we start?” Workers who perform this thinking before every task — not just for major lifts or confined space entries, but for every task — are the ones who go home uninjured.

Hazard vs. Risk — An Important Distinction

Hazard

  • A hazard is any source of potential harm — a condition, substance, object, or situation that has the potential to cause injury, illness, or damage
  • A hazard exists independently of whether anyone is present
  • Examples: an open tank hatch, pressurized piping, H&sub2;S in a tank, a wet metal surface, a suspended load
  • Hazards cannot always be eliminated; they must be identified and controlled

Risk

  • Risk is the combination of likelihood and consequence — how probable it is that the hazard will cause harm, and how serious that harm would be if it occurred
  • Risk depends on who is exposed, how often, and with what controls in place
  • Example: An open tank hatch (hazard) + a worker who frequently crosses the tank top without a guardrail (high likelihood) + a 20-foot fall to the tank bottom (serious consequence) = HIGH RISK
  • Risk is what we measure and control

Why Risk Assessment Is Required at BBB Tank Services: OSHA standards including the PSM standard (1910.119), the Confined Space standard (1910.146), and the Permit-Required Confined Space standard all require formal hazard analysis before specific high-risk work. Many customer facilities require contractor risk assessments or JSAs before permitting any maintenance work. Beyond compliance, risk assessment is the primary tool that separates workers who identify hazards before they cause harm from workers who discover hazards the hard way.

02 Hazard Identification

▼

You cannot control a hazard you have not identified. Hazard identification is the most critical step in risk assessment — and the one most frequently done poorly. Workers who rush through a hazard identification scan and say “it looks fine” miss the hazards that are not immediately obvious: the valve that could be energized from a remote control panel, the insulation that might contain asbestos, the gas that accumulated in the low point while the work permit was being signed.

Six Categories of Workplace Hazards

⚡ Physical

  • Struck-by objects
  • Falls from height
  • Noise and vibration
  • Extreme temperatures
  • Radiation (UV, IR)
  • Pressure hazards

⚗️ Chemical

  • Toxic gases (H&sub2;S, CO)
  • Flammable vapors
  • Corrosives (acids, caustics)
  • Carcinogens (benzene, Cr VI)
  • Asphyxiants (O&sub2; deficiency)
  • Reactive chemicals

👷 Ergonomic

  • Manual handling / lifting
  • Awkward postures in confined spaces
  • Repetitive motion
  • Vibration from power tools
  • Sustained force or exertion

🦠 Biological

  • Venomous snakes and insects at tank sites
  • Legionella in cooling towers
  • Bloodborne pathogens (first aid)
  • Bacteria in process water
  • Mold in confined structures

🌉 Environmental

  • Extreme heat (Texas summers)
  • Lightning and severe weather
  • High winds on elevated work
  • Sun exposure / UV radiation
  • Slippery surfaces from rain or dew

🧠 Psychosocial

  • Production pressure leading to shortcuts
  • Fatigue from extended hours
  • Complacency on familiar tasks
  • Communication failures
  • Normalization of deviance

Hazard Identification Techniques

Effective hazard identification uses multiple techniques rather than a single scan:

Pre-Task Walkdown

  • Physically walk through the entire work area before starting, not from memory or from a drawing
  • Look up (overhead hazards), down (surface conditions), and around (adjacent operations that could affect the work area)
  • Test assumptions: confirm that valves described as “closed” are actually closed; confirm gauge readings match expected conditions; confirm atmospheric readings match the permit
  • Ask: “What has changed since the last time this task was done or since the permit was written?”

WHAT IF? Questioning

  • “What if the atmospheric monitor alarms during this task?”
  • “What if the crane load shifts during the lift?”
  • “What if we need to evacuate quickly — is the egress route clear?”
  • “What if a worker is injured in this location — how does EMS reach them?”
  • “What if this task takes twice as long as planned — does that create a shift-change hazard?”
  • There are no wrong What If questions

Past Incident Review

  • Check incident logs and near-miss reports for this type of task at this facility or similar ones
  • Incidents and near-misses are the industry’s compiled experience with hazards you have not yet encountered personally
  • OSHA investigation reports for similar tasks are publicly available and valuable references

Crew Knowledge

  • Ask the entire crew during the toolbox talk: “What hazards do you see with this task?”
  • Workers performing the task often see hazards that supervisors planning it miss
  • Experienced workers have encountered hazards in similar tasks that are worth sharing; newer workers often ask the most useful “What if” questions because they don’t assume they already know the answer

03 Risk Evaluation — The Risk Matrix

▼

Once hazards are identified, each one must be evaluated to determine its risk level. Risk is a function of two factors: Likelihood (how probable is it that this hazard will cause harm?) and Consequence (how severe would the harm be if it occurred?). The Risk Matrix combines these two dimensions into a single risk rating that guides decision-making about controls.

What Each Risk Level Requires

Risk LevelScoreRequired Action
■ Low 1–2 Proceed with standard precautions. Document the assessment. Monitor conditions.
■ Medium 3–4 Implement additional controls to reduce risk before starting. Supervisor reviews the assessment. Work may proceed once controls are confirmed.

⚠ Evaluate Risk BEFORE Controls, Then Recalculate AFTER. The Risk Matrix must be applied twice: first with no controls in place (the “inherent risk”) to understand the true hazard level, and then again with the planned controls in place (the “residual risk”) to confirm the controls are adequate. A task with an inherent risk of Extreme that is reduced to Medium through engineering controls is acceptable. A task where the residual risk remains High or Extreme even with controls applied should be escalated or reconsidered.
Scenario — Risk Scoring

Replacing a Flange Gasket on a Crude Oil Line

Hazards identified: (1) Asbestos-containing gasket in a pre-1980 line; (2) Residual crude oil in the isolated section; (3) H&sub2;S in the crude oil; (4) Slip hazard from crude oil on metal grating.

Without controls: Asbestos exposure during gasket removal = Likely (3) × Serious (3) = High (9). H&sub2;S exposure if residual crude releases = Possible (2) × Catastrophic (4) = High (8).

Controls applied: Wet methods + supplied-air respirator for asbestos; confirmed double-block-and-bleed with pressure gauge verification + 4-gas monitor for H&sub2;S. After controls: Asbestos = Unlikely (1) × Minor (1) = Low (1). H&sub2;S = Unlikely (1) × Serious (3) = Medium (3). Residual risk is acceptable; work may proceed with controls verified in place.

04 Risk Controls & the Hierarchy

▼

Once the risk level is established, controls must be selected and implemented to reduce that risk to an acceptable level. The Hierarchy of Controls provides the framework for selecting the most effective controls. Controls higher in the hierarchy are more reliable because they do not depend on consistent human behavior. Controls lower in the hierarchy (PPE) are less reliable because their effectiveness depends entirely on correct selection, use, and maintenance every time.

The goal is always to select controls as high in the hierarchy as feasible. PPE is the last resort — added on top of higher-level controls, not substituted for them.

Combining Controls

For most tasks, multiple levels of the hierarchy are used simultaneously. This is the correct approach. A single control, even a good engineering control, can fail. Combining a reliable engineering control with an administrative control and appropriate PPE creates layers of protection — the “Swiss cheese” model where no single layer is perfect, but multiple layers together prevent the hazard from reaching the worker.

✓ The Control Adequacy Test: After selecting controls, ask: “If this control fails, what prevents the hazard from causing harm?” If the answer is “nothing” — only one control stands between the worker and the hazard — add another layer. Single-layer protection is inadequate for any High or Extreme risk task.

05 Job Safety Analysis (JSA)

▼

A Job Safety Analysis (JSA) — also called a Job Hazard Analysis (JHA) — is the formal, written risk assessment tool used by BBB Tank Services before all significant maintenance, repair, and service tasks. It breaks a job into specific steps, identifies the hazards associated with each step, and documents the controls required before that step can be performed. The JSA is reviewed with the entire crew before work begins and is a living document that must be updated if conditions change.

How to Complete a JSA

  1. 1
    Define the task. Write a clear, specific description of the task to be performed. Vague task descriptions produce vague hazard identification. “Nozzle maintenance on Tank 7, south side” is better than “tank work.”
  2. 2
    Break the task into sequential steps. List every distinct step in the order it will be performed. Include setup steps (obtaining tools, setting up scaffold) and closeout steps (cleaning up, returning tools). Steps should be specific enough that a new worker could follow them.
  3. 3
    Identify hazards for each step. For every step, ask: What could go wrong? What hazards are present? Use the six hazard categories. Do not combine hazards from different steps or write “all standard hazards” — be specific to the step.
  4. 4
    Evaluate the risk. Score each hazard using the risk matrix before controls are applied. This confirms that the hazard warrants the controls you are planning.
  5. 5
    Document the controls. For each hazard, list the specific controls required using the hierarchy. Name the PPE specifically — not just “PPE required” but “full-face APR with OV+P100 cartridges.”
  6. 6
    Brief the crew. Review every step and hazard with the entire crew before work begins. Every worker must sign the JSA confirming they have been briefed and understand their responsibilities.

06 Dynamic Risk Assessment & When to Stop

▼

Dynamic Risk Assessment

A formal JSA or risk assessment completed before a job starts is a pre-task assessment based on known conditions at the time of planning. But conditions at petroleum tank sites change constantly — wind shifts, adjacent operations begin, equipment behaves unexpectedly, and previously unknown hazards are discovered mid-task. Dynamic risk assessment is the continuous, ongoing mental process of evaluating whether the current conditions still match the risk assessment that authorized the work.

Every worker performs dynamic risk assessment every time they pause to consider whether what they are about to do is safe. The key skill is acting on that evaluation — stopping work when new hazards emerge rather than continuing because “the permit said it was okay.”

When the Risk Assessment Must Be Reviewed or Restarted

Mandatory Review Triggers

  • A personal gas monitor alarms during work
  • Unexpected product, pressure, or temperature is found in an “isolated” system
  • A new hazard is discovered that was not in the original JSA
  • A near-miss or incident occurs, even a minor one
  • Work scope changes from what was planned (even if the change seems minor)
  • A new worker joins the crew mid-task who was not briefed on the JSA
  • Weather or environmental conditions change significantly (wind shift near a tank, rain on an elevated surface, lightning in the area)
  • Work continues across a shift change

What Review Means in Practice

  • Stop work immediately when a review trigger occurs
  • Gather the crew and assess the new conditions
  • Update the JSA to document the new hazard and the controls being implemented
  • Re-brief all crew members on the updated assessment — including any who were not present when the trigger occurred
  • If the new hazard cannot be adequately controlled with available resources, do not resume — escalate to the BBB supervisor and the facility
  • Document the review event and the decision made

Risk Assessment & Stop Work Authority

Risk assessment and Stop Work Authority (Module 026) are directly linked. Stop Work Authority is the action that follows when dynamic risk assessment reveals that conditions have exceeded what is safely manageable with current controls. A worker who is continuously assessing risk during their work and uses SWA when they identify an unacceptable hazard is applying both tools correctly.

The key connection: SWA gives you the right to stop. Risk assessment gives you the information to know when to stop.

✓ BBB Tank Services Risk Assessment Requirements Summary:
  • A written JSA is required for all significant maintenance, repair, and service tasks
  • The JSA must be reviewed with the entire crew and signed before work begins
  • Hazards must be evaluated using the risk matrix before and after controls are applied
  • Controls must be selected using the hierarchy — highest feasible level first
  • Dynamic risk assessment is continuous throughout all tasks
  • Work stops whenever conditions exceed the risk assessment’s scope or new hazards emerge that have not been assessed and controlled
